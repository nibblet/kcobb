## Narrated Journey

### Choosing the Ground You Will Stand On
Careers often look inevitable in hindsight, but they rarely feel that way at the beginning. Keith's early professional life was shaped by a deceptively simple decision: choose the place that fit. That instinct would echo through everything that followed. He did not describe career-building as a march toward prestige for its own sake. He described it as a long test of alignment, judgment, and willingness to grow into the work before him.

That choice mattered because it placed him inside institutions and relationships where talent could be developed through use. The first years were not glamorous. They were instructional. He learned how much preparation matters, how often someone has to volunteer before being invited, and how quickly responsibility finds the person willing to carry it.

**Sources:** [[P1_S16]], [[P1_S17]], [[P1_S18]], [[IV_S03]]

### Advancement Through Ownership
One theme keeps resurfacing in Keith's career stories: when there was a gap, he stepped into it. That habit did more than win praise. It trained him to think like an owner before he formally became one. Moving into new cities, new offices, and new levels of responsibility required adaptability, but it also required a certain calm confidence, the kind that comes from doing the hard preparatory work first.

The memoir stories show the texture of those years: launching, relocating, proving himself, and learning that initiative can change not only how others see you but how you understand your own capabilities. The interview account adds scale. Keith remembers not just a series of jobs, but a long KPMG journey across Jackson, Orlando, Fort Lauderdale, Baltimore, Philadelphia, and beyond. Read together, the sources reveal that the career was not built in one leap. It was accumulated through repeated acts of readiness.

**Sources:** [[P1_S19]], [[P1_S20]], [[P1_S22]], [[P1_S23]], [[IV_S03]]

### Relationships as Career Infrastructure
By the time the career matures in this journey, technical competence is assumed. Keith is clear that hard work and capability matter, but he is just as clear that they are not the whole story. What distinguishes durable leadership is the capacity to recruit, trust, and build real relationships. In later reflections, he describes this almost militarily: build a relationship army. That phrase turns networking from a social hobby into a disciplined professional practice.

Seen this way, the making of a career is not only about promotion. It is about becoming the kind of person others want to follow, call, recommend, and remember. Keith's stories suggest that a career is strongest when competence and relationships reinforce each other. One earns credibility; the other multiplies its reach.
