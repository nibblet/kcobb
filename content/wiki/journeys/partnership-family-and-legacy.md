## Narrated Journey

### The Choice That Organizes Everything Else
Some relationships decorate a life; others structure it. Keith's love story with Dot is not offered as sentimental interlude. It reads as foundational architecture: the decision that reframes priorities, stabilizes ambition, and turns memory into a shared project. Marriage, in this telling, is less a mood than a covenant—a willingness to bind the future to another person's wellbeing. From that center, much else becomes intelligible: why risk is calculated differently, why relocation is negotiated rather than imposed, why success is remembered in terms of who shared it.

**Sources:** [[P1_S12]]

### Children, Sacrifice, and the Long Education of Parents
Parenting stories carry their own moral physics: sleepless seasons, educational priorities, the ordinary heroism of showing up, and the gradual transfer of values through patience rather than speechmaking. What Keith emphasizes is not perfection but investment—attention paid, opportunities sought, standards held with tenderness. Children become the visible continuation of a family's beliefs. In that sense, parenting is not a distraction from career; it is one of the arenas where character proves whether it is real.

**Sources:** [[P1_S34]], [[P1_S35]]

### Sixty Years of Partnership, Still a Plot Twist
The interview adds a different lens: astonishment tempered by familiarity. Two people can grow up a block apart, live entire adult lives of complexity, and still belong to each other in a way that feels both miraculous and obvious. Long marriage is not sustained by drama; it is sustained by loyalty, humor, forgiveness, and the willingness to keep choosing each other when easier options exist. Partnership is also practical infrastructure—shared memory, shared resilience, shared courage when health or circumstance intrudes.

**Sources:** [[IV_S09]]

### Legacy Is What You Can Still Feel
When memory loosens its grip on detail, certain moments remain: vows, births, holidays, reunions, silences that meant more than speeches. Legacy is not only what is written down; it is what descendants recognize as true about themselves because someone lived it first. Keith's later reflections suggest that family legacy is finally emotional mathematics: gratitude multiplied across generations, obligations transformed into joy, and the quiet hope that love will outlast achievements.

**Sources:** [[P1_S39]]
