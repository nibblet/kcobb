## Narrated Journey

### Red Clay Beginnings
When Keith looks back on the beginning, he does not start with size or status. He starts with place. New Albany was small enough for people to know one another and large enough to hold a whole education in miniature. The red clay hills were not just scenery; they were the setting in which habits of attention, duty, and belonging took root. In that kind of town, a young person learned early that reputation traveled fast, work was visible, and community could shape you before you were old enough to name what was happening.

Years later, in interview, he still described that small Mississippi world with gratitude rather than apology. He saw it as a microcosm, not a limitation. That perspective matters. The journey does not begin with escape from humble beginnings. It begins with the discovery that a humble beginning can be rich with instruction if you learn how to see it.

### People Who Set the Standard
The next thread in the story is not ambition but example. A remarkable teacher could open a child's mind to the idea that excellence was real and reachable. At home, Bayne Cobb modeled steadiness, discipline, and decency without turning those virtues into speeches. Frances Cobb showed another kind of strength: practical, resourceful, and quietly creative, the kind that keeps a family moving and enlarges what seems possible.

Taken together, these influences gave Keith a definition of leadership long before he ever held a formal title. It meant showing up prepared. It meant caring how things were done, not merely whether they got done. It meant understanding that character is formed in repeated daily acts, often under the eyes of people who will never call themselves mentors even as they are shaping you for life.

**Sources:** [[P1_S03]], [[P1_S06]], [[P1_S07]], [[IV_S02]]

### Friendship, Memory, and a Lasting Sense of Home
No childhood is formed only by parents and teachers. Friends matter too, because they teach us how to belong beyond the family circle. In Keith's telling, friendship carries warmth, color, and texture. It reminds us that growing up is not only a process of discipline but also of affection, humor, and shared memory. The people around him gave the place emotional permanence.

That is why the South in this journey is not simply a geographic region. It is a moral landscape. It is where Keith learned that people can be plainspoken without being simple, proud without being loud, and deeply rooted without being closed to the wider world. The later chapters of his life would take him far from those hills, but they never really took the hills out of him.
