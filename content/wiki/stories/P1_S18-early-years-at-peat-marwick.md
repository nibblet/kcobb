# Early Years at Peat Marwick

## Full Text

Early Y ears at
Peat Marwick
On July 1, 1963, a skinny young guy with a flattop haircut, fresh out of college, newly married and essentially broke, stumbled into
Jackson, Mississippi in his beat-up 1957 Ford and reported for work at a venerable accounting firm. The firm was then named Peat,
Marwick, Mitchell & Co. At the time it was the largest of the Big
Eight accounting firms, with offices scattered worldwide.
Peat Marwick had emerged at the top of the stack during my job interviews primarily due to a perceived personality fit with the firm overall and the pro file and comfort of the Jackson office.
It was an intimidating environment —a city with high-rise office buildings, professionals dressed in formal business suits, rules and regulations to learn, rigorous schedules and deadlines, and unstated or undeclared expectations. I was a bit nervous and anxious, but only for a few days. Then I called upon some of the self-con fidence
I had developed in college and dove headlong into a thirty-two-year career with the firm.
My starting salary as a junior accountant on the audit sta ff was the lowest of all the o ffers I’d had in my senior year, but it proved sufficient for Dot and me to stay a float. I had a bank account with
$100 (a wedding gift from my parents) and $125 in Series E bonds that I had saved for ten years. Dot and I had rented a small furnished apartment on the west side of Jackson for $75 per month, and walked hand-in-hand over to a First National Bank branch near the apartment to cash in my bonds. On the way home, we stopped off at
Kroger’s grocery store and used up most of that money for groceries and household supplies. We smiled nervously at each other as we pushed the loaded cart all the way to our apartment and reflected on our empty pockets. (We returned the cart to the store, of course.)
We had been married only two weeks when I reported for my first day of work.
The Jackson office employed about twenty- five professionals and enjoyed a reputation as the preeminent accounting firm in
Mississippi. The office was respected and well connected throughout the entire business community. It had operated as an independent firm, Dick D. Quinn & Co., for many years before merging with Peat
Marwick in 1961. The rapid transition from a small, local business into part of a multinational one had apparently proven challenging over the previous two years for most of the Peat Marwick staff there,
and it soon became clear that I wasn’t the only anxious soul in the office. I would see this scenario play out again and again during my thirty-two years, whenever a small local company merged with a larger and more complicated organization.
Fortified by my excellent accounting education at USM and summer internships at M.M. Winkler & Associates and Humble Oil
& Refining Co., I was fortunate to be able to hit the ground running.
My first assignment was the annual audit of Mississippi School
Supply Company and related companies. Bob Duncan was the senior accountant on the engagement. He was an energetic, thoughtful guy and was pivotal in ge tting me started o ff on the right foot, with his great balance of instruction, delegation and supervision. I can still see his cryptic review notes scrawled with his green-leaded pencil.
I can also still close my eyes and see the twinkle in his eyes as he labored to teach me the nuts and bolts of auditing. Bob gave me superior ratings for my performance, which led to new and more varied assignments throughout my first year. I still have copies of some of his evaluations. Meanwhile, I continued working hard —it was a year of constant activity —and gaining invaluable experience,
and good performance evaluations continued. At the end of a first year, I was astonished and grati fied to receive a 20 percent raise to
$7,200, something virtually unheard of at the time. That feverish and fruitful first year set in motion a pace and momentum that lasted more than thirty years.
I took the three-day CPA examination in November 1963,
five months after joining the firm, and passed all three accounting sections with excellent scores; I completed the business law section the next May. I was then awarded the CPA certificate and license, the passport to my chosen profession.
I moved quickly from junior accountant to senior. At the end of my third year, I was promoted to supervisor, a full year ahead of the normal schedule. Two years later, I was promoted to manager,
again considerably ahead of the norm. During those first five years, I had the opportunity to work on a highly diversi fied array of client accounts. My assignments included a wide assortment of commercial businesses—lumber, meat packing, wholesale clothing,
retail furniture, newspapers, a television station and a printing company. I also had the opportunity to be exposed to several specialized industries —savings and loan associations, banks,
municipalities, construction, bond dealers and colleges. Usually,
I spent a few weeks during the winters in the tax department,
preparing individual returns.
This early experience included more than two dozen personal,
trust and partnership tax returns each year for the extensive
Hederman clan, our wealthiest clients. I also audited their sprawling newspaper businesses. The three brothers and one cousin in this large family werevery private, but for some reason they developed a trust in me and brought me into their confidence with virtually every aspect of their financial lives. Twice, the Hedermans o ffered me attractive positions, attempting to lure me away from Peat Marwick.
Where else on earth could a young man straight out of college have found such a diversified opportunity to learn the fundamentals of business and financial management but right there at Peat Marwick in Jackson, Mississippi? Still locked —but accessible!—in my mind today is a treasure trove of valuable lessons and unique experiences gleaned from this wide-ranging set of businesses and organizations.
William L. Norton was the office managing partner and a respected member of the Jackson business establishment. He was the most revered mentor for me in my entire career and was also one of the finest friends I have ever had. He wrapped his arms around me from the get-go and never let go until the day he died. He had an unflagging confidence in my ability and continuously heaped larger and larger responsibilities on me. I was never really sure why, but he believed in me and promoted me everywhere he went.
In many ways, Bill was an odd duck. His demeanor was much like that of the stereotypical absent-minded professor, as he stumbled along in his own self-contained world. Sometimes in the midst of a business crisis, one could find him tending his orchids in his greenhouse. He was extremely intelligent and financially astute,
but always seemed like an old man to me. I always respectfully called him Mr. Norton, never Bill. One day shortly after I was promoted to
William L. Norton manager, his secretary, Gloria Smith, came into my office, closed the door and said in a low voice, “Keith, Mr. Norton would like for you to call him Bill!” So I did, from that point forward. Bill practically adopted me as his surrogate during my two-year stint as a manager.
In many respects I served as his alter ego.
My workload increased dramatically as a manager, with responsibilities that were technical as well as managerial. On two major engagements, I was e ffectively the partner with full responsibility and almost sole contact with top-level client management. One of these was L&A Contracting Company, a road and bridge builder. L&A was a family business with a father and two brothers as the owners. I developed an excellent relationship with my primary contract there, Harley Sims (one of the brothers), and he consulted me regularly on virtually everything financial. Because of this close relationship, I gained a deep understanding of their business, as well as a serious commitment to their welfare.
In late 1968, I was handling a complex audit assignment at a pivotal time in L&A’s history. Bill Norton, who was the official client service partner, was delinquent in his a ttention to this client, and we were down to the wire with our report —a report where the company’s very survival hung in the balance. The company had a major construction contract for the Golden Glades interchange in
Miami, and their Florida license was being held in abeyance pending submission of audited financial statements. The day before the deadline, Bill still had made no review of our work. I was desperately trying to press him into action with predictions of dire consequences.
Seemingly a bit frustrated with me, he picked up the previously untouched report drafts lying on the floor behind his desk, peered over his glasses with a sober stare and said, “Cobb, is it OK for me to sign this report?” I said, “Sure… ” and without further hesitation he heavily signed the firm’s name—Peat, Marwick, Mitchell & Co.
Then he escorted me out of his office, his hand on my shoulder and the reports under my arm.
It was an expression of absolute confidence. I stayed up all night double checking every one of the numbers! This single event was a turning point in my career, when I began to realize the burden of decision-making. From that point forward, I began to take total personal ownership of my work. T oday, almost fifty years later, I still think about this experience and the lesson it taught me every time
I drive down I-95 to Miami and pass through that Golden Glades interchange.
An even more signi ficant turning point occurred shortly thereafter. Cal-Maine Foods, Inc. ’s initial public offering was in 1969.
At that time, I was the audit manager for Adams Egg Farms, a major egg producer in Mississippi, when Fred Adams decided to embark on a huge three-way merger with Dairy Fresh Products in California and Maine Egg Farms in Portland, Maine, to create the largest egg supplier in the United States. He indeed did that—by a wide margin.
The three businesses standing alone were themselves incredibly complex conglomerations of corporations, partnerships and sole proprietorships. T rying to combine and amalgamate them into a cohesive single entity was nothing short of mind-boggling. Making matters worse, the financial records of all the entities were a mess.
This is when, as if the merger didn’t provide enough complexity,
Fred decided to simultaneously launch an initial public offering and raise capital for further expansion.
My team worked feverishly on the Cal-Maine project for six months, migrating from one emergency to the next. Near the end, I
was working seven days a week, twelve to fourteen chargeable hours each day, to the point of almost total exhaustion. It was my first experience with a public offering or even an SEC filing. Coupled with the backbreaking workload and exponential learning curve, I had virtually no partner support. Guy Story, the designated engagement partner, was perpetually AWOL. He had basically checked out, in a shameful demonstration of unprofessional conduct. So I bypassed the engagement partner and took on the partner responsibilities myself. It wasn’t that I was so cocky at thevery young age of twenty-eight; I was desperate and determined to serve the client professionally and responsibly.
I enlisted the guidance of a senior Peat Marwick SEC reviewing partner in the Houston office named Bill Su ttle and he agreed to review my work and sign off on behalf of the firm. Bill was a serious professional, solemn and straight-laced. He was the quintessential technical accountant nearing the end of an illustrious career with the firm. He was a man of few words—tough and rigid, yet practical and level headed. His maturity and judgment were critical assets to me.
Without him, I would never have made it through the engagement.
Eventually, we got the job done: the merger was completed and the surviving company became publicly owned. Despite the agony, the rigorous experience turned out to be a godsend to me personally.
Bill Suttle was impressed by the quality of my work on Cal-Maine,
much as a proud father might view his only son. He was lavish in his praise and compliments, which was uncharacteristic of him. He even took the initiative to provide wri tten recommendations to regional partner-in-charge Gray Wake field, saying that Cal-Maine was the most complex audit engagement he had ever seen in his long career and that my performance was the best he had ever witnessed in any professional setting. That recommendation essentially became my ticket on a continuing bullet train.
Buried somewhere in my memorabilia, I have retained the SEC
prospectus from that 1969 Cal-Maine IPO. It still looks complicated!
The company has survived all these years, through all the ups and downs of the commodities marketplace, with Fred Adams still serving as the chairman emeritus. His son-in-law is now the CEO
and the company has a flock of over thirty- five million chickens producing a billion dozen eggs per year. Y es, a billion dozen! T oday,
the company’s revenues represent over 20 percent of the nation’s total egg consumption. Whenever I read about Cal-Maine, I quietly take pride in knowing that I played a major role in its financial engineering and complex formation. I conjure up a wry smile when
I read its annual reports and come across a few extant words that I
actually wrote for its initial prospectus over forty years ago.
I lost track of Fred for many years as I moved around the country, but bumped into him at a Federal Reserve dinner in Atlanta a few years ago. Seeing me, he embraced me in a huge bear hug and bounced me up and down for several minutes (he’s a giant of a man).
He then began reciting war stories from 1969. Good memories of some tough times!
One of the highlights during my time in Jackson was the assortment of people whom I had the privilege of knowing. There was Cowan Gooch, an elderly distinguished partner who was a great teacher with a fatherly demeanor. Guy Story, an audit partner, was a Barney Fife look-a-like and a rather goofy guy who didn’t work very hard —certainly the antithesis of the traditional image of a
Big Eight partner. Rhyne Neubert was a taciturn tax partner who seldom had a nice thing to say about anything. Then there was the positive Julian Noel, a career manager who had worked in the office for more than thirty years and handled some of the old-line clients.
Bob Duncan was an Auburn University transplant, a smart, energetic and conscientious professional. Jimmy Smith was a hard-drinking,
often vulgar supervisor and, nevertheless, a capable auditor who was dedicated to his clients. And so the list went on. It was quite a menagerie of personalities. I didn’t necessarily follow or buddy up with any of them, but I learned a lot from all. There were also a number of contemporaries with whom I worked or who worked for me. I had a lot of respect for them —Murray Underwood, Atlee
Harmon, Paul Breazeale, Mike Green, Homer Cummings, Bill
Eubank, Johnny Black, Cecil Brown and many others. But the most important person of all these associates was Bill Norton.
I mentioned him before but I didn’t write that he was a vocabulary and grammar nut, a word connoisseur. For years and years, I played an odd game with him —a game of vocabulary riddles. The game was our own invention and we never actually discussed the origin or rules; we just played it. It normally started by him making a statement using some arcane word. Then I would try to craft an esoteric response, letting him know that I understood and causing him to think hard about my response. This would in turn force him into an even more abstruse rejoinder. And so on.
Sometimes these comebacks would be weeks apart —a subtle offhand remark, a cryptic note in a margin, an outright question.
This made the game even more di fficult, calling on the memory of the last iteration. And we often had multiple games going at once. In fact, it would occasionally be months or years before we would pick up once again on an old string. All the while, we never outwardly acknowledged that it was a game at all. The game actually started when Bill used the word “quixotic” incorrectly once in describing
Ernie Jenkins, a client who changed his mind frequently. I picked up on the error, and rather than correct him overtly, I crafted an oblique response that subtly caused him to realize his error and consult the huge dictionary in the living room of his home. A week or so later,
he mentioned that I was much like Don Quixote in chasing a wild-eyed tax scheme, but he suspected that I was unwilling to change my mind. I knew then that he knew that I knew that he knew what quixotic meant.
And so it went until he died at the age of eighty-nine. What an interesting man! I really miss him. If I could see him today, I would casually mutter something like, “Y ou know, Bill, ten years is just too long for any caesura to last. Don’t you think?” Y ears ago, I bought a two-thousand-page Random House dictionary just like his and it sits prominently today on a stand in my study.
But back to the chronology of the working years. In late 1969, I
was approached by Dave Thompson, partner in charge of personnel for Peat Marwick, asking me to transfer to New Y ork and work in the
Department of Professional Practice (DPP) for two years. DPP was a highly coveted assignment in the technical nerve center of the firm and a virtual guaranteed pathway to partnership. Only three or four managers were selected annually for the prestigious assignment. I
was flattered and honored to be among this elite corps, but basically
I didn’t want to do it. Though I felt that I was technically capable of the task, I did not relish the idea of sitting in an ivory tower without client contact for two years. Moreover, I didn’t want to move to New
Y ork. I fretted and fre tted over the decision, fearing that declining the o ffer would signal the end of my career. Nobody ever turned down such a rare opportunity! Nevertheless, I did my best at politely explaining my views and sheepishly turned my back on the brass ring. Dave Thompson appeared stricken by a state of lockjaw, but he eventually moved on and let me be.
Lo and behold, only a few months later the unbelievable happened. On a Sunday afternoon in June 1970, Bill Norton showed up unexpectedly at my home and told me that I had been elected to partnership in Peat, Marwick, Mitchell & Co. I was stunned.
And overwhelmed. It hadn’t even occurred to me that I would be nominated, much less elected. Here I was, just a regular guy, barely twenty-nine years old, stuck in the back woods of the vast empire of Peat Marwick. Y et I was being o ffered a seat in the club. Wow!
As it turned out, I was the youngest person ever to be admi tted to partnership in the seventy-three-year history of Peat Marwick. How did this happen? I was not totally certain. Everything about the partnership was a big secret back then. But I later learned that Bill
Norton made a forceful and passionate nomination, and Bill Su ttle championed my candidacy in the background. Gray Wake field thought Bill Suttle could do no wrong and thus decided that I must be the best thing to come along since ice cream. Dave Thompson was apparently not in the mix of this decision-making process. Gray picked up Bill Su ttle’s mantle and, I think, rammed my nomination through the executive commi ttee. Gray at least claimed full credit for it. For the next twenty- five years, even on a couple of occasions far after his retirement, whenever I saw Gray he always crowed about making me the youngest partner in the history of the firm. I was grateful for all of their efforts, to say the least.
So, I ended this phase of my career as a paid sta ff member and joined a group of about five hundred distinguished professionals as a full general partner. My partnership position became e ffective exactly seven years to the day from the time I started with the firm.
My compensation had compounded at a rate of about 30 percent per year since my first day. In my last year as a manager, I earned
$26,753. From that point forward, my income would be wholly dependent on a share of the firm’s earnings, a share that would prove to increase steadily over time.
Peat Marwick partners’ meeting, 1970
Among my most cherished memorabilia from those early days is a photograph of the five hundred partners attending the seventy-third annual partners’ meeting in October 1970. The meeting was held at the luxurious Greenbrier resort in White Sulphur Springs,
West Virginia. The partners in the photograph are all dressed in black tuxedos and reflect a countenance that conjures up images of the captains of industry in the mid-1900s. Simply put, I felt as if I
had arrived! It is di fficult to describe my emotions as I sat in that massive ballroom, my mind racing all the way back to my childhood in Calhoun City, through college and then all the hard work over the past seven years. Most importantly though, I re flected on all of my mentors over the years, much like the scrolling credits at the end of an epic movie —Miss Frances Mallory, Dr. Carter Dobbs, Dr.
McCain, Dr. Switzer, Bill Norton, Bill Su ttle and a host of others.
They had all played lead roles in my drama. And I thought about
Dot, the star headliner of the movie, who loved me and supported me through those frenetic times. I pinched myself to be sure it was real. When the goosebumps eventually subsided, I saddled up and began moving on the next phase of my career.

## Principles

- Assume personal ownership of deliverables when accountability gaps appear.
- Cultivate and rely on mentors and sponsors to champion advancement.
- Seek technical expertise and formal review when operating at the edge of regulatory complexity.
- Embrace intense, challenging assignments as accelerants for learning and career momentum.

## Decision heuristics

- When partner support is absent, assume necessary responsibilities and secure an independent, qualified reviewer to validate the work.
- Prioritize client-critical deadlines and act decisively if the client's survival depends on timely deliverables.
- Decline prestigious roles that sacrifice the type of work you value after weighing long-term fit and personal priorities.
- Treat explicit expressions of mentor confidence as authorization to accept greater responsibility and act without hesitation.
