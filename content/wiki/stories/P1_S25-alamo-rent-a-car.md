# Alamo Rent A Car

> The narrator becomes CEO of Alamo Rent A Car in 1995, implements rapid strategic, operational, financial and cultural changes to turn the company from large losses to profitability, and ultimately sells it to AutoNation. He emphasizes clear agreements, decisive leadership, and rebuilding key relationships.

Alamo Rent A Car
Mike Egan could not have been more generous and gracious during my negotiations for the CEO position at Alamo Rent A Car.
He was sympathetic to my loyalty to KPMG and my desire to make my departure as smooth and seamless as possible. On the other hand, he was anxious for me to start. A fter some discussion, we finally agreed on a start date of April 1, 1995.
Having dealt with Mike on countless occasions over the years, I
realized the need to be comprehensive in laying out the terms of my contract. If Mike agrees to something, he is always a man of his word and will live up to the agreement. But if there is ambiguity or a lack of explicit understanding, he can be di fficult, particularly when the going gets tough. So, on the flight down to Fort Lauderdale, I used my Macintosh laptop to prepare a detailed term sheet, setting forth the parameters of my employment. Rosalie Arthur was now Mike’s chief of sta ff, so I slipped into her office and asked her to print out the term sheet prior to meeting with Mike. Mike sat contemplatively and read the term sheet carefully. He then looked up and agreed to virtually everything I had suggested, tweaking a few small things that were apparently his hot bu ttons. He sent the redacted term sheet down to the T ripp Sco tt law firm for conversion into a legal contract. Shortly thereafter, Norm T ripp came storming upstairs,
interrupted our meeting and said, “Who wrote this?” , waving the term sheet in the air. I replied, “I did. ” He snorted, “It certainly looks like it. Mike, have you read this?” Mike laughed and instructed him to just dra ft the contract, that we didn’t require his advice on the terms! On balance, it was an excellent contract —both for me and for the company, in my view.
The deal I struck with Mike gave me full range of authority and responsibility as CEO. Mike would stay involved on the periphery but would look to me as the leader of the organization.
As we were signing o ff on my deal, Mike told me about negotiations he had been having with Roger Ballou. Roger was a senior executive with American Express, and Mike had met him at travel industry functions. Mike was impressed with Roger and, in fact, had been considering him for the CEO position, but decided on me instead. Mike wanted to know what I thought about bringing him on board as the number two executive with a title of chief marketing officer. Even though I had never met Roger, I trusted
Mike’s instincts and respected his proposal. As uncharacteristic as this was for me —acting without verifying —I agreed. It was one of the best decisions I ever made. Roger and I fit like two gloves. We worked together like a symphony. More about this later.
Alamo was in desperate straits, as Mike had hinted in our initial New Y ork meeting. The company had incurred a net loss of
$50 million in 1994 and the prospects for 1995 were equally grim.
Relationships with General Motors and the banks were strained to the breaking point. Competitors were marching the company around the marketplace like a bunch of toy soldiers. And Alamo’s payroll was seriously bloated. In short, the company was a mess. And it was getting messier by the day.

Roger reported shortly after I did, and we went to work immediately. I called on my KPMG financial service experiences and the earlier advice of Marc Gerstein to develop meaningful strategies around five key sectors of the business. Roger and I quickly identified dozens of issues that needed a ttention and began sorting out those with the highest priorities. Before long we had articulated our five-point strategy. It centered on the following areas:
• Revenue management—developing systems that would enhance rental yields while remaining competitive in the marketplace.
• Fleet management—stopping the perpetual over-fleeting condition and having too many cars in stock, stabilizing utilization and cultivating be tter relationships with suppliers.
• Marketing —building a marketing program based on customer service rather than pure price competition.
• T alent management —right-sizing the company and motivating personnel to line up behind a coherent strategy.
• Financial management —restructuring the corporate debt and maximizing leverage on the company’s weak capital position.
Once the priorities were se ttled, we began moving with lightning speed. There was no time to waste. We were sure of what we needed to do and let nothing step into our pathways. We had pushback from the old guard from time to time, but we ignored it and pressed forward.
At the end of our first month on the job, we announced a major layo ff of personnel, terminating employment for about five hundred people. This was a traumatic event, inasmuch as it was completely counter-cultural for Alamo. The company had a history of a family work environment. Involuntary terminations were virtually unheard of. We reasoned that we should bite the bullet and get this out of the way up front. Sure, we would su ffer some heat; but we would try to redirect the remaining sta ff on a more positive track. So, we sliced approximately $3 million off the payroll in one big move in the first sixty days. We also reshuffled and reorganized the management team around our strategy and articulated a no-nonsense approach to the business.
General Motors was the company’s major supplier of cars; its vehicles comprised roughly 75 percent of the Alamo fleet. And
Alamo was one of the top five customers of General Motors.
Nevertheless, the customer/supplier relationship was terrible,
bordering on dysfunctional. The Alamo executives, including
Mike, hated General Motors, and vice versa. This made no sense to me. Not being a part of the history, I took on the job of trying to repair the damage. A fter about a month on the job,
I placed a call to George Frink, the senior vice president (the
SVP) responsible for GM fleet sales, and requested a meeting.
I insisted that it be a one-on-one meeting and that it be held at a location other than the GM headquarters. He agreed, and I
arranged for the two of us to meet at a Marrio tt hotel in Detroit.
I told George about new fleet strategies we were considering that would dramatically reduce the GM concentration, hinting that we were in negotiations with Japanese, Korean and European manufacturers. This was somewhat of a veiled threat —and frankly, a bit of a blu ff. Although I didn’t acknowledge our dependency, we needed General Motors. We had to have them.
But I recognized that they needed us, too. The Alamo business was a huge invoice, even in Detroit. I demanded a new relationship that re flected mutual respect and mutual dependency. I think
George found my resoluteness and toughness disarming. A fter a couple of hours of banter and bluster on both sides of the table,
I sensed his arrogance begin to evaporate. He slowly began to cave, and we soon got down to real business. That night, after meeting with George for five hours, I flew home with $17 million in concessions on the 1995 fleet contract. A very productive trip!
At the time of our arrival, Alamo had a fleet of 125,000
automobiles. Roger and I reasoned that the optimum level,
based on our business volume, was about 115,000. Making that inventory reduction, however, would not be as simple as it might appear. There were numerous delivery contracts in place,
complex buy-back arrangements with sti ff holding periods, an erratic used car market, etc. Overlaid on these obstacles was a fleet management culture in place that had an ingrained bias toward buying new cars. There was a corollary bias against disposition. That is, it was more fun to buy a car than to sell one!
The result of all this was an average 8 to 10 percent over- fleeting condition. Almost constantly. Roger and I pressed hard on fleet reductions, initially to no avail. Finally, in a state of frustration, I
fired the senior vice president of fleet operations and appointed a new executive. Bingo! Things started to move immediately and within a few months we had our 10,000-car fleet reduction. The consequences of this move alone resulted in a financial impact of over $40 million and the termination of this SVP turned out to be a pivotal move, sending a signal that we were serious about our strategy and would not tolerate rogue executives with other agendas. The change in mood inside the company was palpable.
T ackling the revenue management issues proved to be a more difficult task. Fierce competition in the car rental industry had kept rates arti ficially low for years. Revenue management is an extremely complex ma tter and the industry simply didn’t have the brainpower to manage it. The result was dramatic price competition that led to net losses for the entire industry. Though
I never made the actual calculation, I believe the combined net income of the five major car rental companies (excluding
Enterprise) for the last twenty- five years was approximately zero. Roger and I looked at techniques being employed by the airline and hotel industries and decided to invest $4 million in a sophisticated arti ficial intelligence system that promised enhanced and predictable yields. It was counter-cultural for our people, but it worked.
I often recall one event in late 1995, when we faced o ff with our internal management over a revenue management decision.
Hertz had made a surprise announcement of a $5 across-the-board increase in its daily rates. Learning of this, we called an immediate meeting of sixteen senior executives to discuss how we should respond. Amazingly, our people argued strongly that we should lower our rates! They were married to the concept of low-price competition (and they literally hated Hertz). Their crazy logic went something along these lines: “This is our golden opportunity to snap up market share from Hertz. We can cut their legs o ff at the knees. ” I remember Roger retorting, “Guys, you lost $50 million last year with a 13 percent market share. Why would you want more market share at lower rates?” Someone suggested we vote. The vote turned out to be fourteen to two in favor of lowering our rates and pu tting competitive pressure on
Hertz. Roger and I met the next morning at seven and dra fted an announcement that Alamo would increase rates by $3 across the board. So much for democracy in a screwball culture that had been warped by a number of bad habits, including illogical price competition.
Because of the huge employee layo ff, however, we knew we needed to do some repair work on the overall morale and motivation of the troops. The rumor mill was at a fever pitch and legitimate information flow within the organization was lousy. We held several meetings and initiated newsle tters and such, all with modest results. Then I came up with an idea, that we randomly select five non-supervisory personnel to meet with me for breakfast once a month. The human resources department would select the individuals and announce their names prior to the monthly breakfast. Every word I said at those breakfasts spread like wild fire within the company. A fter a while, I became pretty adept at these meetings. I was able to extract all sorts of previously unknown information about conditions within the company and to deliver my focused messages through the employees’ grapevine.
Dozens of fascinating anecdotes emerged. I’ll mention only one. A mailroom worker at one breakfast meeting asked me, “Why does the company waste so much money mailing marketing materials to bad addresses?” Not knowing anything about it, I hemmed and hawed and danced around the subject until he finally said, “Come down to the mailroom sometime and I’ll show you what I’m talking about. ” Honestly, I didn’t even know where the mailroom was. But, interested, I followed him back there after the breakfast. He took me to a room stacked to the ceiling and packed solid with thousands and thousands (if not millions) of pieces of returned mail. Astonishing! As one might imagine, my next stop was upstairs for a sit-down with the SVP
of marketing.
I held these breakfast meetings for eighteen months in a row,
ninety employees in all. It was one of the best things I did and it was certainly the most enjoyable. For years afterward, I would bump into someone in Fort Lauderdale who would remind me that they had attended one of my breakfasts and generally acted as if we were best friends.
I also spent valuable time cultivating relationships with our customers and suppliers. I attended the major travel conventions and used my time wisely to schmooze with our contacts.
Alamo owned a beautiful five-bedroom home at Ocean Reef in the Florida Keys, fully sta ffed and all fitted out with bells and whistles for entertainment. Dot and I —usually with Roger and
Georgeann—spent about ten weekends there entertaining auto manufacturers, travel agencies, tour operators, bankers and other people with whom we wanted to cultivate a good relationship.
One would be hard pressed to imagine a be tter se tting for corporate entertainment and relationship development. We also entertained at our home in Fort Lauderdale; I fondly remember having forty German travel agency representatives at our home for dinner once, with few English-speaking people in the crowd.
Alamo had a significant presence in both the United Kingdom and Germany, products of acquisitions that Mike had made a few years earlier. Both were di fficult situations, but the German business was a real problem. It was losing money like a sieve.
Significant money. I made several trips over there, a ttempting to better understand the situation and get the businesses back on track. I moved a quality guy to London to manage that business,
but we were contractually stuck with the manager of the German business. He was a nice guy, as it turned out, but he couldn’t manage his way out of a shoebox. Despite several trips to Germany,
I ran out of time and was never able to stop the bleeding.
Another area that demanded immediate a ttention was the precarious financial condition of the company. Alamo had a patchwork quilt of complex and disconnected automobile financing arrangements with various financial institutions. Each agreement had its own wrinkles. Management of the rats’ nest was next to impossible, and there were constant deadlines and threats of default and doom. Moreover, our treasury management team was not the most experienced in the world. I reasoned that we needed a master strategy so that we would be in better control of our own destiny. So, I went to Citibank and for $500,000 engaged them to design a master corporate financing arrangement, one crafted from our perspective. There was an understanding that
Citibank would be an independent consultant in this engagement,
and not be among our quali fied lenders. They did a remarkable job. Bank of America immediately signed on for a $1.5 billion slice under the arrangement. Other financial institutions quickly followed and voilà, we had a hugely simplified structure and were able to reduce our treasury sta ff signi ficantly. We also designed and completed a $100 million high-yield bond o ffering that provided much-needed non-vehicle financing for our far- flung operations.
And so it went. We tackled issue after issue, mostly with positive and measurable results. Within just a few months, we had the consolidated operations turned around and were on the road to profitability.
Roger Ballou was a godsend for me. He and I had virtually identical perspectives on the business and a common set of values and work ethic —and we possessed uncanny complementary skills. Much di fferent, but complementary. We immediately developed a close bond and working relationship, o ften practically finishing each other’s sentences. We quickly developed into a partnership, joining hands in our quest to right the ship.
When in town at the same time, we would meet in the office at seven a.m. each day to recalibrate our tactics well before anyone else showed up for work. Then we would march o ff in separate directions to execute our strategy. T ogether we developed a deck of seventy- five slides that addressed what we believed to be the most critical strategic functions and metrics of the business. The slides represented a virtual primer on the company dynamics. Our sta ff updated the slide information regularly.
We used these visual aids (or portions of them) everywhere we went—management meetings, banker presentations, industry conventions, road shows, site visits, sales meetings and the like.
They represented the essence of our strategy. Roger and I usually made joint presentations, alternating our commentary back and forth at roughly one- to two-minute intervals, without notes or presentation devices. We were so philosophically synchronized and familiar with the materials that coins were sometimes flipped to see who went first. From our point of view, this technique was incredibly efficient and handy. From the audience perspective, it came across as an unusual display of executive leadership.
Interestingly, people o ften misjudged our personal talents and roles within the company. Considering our backgrounds,
most reasoned that I was the financial expert and Roger the management and marketing guru. In fact, it was much the opposite. My primary strengths were management and leadership of large organizations, and Roger was a far better financial analyst than I was. Without a doubt, Roger Ballou is one of the smartest people I have ever met. It was just good fortune that we landed together—two guys in the right place at the right time.
Roger and I remain close friends and serve together on the board of Alliance Data Systems Corporation. There we have experienced the same symbiotic relationship we had at Alamo.
We still finish each other’s sentences.
From the beginning, Mike had expressed an interest in selling at least a part of the business and monetizing some of his holdings.
It was for that reason that I insisted on a lucrative change of control clause in my contract. Mike owned 94 percent of the company,
and this investment represented essentially all of his net worth.
He had mixed feelings about selling, however. Alamo was his baby. He was hungry for the liquidity, but he didn’t want to give up the baby. In mid-1996 Henry Silverman, CEO of Cendant,
Inc., approached us about a possible acquisition of Alamo. A fter several rounds of talks, negotiations began to get serious. Roger and I worked feverishly on these negotiations, while Mike fretted and kibitzed in the background. In the end, Henry’s complex offer for 100 percent of the company amounted to approximately
$600 million, an unbelievable sum given the company’s operating history over the last few years—notwithstanding the turnaround we had in place. A fter a long day of hammering out details of a deal with Silverman and attorneys in New Y ork, Mike abruptly rejected the deal. Basically, he didn’t like Henry and didn’t like the fact that Henry told him he wouldn’t have a place in the company.
So we packed our bags and headed home.
Mike called Wayne Huizenga the next day to renew an earlier discussion, and we shi fted gears and started another round of negotiations. I had first approached Wayne one day in the parking lot of the Performing Arts Center and advanced the notion of
AutoNation, which he founded and controlled, acquiring Alamo.
There was some logic to this combination due to the volume of cars bought and sold by both organizations. He brushed me o ff,
saying he didn’t know anything about the car rental business and wasn’t interested. Despite this apparent rebuff, I received a call the next day from Steve Berrard, Wayne’s right-hand man, asking to meet with me and discuss the car rental business. In fact, they did have some interest. In a sense, we had them on the back burner all along. The negotiations with Wayne went quickly, with only a modicum of due diligence. Using his legendary intuitive judgment, Wayne simply pivoted o ff the aborted Cendant deal and rounded the price up to $625 million. Six weeks later, we closed the deal, and AutoNation owned Alamo.
At the time of closing, Wayne met with me to discuss my continuing role in the company. My contract provided for five years’ compensation in the event of a change of control. As I
pondered what I might do, I made a call on my good friend Jim
Blosser. Jim and I had consulted with each other several times over a period of twenty years or more, each time one of us contemplated a career move or major change in our lives. I valued his advice and still do. Jim had worked for Wayne for a few years and of course he knew me well. He said, “Keith, I honestly don’t think this is a good fit for you. ” I appreciated that candor.
Wayne offered to roll my contract into a new one. The new one would have no immediate payout, but it (of course) would have all sorts of incentive conditions. I felt as if I had already proved my value and earned my reward, so I declined his offer, suggesting that he pay me the present value of my five-year compensation plan—and then we could discuss a new contract. He didn’t like that proposal. So, I told him I would step away and just collect on my severance arrangement. I suggested that Roger was fully capable of running the company. Wayne looked a bit surprised and remarked, “So, you’re just going to take your money and run?” And I responded, “Y eah, it looks like that’s what I’m about to do!” Wayne and I are still friendly, but I’ve always suspected that he remains aware that I didn’t jump at the chance to work for him.
I turned over the reins of Alamo to AutoNation in early 1997,
not quite two years from the time I had joined it. We had just reported net earnings of $40 million for the 1996 year, an incredible turnaround from the $50 million loss when I got there.
Meanwhile, on the night of the closing of the AutoNation/
Alamo transaction, I was si tting with Wayne at about midnight waiting for the lawyers to finalize all of the documents. Wayne had been focused on the high-level strategic rationale for the acquisition
(the volume of business with Detroit manufacturers and the movement of used cars from one company to the other) and I was attempting to explain to him just how complicated the day-to-day management of this business was. I felt he might be discounting the complexities. I respected and admired Wayne, and I wanted to be certain he didn’t feel misled down the road. He listened attentively for a while, then said, “Come on. Give me a break. Y ou buy cars, you rent them for a while and then you sell them. How complicated can that be?” Unfortunately for AutoNation, he found out the hard way.
A couple of months later, Wayne doubled down and bought
National Car Rental, bringing the combined fleet to over 200,000
cars. Then over the next four years, they followed the twenty- five-year experience of all of the other large car rental companies: they lost their shirts. AutoNation spun off the car rental businesses into a separate public company after absorbing a loss of approximately $1.2
billion, essentially everything they had paid for the two companies.
In early 2001, I received a call asking me to come back and help
ANC Rental, the spun-o ff company, sell either Alamo or National,
or both. I spent about eight months as a part-time lead consultant on this project. I darted around all over the country hawking deals with all sorts of prospects, including a day-long discussion with Roger
Penske in New Y ork. I had the Alamo segment practically sold to
Cendant (yeah, the same guys!) and was si tting in the conference room ironing out final details on September 11th when the overhead television announced that two planes had struck the World T rade
Center. Serendipity was not with me this time. The Cendant board of directors met the next day and called off all proposed travel industry deals. The marketplace for merger and acquisition transactions dried up totally, so I wrapped up my consulting arrangement with this event.
Alamo was a watershed experience for me. It was a tremendous way to top o ff a wonderful career. In a sense, I was well prepared for this experience, due to my rich history with literally hundreds of clients at KPMG. In another sense, however, I was extremely fortunate to fall into a situation so ripe with opportunity. As mentioned, the car rental industry was not blessed with a lot of intelligence. The space had been occupied for decades by a bunch of car guys —people who knew and loved cars, but had a dearth of business acumen. Alamo was no exception. Though we would never have been so bold or narcissistic to say this to anyone else,
Roger and I believed we were the two smartest guys in the industry.
Actually, I still believe that, Mike Egan notwithstanding. That says more about the lackluster industry than it does about us. Mike is a brilliant man, and his intellect and market instincts catapulted him to be a major player in a fiercely competitive business climate. But even he lacked the skills to manage a large-scale organization, a fact he grudgingly admitted to me privately in our 1995 meeting in New
Y ork. Roger and I brought a level of business sophistication and dispassionate judgment to the company. We were not hamstrung by a history of wheeling and dealing in cars. We applied coherent thought and strategic planning to complex issues, annoying as that was to a group of executives accustomed to flying by the seat of their pants. Additionally, we were keenly analytical, carefully weighed our decisions and stuck to our guns, moving rapidly always.
Roger and I fed on each other, and the power of our two-pronged leadership was perceptible. For these reasons, I think, we managed to take a moribund company that was losing mega-millions and was practically bankrupt, transform it into a profitable enterprise and sell it for $625 million, all in less than two years!
I walked away from Alamo with my head high. I knew I had accomplished a remarkable feat. And I was proud of myself.

## Principles

- Make key agreements explicit in writing to eliminate ambiguity and secure authority.
- Move quickly and decisively once priorities are set.
- Repair damaged supplier relationships through direct, face-to-face negotiation in neutral settings.
- Signal seriousness about strategic priorities by changing leadership when execution stalls.
- Invest in data and technology to change industry economics where conventional approaches fail.
- Engage front-line employees directly to surface operational problems and improve communication.

## Decision heuristics

- Before finalizing an executive contract, prepare and present a detailed term sheet to remove ambiguity.
- When a critical supplier relationship is dysfunctional, insist on a private, neutral-location meeting to negotiate from mutual dependency.
- If a functional area persistently fails to deliver critical outcomes, replace leadership swiftly to signal seriousness and accelerate execution.
- When industry practice is failing you, adopt proven techniques from other sectors and fund them adequately.
- Address morale and information gaps by creating recurring, informal direct-access forums with randomly selected front-line employees.
- When offered a post-acquisition role that feels misaligned, consult a trusted advisor and prefer taking secured compensation if fit is doubtful.
