Early Wanderlust
I think I may have been born a wanderer. T ravel has been a focus and a theme for most of my life. So much of my memory is centered on places—places and experiences outside my immediate environment.
My journeys began early in life. Some of my earliest memories are visits to my grandparents’ homes. The Cobb grandparents lived in Banner, a whopping eighteen miles from Calhoun City. The
Clements lived near Oxford, about forty miles from my home. Those distances seemed enormous when I was four or five years old. I have a vivid memory of one trip to Banner in the old red grocery truck that my Dad drove for the Justice Company. It was a cold and rainy winter day. The road was muddy and bumpy. Daddy, Mother, Sandy and I
were all stuffed like sardines into the cab of the truck. The engine was loud and noisy. The inside of the cab was shivering cold and smelled like oil. I sat in the middle, with my legs straddling the long gearshift protruding from the floor, watching the windshield wipers flop back and forth and feeling Daddy’s tension as he navigated the slippery hills and curves. Mother nagged on and on about how foolish we were to be out visiting on a day like this. Especially just to spend time with her in-laws! Despite the discomforts of the day, however, I
was experiencing a thrill of wanderlust. I was going somewhere. And
I was anxious and excited. There were new things to learn about:
muddy roads, noisy trucks, how gear shi fts and windshield wipers work, my grandmother’s fried apple pies, an old hand-cranked
Victrola that played scratchy music, and on and on. The tension in the air was palpable, too. So many new and different things to learn,
all crammed into a single day on the road to Banner. In a sense, this was the beginning of a lifelong quest for experiencing the mysteries of life beyond my immediate surroundings.
In 1951, Daddy bought our first automobile—a brand new two-door maroon Plymouth. One of our first trips in this car was to visit my Uncle Bill and Aunt Katherine in Oak Ridge, T ennessee. It was a long trip, interrupted by frequent restroom stops, gas fill-ups and lunch on a roadside picnic table. Daddy grumbled about the price of gas and putting almost one thousand miles on a brand new car. I
remember getting bored and tired from being cramped in the back seat for hours with nothing to do. But I also remember that there was mystery and intrigue in the air, which piqued my interest. Uncle
Bill worked for the Atomic Energy Commission, and his work was reportedly top-secret stu ff. There was intense speculation about whether he was building atomic bombs for the military. I wanted to know and asked him a lot of questions. But he wasn’t talking.
Uncle Bill also had a huge ham radio setup in his basement. This was ostensibly his hobby. All the sophisticated equipment with lights,
buttons, dials and indecipherable abbreviations mesmerized me. He would sit and talk to people who claimed to be located all over the world. Amazing! I remember wondering, even at age ten or eleven, if he might be a secret spy! He was amused at my curiosity, and I think urged me along by spinning yarns and watching my jaw drop. Uncle
Bill took us to an atomic energy museum in Oak Ridge and told more hair-raising stories. I have no idea if any of them were true or if he just delighted in the fantasy of it all. And then there was the long trip home—hot, sweaty, but not so boring this time because of the vivid images of atomic bombs and secret ham radio communications.
These memories of this trip have been lodged in my brain ever since.
Just the mention of the words “Oak Ridge” causes my nostalgia to spring to life, even to this day. This was just one early life experience fueled by travel—learning that never could have happened at home.
My first signi ficant travel on my own was a journey to Valley
Forge, Pennsylvania in 1957 to a ttend the National Boy Scout
Jamboree. A small group of teenage boys from T roop 40 in Calhoun
City piled into a bus with scouts from nearby towns and a couple of chaperones. The bus rumbled along two-lane roads for the thousand-mile trek to Philadelphia. Then, upon arrival at Valley Forge, we proceeded to our assigned campsites, pitched our tents and set up camp. We had all the traditional camping gear, along with enough food and supplies for a week’s stay. The jamboree was a beehive of activity for days —sporting activities, educational programs, skills contests, camp fires, singing, patriotic programs, etc. I remember carrying with me a little hard-earned money and spending a lot of it buying various souvenirs. It is one of my earliest recollections of useless trinket collection. Many of my newly acquired souvenirs were immediately traded at some of the huge trading bazaars that were set up. I distinctly recall the dismal feeling of a sense of loss. I
felt a strong suspicion that perhaps I had been outsmarted by some urbane traders who might have been much shrewder than I was.
This was an early lesson that served me well later in life. I resolved then and there to be more careful with my hard-earned money. I also remember meeting scouts from all over the U.S. and keeping a log of their names and addresses. I later communicated with many of them as pen pals from time to time.
From Valley Forge, our bus made a side trip to New Y ork City,
where we had the privilege of seeing the Brooklyn Dodgers and
Chicago Cubs play in Ebbets Field and a ttending a Billy Graham crusade service in Yankee Stadium. Both were rather historic events.
At this Dodgers game, Duke Snyder hit his three-hundredth career home run. Also, it was one of the last games in historic Ebbets Field,
which was closed a few weeks later when the Dodgers moved to Los
Angeles. There were over 100,000 people at the Billy Graham service,
which was later reported to be the largest crowd ever gathered for a religious event. The good fortune of me being at these two unique occasions was nothing short of amazing. Like the Oak Ridge trip,
the long journey home was not so bad because of the panoply of remarkable experiences that kept tumbling over in my mind. Thirty-five years later, when I was living in Philadelphia, I went out to Valley
Forge National Park one Sunday afternoon and found the exact spot where we had pitched our tent. I sat down on the grass and relived in my mind every moment of the experiences that I could recall.
A couple of years later, I found myself in New Y ork City again,
marching down Fi fth Avenue with the Lion’s All-State Band.
Sidestepping the story about how this opportunity came about, I
have fond memories of this experience as well. It was on this trip that
I saw my first Broadway musical —My Fair Lady with the original cast, including Rex Harrison and Julie Andrews. This was truly a spellbinding experience. I have listened to the sound track of that performance hundreds of times over the years. Each time I hear the overture theme and the score for “I Could Have Danced All Night” ,
those same goosebumps pop up on my arms —the same ones that appeared on a mesmerized seventeen-year-old si tting in orchestra seats in the Broadway Theater. How could one ever forget such an experience?
And then there were a number of travel experiences closer to home. These were mostly related to Boy Scout camping activities and band events. I served as a counselor one summer at Camp Palila in Louisville for a couple of weeks. Our band marched in parades throughout the state, usually around Christmas time, and I attended a band clinic in Jackson and one on the Gulf Coast. My recollection of these experiences is peppered with emotional memories related to my observations about the skills of musicians from other places.
The competition curve was noticeably steeper, I quickly noticed. I
was accustomed, for example, to being one of the best musicians in our high school band, but when I attended the state band clinics,
I was slapped with the reality of my own mediocrity. These were humiliating realizations at times, but valuable lessons nevertheless.
They were lessons I would never have learned if I had stayed at home.
It was during my senior year in high school that I reached a decision to a ttend the University of Southern Mississippi, which was a journey itself in a way. I wanted to engage with a world beyond my small-town con finement. Largely for this reason, I wandered two hundred miles away for a four-year odyssey in Ha ttiesburg,
Mississippi. My parents were clearly disappointed by my exile, as were many others in the local community. Some looked upon me as a disloyal deserter. But it was the right decision for me. No doubt about it.
Inching into my college days, my glimpses of the world outside began to expand. I became active in the Student Government
Association, and through that medium I had the opportunity to participate in activities of the Southern Universities Student
Government Association (SUSGA), an organization of student leaders throughout the South. I attended conventions at Clemson and Florida State University and visited other universities. These were nothing short of eye-popping experiences for me in so many respects. I met fascinating student leaders and saw impressive college venues and programs that would have been foreign to me if I had remained tucked away in my dorm playing cards with the locals. It was at one of these SUSGA conventions, for example, that I first met and became friends with T rent Lo tt, who later became a powerful
United States senator. A few years ago, T rent and I reminisced about the value of these student government experiences. The student leadership days and the associated travel le ft enormous lifetime impressions on me. They became an integral part of my personal fabric as I matured over the years.
On a different plane, I also remember walking across the USM
campus one morning on a Saturday in February, with my friend
John Simpson, when he said, “Hey, why don’t we go down to Mardi
Gras this weekend?” At the time, Mardi Gras was nothing more than two words to me; I knew absolutely nothing about it other than the fact that it was some kind of celebration in New Orleans.
Without breaking stride, John and I continued walking, straight to the highway, stuck out our thumbs and hitchhiked to New Orleans.
Just like that. We probably had less than $5 in our pockets between us. A fter three or four rides, we somehow arrived in the French
Quarter mid-afternoon. We immediately joined in all the festivities and revelry, wandering from bar to bar far into the night. Most of the details of that evening are a vague fog today, but I do have vivid memories of seeing and doing things I had never even thought about,
much less experienced. Another notch on my wanderlust gun!
During my senior year at USM, I began campus interviews with a number of firms. These all went well, and I was invited to visit businesses in Memphis, New Orleans, Dallas and Houston.
Naturally, I accepted all these invitations and embarked on expense-paid treks to these places. I marveled at the tall buildings, nice hotels and plush offices. The splendor of these business environments was magnetic and made my career decision very difficult. But the overall attraction of the travel experiences, frankly, was just as exciting as the job opportunities. Once again I was seeing new worlds previously foreign to me!
So, by college graduation I had developed a track record of wandering around and thoroughly enjoying it. My life experiences had been immeasurably enriched by my limited travels —whether it was Banner, Oak Ridge, New Orleans or New Y ork—and I knew it. This set the stage for a lifetime of worldwide adventure and an understanding of the emotions embodied in Dr. Seuss’s classic, Oh,
The Places I Have Been.
