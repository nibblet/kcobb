## Full Text

T o God Be The Glory
The First Presbyterian Church of Fort Lauderdale has a special dinner for elders and trustees in January of each year. At this dinner, the newly elected members are expected to stand and tell the group about their religious experience. The charge is to present one’s personal testimony, as it is called in Baptist churches. It is an emotional time for most people —both the presenters and the audience members —and most of the speakers are nervous and anxious. I have sat through several of these evenings during my years as a church elder, and invariably come away with a warm glow after hearing so many beautiful stories. My story has been presented twice, and the following paragraphs re flect much of the message I
have attempted to relate.
I have been associated with the church all of my life. My Christian faith has been a cornerstone of who I am as a person. From as early as I can remember, my mother hustled me o ff to the First Baptist
Church on Sundays and to all of the special programs sponsored by the church—Sunday School, T raining Union, Royal Ambassadors,
etc. We were usually there when the doors opened.
A highlight of every summer was Vacation Bible School (VBS).
It is uncanny how the messages of those summer programs remain lodged in my mind. We attended classes, sang songs, played games and involved ourselves with various cra fts. My favorite cra ft was building birdhouses. I always painted mine bright red. But VBS was not all fun and games. We also learned to recite the sixty-six books of the Holy Bible and memorized scripture verses. I can still visualize the colored cards —green, purple, yellow, orange, etc. —with printed memory verses and rote memory exercises. I remember once memorizing the entire chapter of Ephesians 6, all twenty-three verses. I still think of those days whenever I hear words or phrases like “the armor of God” or “breastplate of righteousness” . I remember maps of the Holy Land, pictures of Christ and the apostles, and larger-than-life stories of Bible characters —King Solomon, King
David, Samson, the Apostle Paul and many others. I remember learning and reciting the pledge of allegiance to the flags—both the
American flag and the Christian flag. The moderator would intone
“Attention, salute, pledge,” as we all lined up at the front of the sanctuary. There was a lot of meat in that VBS sandwich!
In 1952, when I was eleven years old, all of those messages from Sunday School, T raining Union and VBS began to come together and take shape at VBS during an intense week of Bible drills, scripture memorization, music, learning projects, etc. I can remember going home near the end of that week and talking with my mother and father about believing in Christ as my savior and what that meant. At the time, my father was not a Christian and,
accordingly, he was not much help. However, he seemed unusually interested, and he involved himself in the discussions. My mother,
who also felt ill prepared for the discussion, invited our minister,
Brother Clarence Cutrell, to come by our home and counsel with us.
As I re flect on this event, I can visualize Brother Cutrell’s piercing eyes, hear his gentle voice with so serious a tone, and feel his firm hand on my shoulder. He seemed to understand me. He made me feel so special that day, and I sensed that he had been sent by God to see me. Perhaps he had. The upshot of this experience was that my father and I both prayed to receive Christ into our lives at the same time. We were baptized together the following week. That day has been an abiding special memory for me for the past sixty years, for it was at that time that I formed an eternal bond with Christ and also a special bond with my father.
Despite being a callow eleven-year-old, I clearly recognized my profession of faith as a life-altering experience. I regarded my action as a firm commitment. No turning back. T oday, I’m pleased that my life is still guided by that pivotal commitment and the knowledge that it will always be so directed.
Dot and I were exposed to the same Christian environment and were grounded in the same fundamentals. We exchanged our marriage vows at the altar of the First Baptist Church. Throughout our marriage, we have struggled mightily to keep those vows, as well as the fundamentals that we learned in the church at a young age.
We have attempted to provide the same Christian leadership to our children that our parents and our church gave to us. God has blessed our lives immensely.
Much has been said and written about standing on the shoulders of giants, and I can name a number of giants in my spiritual background. Brother Cutrell stands at the head of that line. It was through his tutelage and leadership that I began to take spirituality seriously. There have been many others —some I knew personally and some I knew only from a distance. Some of my favorite pastors whom I have known and admired personally were Reverend J.C.
Mitchell of First Baptist Church in Winter Park, Florida; Dr. D.
James Kennedy of Coral Ridge Presbyterian Church; and Dr.
Douglas Brouwer of First Presbyterian in Fort Lauderdale.
I should also mention a special place in my heart for Reverend
Joseph Sellinger, a Jesuit priest who I came to know when I lived in
Baltimore. Father Sellinger (he was also called Father Joe) was truly an ecumenical man —the apotheosis of a servant of God. I believe he was closer to God than any person I have ever known. Sitting with him in his living room on the Loyola University campus, where he was president, was like being lifted out of reality and into another realm.
It was truly a “thin place” , an expression in early Celtic Christianity describing a mesmerizing place where the distance between heaven and earth collapses. This man le ft an enormous impression on me.
Even though I moved to Philadelphia, I travelled back to Baltimore in 1993 to attend his funeral, along with a thousand other admirers of this great man.
Descriptions of other Christian giants I have admired and respected, people on whose shoulders I have stood, would fill an entire chapter in this memoir.
I have participated in a wide range of Christian service areas over the years. These include teaching Sunday School, singing in choirs,
serving on commi ttees, completing intense Bethel Bible study programs, attending men’s Bible studies, making educational trips to Israel and participating in scores of other programs and spiritual growth activities over the years. At First Presbyterian, I have served two terms as an elder and chaired the personnel commi ttee for a couple of years. In the 2005–2006 era, I chaired a successful capital campaign that raised over $16 million for new facilities. Every single one of these activities has enriched my life and served to enhance my faith.
I have always been curious about organized religion and occasionally have spent time trying to learn what the other major religions are all about. Y ears ago, when we were on vacation in
Hawaii, a Mormon missionary stopped me on the street and engaged me in a polite discussion about the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter Day Saints. He gave me a copy of the Book of Mormon. On the long trip home, I read the entire book, cover to cover, and found it fascinating. While I thought some of it was questionable, I came to realize that most of the message is sound and makes a lot of sense.
Another time, when I was in upstate New Y ork and paid a visit to a Buddhist temple, a monk sat down with me for two hours trying to explain the fundamentals of Buddhism. For some odd reason, I
was surprised at how reasonable Buddhism is, too. I have also read a number of books on comparative religion. One interesting scholarly study was a comparison of Christianity, Judaism and Islam in a book called From Moses to Mohammed by Jean Pierre Isbouts. I spent a lot of interesting hours with Isbouts on a cruise in Africa. One of the main points of this book is that much of the Christian Old T estament shows up almost verbatim in the Koran. All of this has enriched my understanding of the world’s religions and made me more respectful of different points of view. It has also helped dispel my notions that religions other than mine are somehow kooky or spooky. However,
none of it has ever pried me away from my Christian fundamentals.
Those are my roots, and I draw my strength from them.
We have found the Holy Land a fascinating and absorbing place to visit. As we toured the countryside with an expert Israeli guide, all that I had learned in those Sunday School and Vacation Bible School days suddenly sprang to life. But the realities of these places were often different from what I had read in stories, heard in sermons, and seen in paintings and illustrations.
I had imagined the countryside to be much larger that it actually is, and the landscape to be much different. I learned that Bethlehem was a suburb of Jerusalem, only six miles outside the main gates of the city. Shocking. Likewise, Jericho was only seventeen miles away. And I never realized that Jericho was simply a lonely oasis, a green spot poking up out of a desolate desert, surrounded by barren sand as far as the eye can see. I had heard so much about the Jordan
River and had always visualized it as a major navigable waterway. In fact, it turned out to be a stream that one can wade across in places.
In Mississippi, we called those creeks! And the Sea of Galilee is a relatively small lake, about one-tenth the size of Lake Okeechobee in Florida. Notwithstanding these physical revelations, the spiritual significance of the Holy Land was very moving. I have deep and abiding memories of standing on the shores of the Sea of Galilee,
walking along the Via Dolorosa, si tting on a bench in the Garden of Gethsemane, visiting the Mount of Olives and seeing so many other remarkable sights. For any Christian, these are unforge ttable experiences.
For more than fifty years, Dot and I have been keenly conscious of the unusual blessings God has bestowed upon us. This awareness has been a potent reminder of an obligation we have both felt to stay grounded in our faith. We also have a conviction that we have a stewardship responsibility to others. Throughout our adult lives,
we have tried to go the extra mile in supporting our church and the community at large. And we have enjoyed every minute of it. We believe we are called upon to be contributors of both our time and our money —payback—and we a ttempt to consistently live up to that core belief.
Although my personal commitment to Christ has never wavered,
I must acknowledge that the level of my personal Christian service has vacillated over time. We have belonged to several churches as we moved around the country, and some periods of my life have been more active than others. I have sometimes likened my activity to an oscillating fan moving from side to side. Occasionally the air faded away a bit and sometimes it blew strongly right in my face. But the soft, cool breeze of God’s presence in my life has always been there—a steady, comforting feeling for me.
The basic story of my spiritual experience is one of gratitude. I am incredibly grateful for so many ways in which God has blessed my life.
Through the leadership of many Christian mentors, I have learned about the immensity and greatness of God. Through his grace, I have been blessed with so many gi fts, some of which I admi ttedly have managed to squander along the way. I have matured as a Christian over the years as I studied and participated in organized religious programs. But my roots are still planted in the fundamentals drilled into me at Vacation Bible School at the Calhoun City First Baptist
Church more than sixty years ago. The message is the same. The fundamentals have not changed. I just believe I understand them a little better. Simply put, I am grateful.
“T o God be the glory. Great things he hath done. ”
