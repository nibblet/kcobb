# Bayne Cobb, Exemplar of a Dad

> A son reflects on his father's quiet, steady leadership across family, work, and adversity, highlighting lessons learned by observation over decades.

## Full Text

Bayne Cobb,
Exemplar of a Dad
All during my formative years in the 1940s and 1950s, I
considered my mother the dominant force in our family. She was the most outspoken, developed all the chores and assignments, and micromanaged our daily lives. It was my mother who set the bar and then persistently raised it above our reach. She was also the one who invariably dished out the punishment for misbehavior or generally not living up to her standards. So, I grew up thinking that my mother ruled the roost, so to speak.
What I failed to realize at the time, however, was that there was a gentle guiding force in the background—a quiet man with a strong work ethic who was the basic provider for the family. He was also a man who was grounded in solid principles. It wasn’t until many years passed that I slowly began to realize and appreciate the fundamental character and gentle spirit that formed the backdrop and foundation for our family over the years.
A bit about my father’s background. Bayne Cobb was born and raised in Banner, a tiny, unincorporated, non-descript community in northeast Mississippi. He was a twel fth-generation descendent of the Cobbs who moved from North Carolina to this small locale in the 1839. His father was the local postmaster, an accomplished gardener and an expert fisherman; Papa Cobb was an intelligent man with an easy-going and gentle spirit, a personality trait that he no doubt handed down to his son.
We don’t know a lot about Daddy’s formative years, except that he started working at a young age in sawmills. He went to Bruce
High School, about eight miles away from his home, and played basketball there. That was in the midst of the Great Depression —
tough economic times in Mississippi. I can remember stories of how he walked three miles from his home to catch the school bus each day. After high school, he attended Draughon’s Business College in
Memphis. He then returned home and found a job as a truck driver for The Justice Company in Calhoun City, where he worked for the next thirty-five years.
Daddy was a handsome blade in his young years, judging by some good photographs from that era. He met and married Frances
Clements, a hairdresser from a nearby community, in 1940. They set up housekeeping on South Madison Street in a house deeded to Daddy in lieu of a bonus at The Justice Company. For the rest of their lives they lived at this spot, in this original house and then another on the same property.
Over the years, Daddy moved from truck driver to shipping clerk and then to route salesman, a position he held for about twenty-five years. He drove around Calhoun and Grenada counties each week calling on grocers and country stores, taking orders for groceries that would be delivered the next day. The hours were long, but he loved it.
He thoroughly enjoyed visiting with the store owners, listening and telling tall tales, and cajoling them into buying more merchandise.
Then, abruptly on Christmas Eve in 1965, Mr. Justice appeared at the
Grenada warehouse and announced that the business was closing at the end of the year. My father received a $25 Christmas bonus coupled with a one-week notice of his termination. There was no severance pay. After thirty- five years of faithful service! Our entire family was devastated and frantic, except for Daddy, who seemed amazingly unflustered by it all. Fortunately, in a matter of days, State
Cash Wholesale came along, purchased the warehouse in Grenada and opened a new business there. As if a miracle sent directly from heaven, they asked my father to manage the warehouse. So, he never skipped a beat—not even a day of work —and was employed there for the next ten years. After that, he worked part time for several years at Rupert’s Supermarket, once again in the grocery business that he loved—a business where he had spent the rest of his adult life.
Daddy had many admirable qualities, which I gradually came to appreciate over the years. Although he was not a super intellect or highly educated, he was wise about much of the ways of the world.
T ravelling from store to store and dealing with simple country folk over the years gave him remarkable insight into human nature and a unique understanding of people. He genuinely respected people —
almost everyone —and rarely ever u ttered a negative comment about anyone.
As I have come to realize over time, I learned many valuable life lessons from observing my father. My mother taught me speci fic skills and pushed me into myriad learning environments in school,
church and elsewhere. Notwithstanding her constant hovering and close supervision, however, it was my father who instilled in me the overarching values that have guided much of my life. A few things come to mind quickly. First, he was simply a decent guy. He was an extremely conscientious and responsible man, taking care of his family first and thinking of himself more as an afterthought. He was trustworthy and honest to the bone. He was frugal and very careful with his money. He had an admirable work ethic, diligently applying himself both at his job and at home. He was a nice guy with an abiding good spirit.
I learned from my father mostly through observation. Thinking of him, I am reminded of a business management exercise I saw where the moderator of a large group provided detailed oral instructions on how to perform a task in three minutes. No one could do it. He then passed out detailed wri tten instructions, which yielded the same result. He then said, “Let me show you how it’s done. ” Everyone in the audience then completed the task in less than two minutes.
Daddy wasn’t one to deal out a lot of instructions or spend time in an official teaching mode. Rather, he just plodded along demonstrating his life according to his own personal golden rule. We were always along to watch and learn from his exemplary example.
I have told many people over the years that I never once saw my father really angry. In fact, in all my years, I never heard him raise his voice. Not once. At his funeral in 2009, Reverend Jimmy Vance related this fact to the a ttendees and asked for a show of hands by anyone who thought their family or friends would be able to say the same thing about them. No hands went up. Even in times of normal family stress, Daddy was always cool, calm and collected. I remember once I wrecked the family car coming home from an errand to the grocery, turning it over completely on its side. While Mother was focused on the drama of the situation and my recklessness, Daddy simply rose from his chair, walked over to the accident with me,
called on some friends to help turn the car back over and told me to drive it home. His only comment to me was, “Son, you need to be a little more careful on those dirt roads, especially when the gravel is piled up in the center as it was in this case. ” I got the point! And
I also got a valuable lesson in dealing thoughtfully with adversity.
As a result of this incident and other similar ones, I developed what
I think is an uncommon resilience to hardship. In situations where many other people panic or let excitement overwhelm them, I
usually experience the opposite. An odd sense of calmness seems to overtake me, to the point that I can feel my body relaxing. I attribute this unique trait to a childhood environment where my father was never rattled or discomposed.
Daddy was frugal to a fault—a trait passed down from his parents,
no doubt. He was extremely careful about all things financial. He disliked debt and didn’t want to owe anyone anything. When the new house was built in 1955, there was quite a bit of stress in the family over the costs. He calmly but strongly resisted Mother’s sweet tooth for all the nicer things and ultimately guided the process to a reasonable compromise. They borrowed $7,500 on a twenty-year mortgage from a savings and loan in Grenada. Haunted by the debt,
Daddy immediately began to make double payments each month and ultimately paid o ff the mortgage in seven years. They never owned a car until he saved $1,200 to buy a new Plymouth for cash in
1951. Every car he ever owned after that was paid for in cash. I don’t think he ever bought a new tire in his lifetime. He always had his old ones recapped or bought used recapped tires from Funderburg’s garage. He even bought used shoes from Cumberland’s Shoe Repair
Shop, claiming he liked them because they were already “broken in” .
Sandy and I have kibitzed and joked about Daddy’s parsimony over the years, observing that he was “tight as the bark on a tree” , as he would say. Nevertheless, we both learned valuable lessons from him about financial responsibility. I seriously doubt that I would be in the financial position I am today, were it not for his leadership in this area.
One particularly poignant moment in the many memories of my father occurred during my junior year in college. I came home at Christmas time and announced that Humble Oil & Re fining
Company had o ffered me an internship in New Orleans for the following summer at a salary of $425 per month. Sitting in his recliner
Daddy seemed unusually moved by this announcement. Big tears began to drift down his face (one of the few times I ever saw such an emotion). Mother promptly called me aside and reprimanded me,
saying that my bragging had embarrassed my father because that was more than he had ever earned in his life. I actually didn’t think that was the case, even at the time. He probably did, in fact, earn less than that, but I didn’t think that was the cause of his emotion. I thought those were tears of pride and I still prefer to believe that.
I also learned some valuable lessons in how Daddy treated my mother. As mentioned, Mother was a dominant force in our family.
She inserted herself into every nook and cranny of our lives with her exceptional intelligence and industriousness. Y et she was in ill health much of her life, with one serious ailment after another, both physical and emotional, as I will describe later. In this challenging environment, my father was nothing short of a saint! He readily took a back seat to all of the hustle and bustle that always surrounded my mother, particularly in the smaller, more insigni ficant matters, and was always unflappable. He invariably kept a cool head and focused stubbornly on doing what was right and prudent, quietly and without fanfare. He treated Mother with respect and seemed to have a patient insight into the emotional rollercoaster she was on. In the final years before her death in 2001, he was the consummate doting husband, tending to her every need and desire. I thoroughly admired his faithfulness and commitment for the sixty-one years they were married, and gleaned some truly priceless lessons from his example.
Again, not because he told me anything speci fically; I just watched him.
I also learned valuable lessons in how he treated Sandy and me.
The word that comes to mind when I think of this is respect. For as far back as I can remember, he treated us both like real people, not as little children, pets or inferior subordinates. I spent many hours trailing along behind him in his garden or squatting in the back yard working on cranky lawn mowers. I felt like his partner. Watching and learning all the time. From time to time, he would take one of us along on his sales route. During these long days, he never lectured us or even overtly showed us many things. He merely allowed us to hang around watching him ply his trade. Those were long days, but
I can still remember them vividly. During the tough teenage years,
he seemed to somehow understand me. And Sandy as well. Mother seemed to take a stronger hand with Sandy than me for some reason,
perhaps because she was a girl and more vulnerable. Or perhaps it was because she was simply two and a half years younger and, due to the age gap, couldn’t seem to measure up to what I was doing.
Anyway, Daddy was always uniquely protective of Sandy and defended her rigorously. While at the time, I wondered about the apparent discrimination, I still admired Daddy’s defense of my sister and his watching her back.
Daddy’s life changed dramatically when Mother died. Although he was passionately devoted to her well-being and was bere ft when she passed away, the change in his personality was palpable. It was as if someone switched on the lights! Almost immediately, he morphed in to a more vibrant personality, spread his wings and reached out to the world around him. He had taken care of virtually everything for the last few years, so Mother’s passing really left no vacant gaps or holes to deal with. He mixed and mingled with the town folk in every venue he could think of, with frequent trips to the post office,
grocery stores, drug stores, etc. He was always at church whenever the doors opened. He would go to the grocery several times a week,
usually to multiple stores. He claimed to be shopping for the best prices, but an underlying reason was probably to see people and visit with them. He loved people and they loved him. Universally,
all who knew him considered him friendly, likable, humorous and remarkably fit for his age. Without a doubt, he was a changed man in these final years, I think mostly due to being unshackled from the responsibilities of the previous years.
Daddy’s health was excellent most of his life, up until his last year, when his heart began to fail him. He took good care of himself,
healthwise. He didn’t smoke or drink, and he generally indulged in a moderate diet. I believe the most important factor in his good health,
however, was attributable to the calm, settled way in which he lived his life. He dealt with stress be tter than anyone I have ever known.
Until his final years, when his body began to falter, he was u tterly unshakable in his demeanor. Clearly, his was not a body ravaged with stress. He retired from his grocery work at about age seventy- five,
but remained active, pu ttering around with home repairs, tending his garden and taking care of Mother. He was a master gardener and maintained a full vegetable garden until he was ninety years old —
peas, bu tter beans, string beans, corn, okra, squash, etc. —and of course his prize tomatoes. He drove his car around town every day until he was ninety-one, when Sandy and I insisted that he give it up. This was indeed a painful transition for him, since he had been driving himself places for seventy- five years! He lived alone for the last eight years of his life and took care of himself until he was ninety-two, at which time we had him admitted to a local retirement home.
Although he mildly resisted the move, he found himself immediately happy for the three or four months that he lived there. The reason for this happiness: there were lots of people there. He passed away in the retirement home rather abruptly and without any pain or su ffering on January 4, 2009, two days after his ninety-third birthday. That was a very sad day for me.
There was a time in my life when I would have said I didn’t have much of a relationship with my father. That’s because I didn’t have a full appreciation of what the relationship really was. My admiration and appreciation for him grew steadily over time, mainly because of my own maturity. As I grew older and more introspective, I began to realize the enormous impact he had on my life. I did, in fact,
become closer to him as he aged, staying in regular contact with him,
particularly in the last ten years. I only regret that it took me so long.
But I will be eternally grateful for the relationship and his in fluence on me as a person.
Bayne Cobb was a good man. No, he was a great man! A
tranquil, gentle giant who lived an exemplary life and set an example of citizenship for everyone he touched. I deeply miss him, but feel his presence every day.
Bayne Cobb, “fit as a fiddle” at age ninety
Keith and Dad

## Principles

- Lead by example rather than by instruction.
- Maintain calm composure under stress; model steadiness.
- Prioritize financial responsibility and avoid debt.
- Respect and trust others; assume positive regard.
- Diligent work ethic and consistent service define leadership.

## Decision heuristics

- When faced with sudden job loss, remain composed, assess immediate local opportunities, and accept stable roles that preserve continuity.
- When financing major commitments, prioritize saving and accelerated repayment to eliminate debt sooner.
- Teach through demonstration: show the behavior you want others to learn instead of lengthy instruction.
- Respond to mistakes with calm, practical corrective actions and succinct guidance rather than drama.
- Avoid unnecessary debt for consumer goods; delay purchase until you can pay cash.
