## Full Text

KPMG—Baltimore
Baltimore has been described as the largest small town in America and as the smallest large town in America. That characterization fits well. And for that reason it fit almost perfectly for us when KPMG
asked us to move there in 1989.
The Baltimore office had been an important cog in the KPMG
wheel for decades with fifteen partners and about 150 professionals.
The office serviced an impressive array of important clients who had been loyal to the firm for many years.
Over the years, the office had developed a rather insular personality, coupled with an exaggerated office collegiality. Most of the professionals in the office had begun their careers right there,
right out of college. That is to say that a large part of their day-to-day activity, other than routine servicing of existing clients, was focused on each other, not on the business community or strategically directing the practice. It was a brotherhood of sorts. Accordingly,
the growth of the office had stagnated, and the financial results were shriveling. The firm’s executive committee read this phenomenon correctly and reasoned that the office needed a leadership change—a leader from outside the practice. Meanwhile, Arch Fort, who had been the managing partner for twenty- five years, had recently reached retirement age and announced his departure from the firm.
The heir apparent to the managing partner position was Art
Mehlman, a fine man who had spent his entire career in the office.
However, the executive committee passed over him, to his extreme disappointment and that of most of the partners in the office. This was my first hurdle —winning over the partners who were deeply loyal and supportive of Art. I liked Art a lot, but knew that I had to rise above this hurdle quickly.
Our Baltimore experience was essentially an applied laboratory for both Dot and me. We took everything we had learned in Fort
Lauderdale, Orlando and Jackson and put it all to work for us. We hit the ground running. We le ft our mistakes behind in the closet and resolved not to duplicate them. In short, our life in Baltimore was a wonderful two years for us.
Although the office was a bit larger than Fort Lauderdale, I
sensed that I knew exactly what to do. Administration was a piece of cake. The KPMG ropes were as plain to me as the back of my hand. At first, I spent some time ge tting to know the partners and staff, but soon I also began hi tting the streets. It became clear that the partners were not much help on the streets, but I plodded on. I
was able to persuade a few partners to introduce me to some of the major clients, usually at the second-tier financial management level,
but managed to leverage a few of these contacts, as well as a few of my Florida contacts, for further introductions.
I also called a few key high-pro file business organizations in
Baltimore and asked if I could get involved —not unlike my calls to David Rush and Hank Watson in Fort Lauderdale years before.
Not surprising to me, but astonishing to our partners, most of them jumped at the o ffer. I made a call like this to the Greater Baltimore
Committee, the leading business organization. The next day I
received a call from Ben Civile tti, chairman of GBC, inviting me to lunch. Ben was a former U.S. attorney general and the high-pro file senior partner of one of the largest law firms in the city. He became a good business friend, and the next thing I knew I found myself with him, Chip Mason, chairman of Legg Mason, and Maryland
Governor Donald Shae ffer on a private plane on my way down to the Eastern Shore to look at a business development opportunity for the State of Maryland. Ben Civiletti quickly earned a spot on the long list of names in my personal Hall of Fame —people to whom I
owe enormous gratitude for helping me along the way.
Dot and I joined a senior Leadership Baltimore program that paved the way for some valuable relationships, and became involved in the United Way, a long-time stalwart for us. Along the way, I was appointed to the board of the Baltimore Museum of Art; I also joined the Center Club, a recognized melting pot for the business community, and began having lunch there regularly with local executives. Dot founded and led a terrific United Way organization,
called Friendly Baltimore, that engaged the spouses of transplanted
CEOs moving to the city.
For the most part, our partners’ contacts with our major clients were centered on the client’s chief financial officer or chief accountant. Partners’ contacts with other executives and their clients’ boards of directors were o ften extremely limited, which was uncomfortable for me. I was accustomed to dealing with the senior executives of our clients. One of our major clients was Johns
Hopkins University, a pillar of the community with a high-pro file
Board of T rustees. (Michael Bloomberg was chairman of the board!)
In 1990, they hired a new university president, Bill Richardson, the former provost at Penn State. Richardson was an elusive executive who was sharply focused on several key issues facing the university,
but I wanted to meet him. Our partners’ highest level of contact at
Johns Hopkins was the CFO; they said they could not arrange an introduction to Richardson. I mentioned this to Dot one evening at dinner, and she said, “Oh, I know his wife through Friendly Baltimore.
Let’s invite them to dinner. ” And so it went. The relationships we developed through aggressive networking paid off time and again.
One other client experience that is worth mentioning involves
RTKL Associates, an architectural and engineering firm with a stellar international reputation. KPMG had inherited the firm as a client in the Main Hurdman merger. With that heritage, RTKL was not a part of the office “club” , so to speak, and accordingly had been given short shrift in client service since the Main Hurdman merger. One of our managers was notified that RTKL planned to switch to another firm that promised better service. Again, our sole contact there was between a tax manager in our office and the controller at RTKL.
Our partners were resigned to lose the account. Not me. I inquired about the client’s leadership, picked up the phone and called Harold
Adams, its chairman and CEO. He was quite cordial and invited me over to his office to discuss the relationship. I came back from that meeting with a new contract —and a new friend. Harold Adams turned out to be a prince of a guy and a highly in fluential figure in the Baltimore business community. His wife, Janice, became one of Dot’s dearest friends. Harold was and still is on the board of directors of Legg Mason, as well as a number of other important organizations. And he was quite helpful to me. T oday, twenty- five years later, I still stay in touch with Harold. I have dinner with him in New Y ork about once a year before an annual conference we both usually attend. And I suspect RTKL might still be a KPMG client.
I also made it a point to get to know our competitors. This rankled most of our office partners. The mantra among the partners went something along the lines of, “We’re the greatest; our competitors stink!” It was a steady drumbeat, loud and repetitive. The partners literally despised the other accounting firms and used up incredible energy to ridicule and demonize them. The facts were plain, however;
they knew hardly anything about our competitors, except for the spurious office lore they themselves had engendered. This of course often led to miscalculations and tactical missteps that resulted in lost opportunities in competitive situations. I didn’t tumble for the lore. I proceeded to call the managing partners of the other five large national firms and invite each of them to lunch, much to the chagrin of my partners. The managing partners at the other firms jumped at the invitation, and through these luncheon meetings, I gathered an enormous amount of factual information, and in the process,
came to respect these people as fellow professionals. They were all aggressive business professionals of the highest quality, and were tightly integrated with their clientele and the business community in general. Not a demon among them! The acquired information served me well in numerous ways over the next two years.
During my second year in Baltimore, the leadership of KPMG
changed as a result of partnership elections. Jon Madonna and
Jim Brocksmith were elected chairman and deputy chairman, and they quickly began to restructure the management of the firm.
Geographic regions were completely rearranged. I was appointed the area partner for the newly formed mid-Atlantic region. With that appointment came a slot as a member of the firm’s Management
Committee. This placed me among the top twenty- five members of the KPMG partnership and bestowed signi ficant responsibility and authority. In addition to my position as managing partner of
Baltimore, I was suddenly responsible for Washington, D.C. and surrounding offices in Maryland and Virginia.
Shortly following the dramatic restructuring of the firm in 1990,
the KPMG Management Commi ttee embarked on a signi ficant downsizing of the partnership, agreeing on a 15 percent reduction in active partners. Each area partner was asked to identify the weakest links in his region and was assigned a proportional number of partners to be counseled out of the firm. I of course had my share of the outplacement strategy and proceeded to focus on three individuals in the Baltimore office. Attempting to be collaborative,
I invited four of the most senior local partners to my home on a
Saturday morning to discuss who should be outplaced. The first two were easy; they were former Main Hurdman partners and thus were anathema to most of the other partners —not legitimate members of the old boys’ club, so to speak. The third candidate
I proposed was Bob Fedo; I had found him to be an extremely non-productive partner with a very light client load and almost no contact outside the inner offices of KPMG Baltimore. The wrinkle was that he was a charter member of the “club” and dearly loved by the other partners. He was a sort of pinch hi tter for the office, fielding technical questions from others. He generally sat around the office kibitzing on all the audit work. From my point of view, he may as well have been the office water boy, worthless as a business professional. The other partners were unanimous in their support of their buddy and literally begged me to spare him from the downsizing. I listened to them politely and thought about their pleas for a while. I then decided to abandon my collaboration strategy. Early Monday morning, I stopped by Bob’s office and gave him the bad news. Interestingly, he did not seem at all astonished,
just surprised that his buddies had been unable to talk me out of it.
I mention this event for a number of reasons. First, I felt good about having the formidability to do the right thing in the face of considerable pressure from a group of mature professionals. Second,
the event established my posture as a leader. Despite the partners’
emotional heartache, they respected the decision-making. Third,
when Bob le ft, it was like removing your finger from a bucket of water and then wondering where the finger was; he was not missed at all, which proved the wisdom of the move. It was absolutely the right decision. Interestingly, over the next few years, countless
Baltimore partners recalled this seminal event and commented to me about how correct the decision was and how misguided their judgment had been.
Another interesting anecdote from that era is lodged in my memory. For years the office had a small satellite office in Columbia,
Maryland; Columbia was the model city developed by the Rouse
Company, and Rouse was a major real estate client. The office was manned by an idle receptionist and served only as a storage dumping place for supplies and outdated audit working papers; it was unused by anyone on our professional staff. When I proposed that we close the office and save the money we were spending on rent, salaries and utilities, the immediate reaction of the office partners was, “Oh,
no! The Columbia office is crucial to our image with the Rouse
Company. Closing it would be a slap in the face to their model city and severely damage our reputation. ” This sounded too much like the old boys club rhetoric, and I didn’t buy it. I placed a call to T ony Deering, the Rouse CEO, and told him I was considering closing our Columbia office as an e fficiency measure. He laughed over the phone and said, “What? Y ou’re telling me you have an office in Columbia? Why? And why are you asking me? Of course you should close it. That doesn’t make any sense at all. ” I actually had him on a speakerphone with two other partners in my office. They were dumbfounded. It was just another example of how a group of people can sit around in their insularity and talk each other into a ridiculous conclusion. This insularity, in my view, was the root of the office’s stagnation over the years.
My intent in these stories is not to disparage the Baltimore partners. They were all fine individuals with high professional standards and an impressive work ethic. They were technically competent and fundamentally good guys. I liked them all. However,
they had allowed themselves to drift away from the marketplace they served, and then rationalized among themselves that their modus operandi was a good thing. My intent is also to illustrate how so many of my previous experiences, along with lessons gleaned from my mentors, were now manifesting themselves in my capabilities and conduct as a more mature professional.
The two years in Baltimore flew by rapidly. In this short time, we were able to reposition the firm in the business community, solidify our relationships with the upper echelons of our major clients,
broaden the professional maturity of the partners and generally get the office back on a positive track.
From a personal point of view, Dot and I thoroughly enjoyed ourselves and were extremely happy in Baltimore. We have many fond memories of our days there and still have friends from that brief period in our lives.
Nevertheless, during our second year in Baltimore and my first months on the KPMG Management Commi ttee, hardly a month passed without a discussion of serious issues that the firm was facing in the Philadelphia office. The commi ttee proposed four or five potential candidates as managing partner replacements, but none of whom worked out, for one reason or another, after they had been vetted. Then, during a management committee meeting in Atlanta, I
ducked out for a pit stop in the men’s room. When I returned to the meeting, Jon Madonna said, “Keith, we’ve just had a little chat while you were out of the room, and we’ve decided that you’re the perfect guy for Philadelphia. ”
I was stunned. Me? I turned to my friend Harold Stokes and asked, “What do I say to this crazy idea?” His humorous retort has stuck with me over the years for some reason. He said, “My friend,
I think you may as well lie down on the track, because the train has already run over you!”
One of the ironies of this development was that Dot and I had taken a spin up to Philadelphia only a couple of weekends before,
just sightseeing and looking around. On the way home, driving past the oil depots and grubby industrial section of the city, Dot said,
“Wow, I don’t think I would ever want to live in a place like this. ”
One can only imagine how difficult my announcement was upon my return from Atlanta.
So, once again we pack up and move out on a new adventure.
Goodbye Friendly Baltimore!

## Principles

- Aggressive, external networking produces tangible business and community results.
- Avoid groupthink and test internal assumptions against external reality.
- Engage clients at senior executive levels to protect and grow major relationships.
- Make difficult personnel decisions when objective performance and client needs demand it.
- Verify internal narratives by asking clients directly before changing operations tied to perceived image.

## Decision heuristics

- When an office lacks senior client access, personally pursue introductions through your contacts and community organizations.
- If formal channels won't produce an introduction, use spouse or informal networks to arrange meetings with senior leaders.
- When a client relationship appears at risk, escalate directly to senior client leadership rather than accept lower-level reports.
- If consensus collaboration with peers is blocked by emotion or loyalty, be prepared to make an independent, defensible decision and act promptly.
- When competitors are being dismissed internally, meet their leaders to gather facts and reduce bias-driven tactical errors.
