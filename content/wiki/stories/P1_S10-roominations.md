## Full Text

Roominations
Age thirteen was a transformational time for me, as I reached a development stage where I began to think for myself and develop a sense of wonder at the world around me. I was beginning to find my way. It was also a transitional time for my parents as they began to realize an ambition—their dream to own a new home.
After years of planning and saving, Bayne and Frances negotiated a deal with Curtis Ball to construct a brand new house.
They watched as workers jacked up the small home they had lived in for so many years and moved it across the ditch to an adjacent lot so that construction could begin on the newly vacated property.
The home construction fascinated me. I watched practically every board and nail put in its place as the home took shape. I knew every detail about the house—the lumber, drywall, hardware, paint,
plumbing, electrical —every detail. It had Masonite siding with
Permastone trim on the front. Large blueprints detailed 1,780 feet of floor space. There were hardwood floors throughout and the kitchen had built-in appliances, an emerging innovation at the time.
The house cost $12,500. Daddy took out a $7,500 mortgage, his first and only debt. He never stopped worrying about it and made double
Childhood home at 401 South Madison Street payments each month until the mortgage was completely retired seven years later.
The most important detail for me was that for the first time,
Sandy and I had our own rooms. My room was on the north end of the house, opposite my parent’s master bedroom and Sandy’s bedroom, and it provided a modicum of privacy for the first time in my life. The room was not huge—about 11 feet x 11 feet—but it was large enough for me to carve out a private existence.
My bedroom was my personal place, with my own personal things. I loved it. It had brand new walnut bedroom furniture, with a matching chest and desk. The bed had a bookcase headboard, and
I could tuck away things that I didn’t want out in the open simply by sliding the little door open and then closing it. A small radio fit neatly into the headboard. With savings from working odd jobs,
I ordered a new stereo hi- fi phonograph, which rested on a stand next to my desk. I also purchased several 33 1/3 stereo records with some of my earnings. Over time, I built up a good collection of great albums, which I stored in a rack under the stereo and played hundreds of times.
A set of 1948 Compton’s Encyclopedias occupied most of a small painted bookcase tucked into the corner of the room. My mother had purchased the encyclopedias for the family under an installment plan and made monthly payments for two or three years, as I recall.
The bookcase also housed my baseball card collection, Boy Scout manuals and miscellaneous keepsakes. Around the room and on the walls, I had photographs, certificates and memorabilia from school,
church, sports teams, the high school band, Boy Scout events and other reminders of my small world.
My room was warm in the winter, a cozy place for a teenager to snuggle and dream in privacy. It was also warm in the summertime!
The house was not air-conditioned. An a ttic fan kept a breeze flowing through the open screened windows. This breeze brought in the distinctive odors of the community —the smells of fresh cut grass and honeysuckle in the summer. It also allowed in the various sounds of the community —birds chirping, the steady hum of the cotton gin two blocks away and the vibrating drone of crickets somewhere out there.
Music filled my room most of the time. I owned the soundtrack from My Fair Lady and listened to the beautiful music as I memorized every word of every song. I also had the soundtrack from The Eddy
Duchin Story, featuring Carmen Cavallaro on the piano. I must have played it a thousand times. I listened hundreds of times to Van
Cliburn’s recording of T chaikovsky’s Concerto in B Flat Minor and his historic performance of Rachmanino ff’s Concerto No. 3 in D
Minor. His artistry and the haunting melodies of the Rachmaninoff music were mesmerizing. When I hear it today, more than fifty years later, it takes me back to my solitude in this small room.
Late at night, I tuned my little orange radio to WWL, a 50,000-watt station in Chicago, which seemed to me to be a million miles away. The station delivered wonderful music, brought to me over the airwaves by loquacious DJs who seemed to know everything about contemporary music. I would close my eyes and wonder what
Chicago might be like and what these DJs were really like in person.
I also listened to sports on my radio. Mostly, I was interested in the St. Louis Cardinals. Although I had never travelled to many places outside of Calhoun City, I could listen to that little orange radio and imagine myself in Busch Stadium in St. Louis, si tting beside Harry Caray as he broadcast the Cardinal games over station
KMOX. I imagined that Harry was my best friend and that I was a valuable assistant to him because I knew so much about the Cardinal players. I even learned to mimic his unique voice as he stretched the first syllables of words and varied the pitch of his commentary.
“Hooooly Cow!” Maybe he could use some help, I thought. I knew the statistics of all the Cardinal players by heart. I knew every obscure fact about Stan Musial, even the fact that he played the harmonica, as
I did. I could lie there on my bed and visualize Stan the Man vividly in my mind —Number 6, left-handed, standing eagerly at first base and later hi tting a triple o ff the right field wall. It was almost real.
Well before the age of sports on television, all these fantasies played out in virtual T echnicolor right there in my little domain.
The encyclopedias were more than a mere adornment for my room. They represented my personal portal to the rest of the world.
I would spend endless hours thumbing through the fifteen volumes.
I routinely read at random about hundreds of things I knew nothing about. Sometimes I would just read through them alphabetically.
I would look up Poetry in search of some fact, then flip the pages and find myself reading about Poisonous Plants, only to flip the pages again and find fascinating information about Poland—and so on through the material. All manner of arcana seemed to be at my fingertips. There was so much I didn’t know! Curiosity welled up inside me.
I took the things I learned in my high school classes back to my personal space—my room—to contemplate and re flect. It seemed that so much of my life gravitated to this room, as though some magic magnet was trying to bring the universe into focus for me,
there in my personal window to the world.
The local culture was insular, at least in my view at the time.
The expectation was that all young people would live out their lives close to their families and would be unquestionably loyal to the local community. High school graduates who went to college normally attended a nearby community college, Ole Miss or Mississippi
State, all of which were less than fifty miles away, came home every weekend and then for good once their college careers ended. Only a handful ventured beyond this area, and most of those never returned. I admired the loyalty and the glue that bound these folks together, and I still do. However, I reasoned —perhaps selfishly—
that it wasn’t for me.
It was in this private place that I began to let my mind wander and began to set my sights on the future. Here in this small room,
much of my fundamental philosophy and destiny developed. I
can remember marveling at all I read, wondering where all these things were. Most everything I saw in Calhoun City could be characterized as a simple life, certainly not as complex and worldly as the thousands of topics covered in the books, school, the radio and musical recordings I was being exposed to. I wondered about what might be on the other side, in places beyond what I had seen.
How were all these things accomplished? Where were these creative people who did such spectacular things —music, art, literature,
engineering, government, travel, sports, etc.? Were they available for me to experience? Would I ever see such things? Surely, they wouldn’t come to Calhoun City! So, with this backdrop of incessant questions, I began to plot my exodus.
Serendipity arrived at my doorstep. I had a chance to a ttend the University of Southern Mississippi, two hundred miles away in Hattiesburg. I seized this opportunity, much as an animal seizes its prey. Thus began my journey toward another world. I was so fascinated by my new surroundings that I only returned home three times my freshman year—Thanksgiving, Christmas and Easter—to the dismay and consternation of my parents. From there I seemed to never stop wandering and I never lost my lust for knowledge and adventure.
There have been lots of times over the last forty or fifty years when I missed my family and old friends. But honestly, the thing
I really missed the most about my hometown surroundings was the solitude and contemplation that took place in that 11 feet x 11
feet room on South Madison Street —a truly emotional place for me. Fi fty years later, just before we sold the house following my father’s death, I made one last visit there, alone. The house had been stripped of all its furnishings. I went into that empty room on the northeast corner of the house and sat down on the barren floor for about an hour. Five years of the most critical time in my life dri fted through my mind as I revisited and cherished the chunks of warm memories of those days in my room. I vaguely recalled an old poem that I had once memorized. It had a verse that went something along the following lines: “My old and cherished friends from years gone by, now changed they are as figures in a dream. But this special place remains the same and most certainly will always reign supreme. ” My room was a special place!
