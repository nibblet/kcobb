Public Service 101
By the end of my freshman year in college, I had met a lot of people and forged solid friendships with students from various venues—my classes, my dormitory, the athletic teams, ROTC and social hangouts on the campus. The University of Southern Mississippi had quickly become my new home, a home without the constant tension caused by my mother’s desperate unhappiness and dissatisfaction with her life. Somewhere along the line that year, friends suggested that I run for the Student Senate and represent some of the non-fraternity/
sorority constituencies. The Greek community had dominated most of the student government organizations for years. Although these fraternity and sorority members were generally viewed as elitists by the rank and file student body, they seemed to know the ropes and managed to get elected to all the high-profile offices.
I mulled this over for a while. My involvement in leadership positions in high school had given me some experience. But I was cautious about wading into a coterie of the privileged and perhaps finding myself out of place. Finally, with some prodding by fellow students, I took the plunge. Some of my friends organized a phalanx of active supporters. And voilà! I was elected.
Thus began my college political career.
I served in the Senate throughout my sophomore year and thoroughly enjoyed every minute of it. I never missed a meeting and participated in the meetings actively. What I quickly noticed was that the majority of the members of the Senate didn’t take the position seriously. They spent most of their time relishing their status and notoriety, and did little real work. I was just the opposite.
I did my homework before every meeting, worked harder than most anyone and represented my constituencies aggressively. Needless to say, this diligence paid off. Before long, I was recognized as a serious leader among this group, a group comprised largely of socialites. So when springtime came, I signed up for the next election, this time as a junior senator. I was easily elected, despite some aggressive competition from fraternity row.
As a second-year senator, I began introducing and ramming through a spate of student legislation that I believed bene fitted all students, and becoming more involved with the executive branch of the Student Government Association (SGA). My good friend John
Simpson was the SGA president. He was a bright and talented guy,
well-liked by everyone. But he was not big on process or details. In those arenas, he defaulted to me and appointed me administrative assistant to the president. Before long, I was practically running the place, and by the end of that year it seemed only natural to me that I
should run for SGA president and succeed John.
I won the election handily and assumed the office in late spring of 1962. So there I was: a country boy from tiny Calhoun City, duly elected as the leader of a college population of roughly five thousand students. I felt like I had arrived! I remember calling my parents to tell them the good news. T o my dismay, neither of them could connect with what I was talking about and didn’t understand thesignificance of my election. Their attitude was sort of a “whatever… ” . They were
College politics no longer my entire world, however. I regained my exuberance and enthusiasm, and poured myself into this new responsibility.
The next year was one of the most pivotal in my life —mostly positive, but at times a bit negative.
The trappings of political office and the a ttendant power,
whether perceived or real, were intoxicating. I have o ften since likened the experience to being the mayor of a small municipality. I
was even paid a salary of $100 per month, funded by the university’s student activity fees. The SGA had an office in the Student Union building that was all fitted out with desks, typewriters, telephones,
file cabinets and such. As if in a flash, I found myself the center of a great deal of a ttention. I was consulted regularly by the Dean of
Student A ffairs and called upon occasionally by the university’s president. Other student leaders sought my counsel or in fluence. I
was asked to speak to various student groups, was interviewed and frequently asked for comments by the college newspaper and invited to greet visiting dignitaries. It was a constant whirlwind of activity,
requiring high energy and a sense of weighty personal responsibility.
I was even invited to fraternity parties. And all this commotion was taking place while I was trying to carry on a romance with Dot one hundred miles away and conducting interviews for my career following graduation. I was a very, very busy guy.
The Student Government Association consumed my senior year. I practically lived in the SGA offices. I normally arrived at the office immediately after an early breakfast and was usually kicked out by Bud Kirkpatrick when the building was closed and the doors locked at about eleven p.m. My dormitory room was nothing more than a place to sleep. I kept all my books and even changes of clothes in the SGA office. I went to classes sporadically, despite taking some advanced academic courses. By this time, most of the professors knew me well and were aware of my leadership role. My excellent academic record was an asset in this regard, and they cut me some slack insofar as a ttendance was concerned. I was still expected to deliver on the periodic tests and examinations, however, which I did.
Somehow I stumbled through this frenetic academic year, got good grades and managed to graduate with honors.
We accomplished a lot in that 1962–1963 school year. A few specific things stand out in my mind.
• Election misconduct had been a perennial problem, with substantial criticism of the fraternities and sororities; they shouldered much of the blame for perceived unfairness in the process. Accusations of fraud abounded. We streamlined the election process and instituted internal controls over it. We even initiated computerized balloting, years before such a thing emerged in large municipalities and long before computers were in general use anywhere. At the time, IBM was developing a punched card balloting process, and we asked if we could be used as a test market.
(No, I don’t remember anything about hanging chads. They showed up forty- five years later!) The computer balloting was a marvelous success, and we demonstrated the system at SUSGA (Southern
University Student Government Associations) conferences around the South. I don’t know if we were actually the first out of the gate with this technology, but we certainly were one of the first.
• Our institution was lobbying the state legislature for a name change from Mississippi Southern College to the University of
Southern Mississippi. This was a big deal in the minds of our faculty and students. It represented a degree of dignity and respect that we believed we deserved. I organized various student groups advocating the name change. I supported the move in appearances throughout the institution and actually met with various state legislators to champion our cause. In the end, the name change was approved. On the day the bill was passed in the Mississippi legislature, I led a small group that poured gasoline on the large MSC le tters carved in the hedge outside the Administration Building and set the hedge on fire.
I can remember the cheers of the large crowd assembled, as well as the screaming sirens of fire trucks in the background.
The phone in my office rang early that afternoon. It was Dr.
William David McCain, the no-nonsense university president,
demanding that I immediately report to his office. After a rather stiff reprimand and a lecture about safety and human lives, he finally said to me, “Keith, if this was anyone other than you, I would have you expelled. But with you, I’m going to resist that impulse. Now go on back and do some more of those other things—better things—that you do so well. ” I have that speech stamped indelibly, verbatim, in my head to this day.
• A later experience with Dr. McCain is even more vivid in my mind. It was in the fall of 1962, when James Meredith made his successful bid for admission to the University of Mississippi.
Shortly after Governor Ross Barne tt reluctantly consented to
Meredith’s admission, I received a call from Dr. McCain requesting that I accompany him to a meeting in Jackson, the state capitol, with the State Board of Institutions of Higher Learning to discuss racial matters. I of course agreed. He drove his car and I rode along on the ninety-mile journey to Jackson. We met and briefly engaged in small talk with some board members, whereupon Dr. McCain directed me to a bench in the massive hallways of the Capitol building. I sat there alone for a while. Sometime later, he appeared and said, “Keith, the governor wants to see us. ”
I remember walking into this seemingly enormous office with a large oak desk sitting squarely in the middle of the room. Governor
Barnett invited us to take a seat and, without any foreplay, looked me squarely in the eye with his penetrating eyes, overshadowed by thick bushy eyebrows, and said, “Son, what do you think we ought to do about all these niggers?” I was absolutely stunned and was speechless for a few moments. Quickly regaining my equilibrium and boldness, however, I said, “Sir, I personally think it’s wrong for us to discriminate against anyone. If the students are quali fied,
then I believe they should be a fforded the opportunity of higher education, regardless of their status in society, and that should be afforded to them wherever the state o ffers educational services. ”
He didn’t respond directly. He lowered his head briefly, indulged in some small talk and then excused us. I doubt that I had any impact whatsoever on his thinking, but I do know I caught his a ttention.
That was evident. It is one of the proudest moments of my life. I
walked out of that Capitol building on a cloud.
On the way home, Dr. McCain drove silently for a while and then he quietly said, “Keith, I was proud of you today. ”
There were many other exciting and memorable occasions —
walking onto the football field and crowning the homecoming queen, dressing up in a tuxedo for formal appearances, speaking to nine hundred incoming freshmen at their orientation assembly,
introducing Bob Hope to a packed gymnasium before a performance,
attending conferences and conventions on behalf of the university,
and on and on. But there were some stressful times as well. And there were times where my fundamental values were tested.
I was constantly bombarded with requests for intercession on behalf of students with issues. I must admit that I fixed a few parking tickets and leaned on the Student Court (which I appointed) for leniency in a few cases involving regulatory infractions. I didn’t like it, but I did it. And I invariably regre tted it soon afterward. My actions weigh on my conscience to this day.
My worst memory is an abject interference on my part with the freedom of the press. Before long in office, I found myself becoming extremely sensitive to any publicity that criticized the Student
Government Association or failed to promote SGA activities. I often disliked the way published accounts were wri tten and found myself arguing with the Student Printz editorial staff frequently. Somewhere along the way, I learned through an informant at the Registrar’s office that the Printz editor had failed to maintain the 2.5 academic grade average required for his position. This was an obscure fact, unknown to anyone in the journalism department or anyone else with influence over him. I confronted him with my knowledge of the situation and overtly threatened to expose him. The editorship was a prestigious and well-paid job, essentially providing a full scholarship to whoever held it. At his suggestion, we covertly entered into a Devil’s bargain wherein I was granted editorial veto over all newspaper coverage of the student government in exchange for keeping his academic grade average from becoming public knowledge. From that day forward, I
spent the late hours of every Thursday evening reviewing the galley proofs of the next day’s newspaper and sometimes ordering changes in the text, headlines, photographs or the placement of articles. I
wasn’t proud of these actions at the time, but I continued to do it nevertheless. And I am still ashamed of this, too.
It was through these lapses that I began to take a serious look at the political world around me. I mused about the negative influence of decisions that seemed justi fied at the time on my fundamental value system. Despite all the public recognition and genuine sense of accomplishment, I felt compromised. Whenever I thought about my position, I thought about my lapses in fundamental ethics because of it. Then and there I resolved to never again seek a political office.
I saw firsthand what it does to well-intentioned people. I wanted no part of it. I entered a more honorable profession and to this day I
have honored that abstinence pledge. No politics for me.
The experiences of my senior year in college are among the most important ones of my life. I find it difficult to describe the impact the student government presidency had on me as a person. I matured many years in a mere twelve months. Somehow, I managed to keep my eyes focused on my constituency, the average student. I had a deeply ingrained sense of gratitude for being elected to this office and I desperately wanted to be faithful to their trust. This unique opportunity was the equivalent of Public Service 101 and I was committed to graduating from that course with the same honors that I had accomplished academically. I believe I did that. I walked away from the experience with a renewed con fidence in my own abilities and a deeper appreciation for hard work and allegiance to the right things.

## Principles

- Diligent preparation builds credibility and influence.
- Implement transparent processes to address institutional unfairness.
- Stand against discrimination publicly, even when confronted by powerful figures.
- Power can lead to ethical compromise; self-awareness and corrective action matter.
- Accept reprimand and learn when mistakes endanger others or break rules.

## Decision heuristics

- When institutional procedures appear unfair, implement controls and transparent processes rather than relying on tradition.
- When facing discriminatory policies or rhetoric, voice principled opposition directly and clearly.
- Avoid secrecy and coercion to manage criticism; preserve press freedom instead of imposing censorship.
- If political office leads to recurring ethical compromises, consider exiting to protect core values.
