## Full Text

By the Light of the Silvery Moon
In the 1940s and 1950s on South Madison Street in Calhoun
City, Mississippi, there resided two kindred spirits. At the time,
neither knew anything about kindred souls, much less suspected that one might be so close by. Keith Cobb and Dot Hill lived less than two blocks apart on the same street and were only a year apart in age. Y et, in so many respects, they occupied different worlds.
My life revolved around school organizations, academics,
the school band, working at various jobs and generally hanging out with friends. Dot’s life was centered on family, music, church,
cheerleading and generally enjoying the fun of an active social life. I
was the more serious one, sharply focused on personal achievement in whatever I did and looking toward a venturesome future. Dot was the consummate free spirit, life of the party, focused on enjoying every step she took and living in the moment.
We knew each other, of course, and often found ourselves in the same venues, usually through the church or school affairs. But in high school neither ever gave the other a serious glance. We were friends for sure, but never seemed attracted to one another. She thought I was a nerd and I thought she was a little zany—both gross exaggerations,
but somewhat descriptive in a vague sense. Occasionally, when one of us needed a date for an event, we would call upon the other for an escort. T otally innocent—merely a neighborhood convenience.
Since we both were interested in music, we were sometimes invited to perform together at functions. The earliest recording of one of these events is a prom program, in which we are listed as singing “By the Light of the Silvery Moon” together. We have a copy of that program in our memorabilia, but neither of us can remember anything about the event or even know the date. My best guess is that we were fourteen or fifteen years old.
We both emerged as leaders in our high school, but in di fferent spheres of school activities. I was the editor of the school newspaper,
for instance. She was the head of the school cheerleaders. I was a year older and a grade ahead, so we never had any classes together.
Only occasionally did we find ourselves in the same activities, such as the glee club or a school play.
Upon graduation, we chugged o ff to di fferent colleges. I exiled myself to the University of Southern Mississippi, where I majored in accounting. Dot enrolled at Mississippi College, where she majored in music.
Then in the summer of 1961, we began the process of binding our pathways together. Here’s how it happened.
I was home from college for the summer and working with
Harry Robinson in the M.M. Winkler & Associates accounting firm. Outside of work, there was little activity in our small town,
and I often wandered the streets in the evenings. Dot was home that summer, along with two of her sisters, and their house, only a couple of blocks from mine, became an entertaining place to hang out in the late afternoons and evenings. They had a small Hammond organ in
First record of Keith and Dot together their home and I enjoyed trying to learn to play it. But I’m not really sure that the organ was anything more than a convenient excuse to enjoy the company of all these girls! In any event, I began spending more and more time there. Before long, Dot and I began to strike out and look for other things to do outside her house. There were few things in town to keep us occupied individually and gradually we began to lean on each other for companionship and entertainment.
Somewhere along the way over this summer, we began to realize a few things that we had in common —commonalities that had escaped us over the years. For example, we both loved music and we both enjoyed a wide spectrum of friends. More importantly,
we discovered a mutual value system, which is so o ften missing in relationships. We both valued honesty, integrity and a work ethic.
We were socially conscious and put family and friendships on a high pedestal. We discovered that we respected these traits in each other. From that point of discovery, we began to develop a mutual dependency, even in the short span of time in a summer. I found her bright personality magnetic, captivating, and at the same time, I was beguiled by her solid values and crystalline honesty. She was totally real—to the bone. And I loved that immensely. I think she found my integrity, solid grounding and serious commitment a ttractive. I
think maybe she began to say to herself, “We would be good for each other. ” I certainly came to believe that she would be good for me!
She made me feel happy! So, looking back, I believe our relationship was planted on a bedrock of mutual dependency.
I am not sure of the exact time when our relationship began its gradual re-characterization as “love” , but it wasn’t long after the summer ended and we had headed back to our respective colleges. We had busy schedules of studies and activities, and extensive networks of friends at college. However, in the midst of all this, we missed each other’s company. Clearly, it wasn’t because we were lonely. We began communicating, by le tters mostly. Long-distance phone calls were expensive at the time, but we managed an occasional call. Gasoline was also expensive, but I scrounged and made the hundred-mile trips to Clinton from time to time. By the end of that school year, we were both clearly hooked.
In the summer of 1962, I was o ffered an attractive summer job on the internal audit sta ff of Humble Oil and Re fining Co. in New
Orleans. The opportunity was a major steppingstone in launching my career, so I accepted it and moved to New Orleans for the summer, while Dot attended summer school at Mississippi State
University. We were forced to be apart for most of the summer, but were in constant communication. Dot came to New Orleans one weekend to see me. Looking back, I think that visit might have been the lynchpin that locked us together. A few weeks later, both of us went home to Calhoun City for a short time before returning to college. I have a vivid recollection of a serious conversation, si tting in my car in Dot’s driveway and discussing all the rami fications we could think of related to a life together. I have no recollection if that conversation took place By the Light of the Silvery Moon. But it was then and there that I came to realize I had found a soul mate and was prepared to spend the rest of my life with her. I recall a deep philosophical conversation I once was having with my good friend
Charlie Brumfield, and he posed the question, “What in this world can we be really sure of?” My immediate answer was, “I’m sure about
Dot. There’s not a doubt in my mind. ”
I saved most of the money earned that summer and began shopping for an engagement ring. On September 15, 1962, we made it official. I formally asked for her hand in marriage and gave her the ring. Thankfully, she said “yes” and accepted it. And thus the long,
lifetime journey began.
Our wedding was a marvelous occasion. We were married in the
First Baptist Church, surrounded by a sanctuary full of friends and family. The photographs of this event are among my most cherished memorabilia.
I love to think back about the life we have built together,
remembering the ups and downs we’ve weathered and feeling proud of the family we’ve created. The mutual dependency and shared values formula has been one that has worked well for us. We have been strong supporters for each other and we have almost never disagreed on the fundamental values that guide our lives.
Every day, I feel so fortunate to have spent my life with Dot.
She is my best and most trusted friend and has filled my days with happiness. She has the most consistent beautiful spirit of anyone
I have ever met. I simply adore this woman. The things that mean the most in my life are all the things I have shared with her —our home, our family and our love. I am so grateful for our more than fifty wonderful years together.
The Big Day—June 15, 1963
The most wonderful wife!
