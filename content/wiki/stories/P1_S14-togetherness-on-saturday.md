# Togetherness on Saturday

## Full Text

T ogetherness on Saturday
Freshmen at the University of Southern Mississippi in 1959
were assigned English classes by lottery. Each class comprised about thirty students. There was no option to transfer out of one’s assigned class; you either hung in or dropped the course. The classes assigned to me in the first two terms had excellent professors; however, my luck changed dramatically in the third quarter. I drew Dr. Wilbur
Stout, who had a long-standing reputation for being the toughest professor on campus. He had not awarded an A in five years. The failure rate of students in his class was in the stratosphere. Moreover,
he was a really weird duck. He had a disheveled appearance, with uncombed silver hair and a sloppy seersucker suit that hung on him like a limp sack. He smoked a long crooked briar pipe and shu ffled when he walked. His eyes wandered blandly around a room and he rarely spoke a word.
So, there I was. Stuck. I could take my chances with this mysterious man or, alternatively, I could drop a course I had already paid for and gamble on a better professor the next year. (The English composition course was required.) On the second day of class,
only twelve people showed up. Eighteen students had dropped the course—a predictable reaction based on Dr. Stout’s reputation. For some reason I can’t explain to this day, I bowed my back and refused to be intimidated by this character. I took a risk and remained in the class.
Here’s how the class was conducted. There were no lectures and no instructions—just a grinding daily drill of writing essays. On the first day, Dr. Stout walked into the classroom promptly on the hour,
wrote a topic on the blackboard and le ft the room. At the end of the fifty-minute period, he returned and collected the papers. The next day he strolled in and handed out the graded papers from the previous day. He then wrote a new topic on the board and left. And so it went. He never said a word; he just ambled in and out of the classroom at the beginning and end of the hour every day for three months. The graded essays were littered with arcane remarks, difficult to interpret or understand. Missing from this bizarre scenario was a forum to have his cryptic commentary explained.
The topics assigned were all offbeat and esoteric. Some subjects I
remember included: “Dividing by Two” , “Wondering or Wandering”
and “I Heard Their Footsteps” . Weird stuff!
On the first essay returned to me, Dr. Stout had bled all over the paper with red ink, including a big red F on the front page accompanied by indecipherable scribbling. I was crestfallen. It was the first F I had ever received on anything in my life! I combed the paper, trying to figure out where I screwed up. That continued for about a week, when at last a D appeared. And then a week or so later a C–. I spent hours poring over these papers trying to figure out a pattern to the grades —i.e., what was the key to this crazy puzzle?
I would place them all out on a table in the library, analyzing them in every minute detail. Finally, I zoned in on a few theories. Volume was obviously important—how much could I speed-write in forty-five minutes? And it was clear that grammar, punctuation, spelling,
etc. were unimportant to this professor. Also, obliqueness seemed to trump clarity every time. It gradually became clear that the more abstract my writing —i.e., not taking the topics so literally —the better the grade. So I began to seriously experiment with these theories and before long I was getting occasional As and Bs on a few of the daily papers.
My day of triumph came near the end of the term when Dr. Stout assigned the topic “T ogetherness on Saturday” . All twelve students stared blankly at the blackboard and said, “Huh?” Suddenly, I came up with an idea. I had worked the past summer at Lunceford’s Bargain
Store in Calhoun City. Next door to it, there was a makeshift café
that catered to African-American farm people. The café specialized in chitlins and hog jowls. On Saturday nights, dozens of customers would come in from the co tton fields and congregate at the café.
Due to the proximity of the café to my work, I came to know them fairly well. Their happy, fun-loving spirit, particularly in light of their destitute plight in life, intrigued me. So, in a sudden flash of creativity, I started to write about the uniqueness of these dirt-poor,
uneducated laborers. I described their “T ogetherness on Saturday”
and how their weekly camaraderie gave them a sense of respectable identity and perhaps contributed to their happiness.
The next day my paper was returned with a big A+ in large letters on the front and this remark scribbled in red at the end: “Mr.
Cobb, this is the best paper I have read in many, many years. ” By the end of the term, I had wri tten forty-four essays on the wildest array of subjects one can imagine. On final exam day, there was no exam and no writing. Dr. Stout simply shu ffled into the room and started handing out scraps of paper with the term grade. My scrap said A. I looked up at him and said, “ Thank you. ” He nodded and miraculously responded with, “Well deserved” Until then, I wasn’t sure he could speak!
I learned some valuable life lessons from this stressful experience: first, the class was a laboratory for problem solving. I
learned to critically analyze, parse out patterns and develop and test solutions. Second, the class sharpened my ability to think on my feet and trust my intuition. Third, it helped me see that sometimes it’s better not to take everything so literally. And, finally, it made me realize that togetherness on Saturday (or any day) with family and friends is a pretty good idea for life as well as for an essay topic.
