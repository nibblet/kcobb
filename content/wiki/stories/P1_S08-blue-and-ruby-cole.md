## Full Text

Blue and Ruby Cole
Sandy and I were blessed in our childhood years to have some wonderful next-door neighbors. Blue and Ruby Cole lived in a small frame house next to ours on South Madison Street. They worked hard and steadily for most of their entire lives, well into their seventies. They labored together on the factory floor at Harrelson
Manufacturing Company during the World War II years. Later, Blue operated the local Standard Oil gasoline depot and Ruby did sewing and odd jobs at Ruth’s Draperies.
These people were the salt of the earth, at least from my vantage point. Oddly, they regularly attended different churches. Blue was a
Methodist and sang in the choir at Lewis Memorial Methodist. Ruby attended First Baptist, across the street, where she was responsible for the flower arrangements every Sunday for as far back as I can remember.
Blue’s real name was Dewi tt T . Cole. I never actually knew where he acquired his nickname or what it signified, but “Blue” was what everyone called him. Blue was a Freemason and Ruby was a member of Eastern Star. These were always mysterious organizations that aroused my curiosity, with all sorts of secrets and such. I never could figure out what the Masons were all about. But I always heard that Blue was high up in the organization and considered a unique authority on the organization in our little part of the world. He was said to be a thirty-second or thirty-third degree Mason, whatever that meant. Based on the hints they dropped occasionally, I had the notion that both organizations had something to do with good works and the promise of rewards in the afterlife based on those good works. But that may have just been my imagination. I simply know that the organizations werevery important in their lives.
Blue’s health deteriorated as he aged, but he never lost his good humor or the twinkle in his eye. He died in 1995 at the age of ninety,
leaving Ruby to live alone for the next thirteen years.
Blue and Ruby never had children, though they always seemed to have a ffection for kids. They practically adopted Sandy and me,
and appointed themselves as virtual surrogate parents. They gave us both nicknames —I was “Bud” and Sandy was “Dune” —when we were small children and continuing practically all our lives. I
do not know how they arrived at these nicknames, but that’s what they always called us. They both were extremely generous with their time, playing with us and teaching us things as we hung out at their house. They would occasionally catch themselves interfering with our upbringing (or meddling in our family a ffairs, as Mother would characterize it). They were o ften openly critical of the strict discipline that we lived under and didn’t hesitate to choose sides when we were being punished.
I remember once when I was playing baseball in the front yard with Blue and accidentally swung the bat the wrong way, hi tting
Ruby in the head and in flicting a big gash. Blue rushed her to the doctor, who repaired the injury with several stitches. When they returned from the doctor with Ruby’s head all bandaged up, Mother happened to be outside and gasped, “Gracious alive, Ruby. What on
Blue Cole, self-appointed surrogate father
Ruby Cole, the consummate next-door neighbor earth happened to you?” Fearing I would undoubtedly be punished,
Ruby rapidly cooked up a tall tale of how she had stumbled going down the back door stairs and hit her head on a concrete block. I just stood there in amazement as she lied through her teeth, while Blue just winked at me and smiled.
Mother always resented their doting attention. One time when I
was very small, I had begged her for a wagon, a Radio Flyer that I had seen in a catalog. She had refused, saying that it was too expensive and I didn’t need it anyway. Then Blue came home one day, in his dark green Standard Oil truck, with a bright red Radio Flyer strapped on the side. He dismantled it, tied a rope to the wagon tongue and began driving up and down the gravel street pulling me in the wagon. Mother witnessed what was going on from her kitchen window and came outside screaming at Blue, using language that was a bit strange for me. Blue just shrugged it all off and said, “Well,
he wanted it, and I wanted him to have it. That’s just all there is to it,
Frances. Y ou should just cool down!”
Our family did not own a car until 1951. Before then, Sandy and
I walked the half-mile to school every day. Whenever the weather was particularly cold or inclement, Ruby would crank up her old black
Plymouth early in the morning and then appear on our doorstep volunteering to take us to school. Mother would reluctantly agree,
mumbling in the background about how she resented having to rely on others to look after the welfare of her children.
I remember so many small things about these wonderful people.
I still remember Ruby’s hugs. Her skin was the so ftest of anyone I
have ever known. I remember her cats, and how she doted on them.
I remember Blue ge tting spruced up for choir practice during the week; he would shave and put on a fresh clean shirt, but he smelled like an odd combination of gasoline, oil, aftershave lotion and toothpaste. I remember him si tting on the floor with me lining up dominos in an S shape and laughing as I toppled them over. I
remember them both laughing a lot and having fun with us, always a refreshing interlude.
During my teenage years, Blue and Ruby moved to another part of town. Although we didn’t see them every day as in the past,
we visited them regularly. Over the years, whenever I was back in
Calhoun City visiting, I always tried to make a point of stopping by to visit with them. On one of my last visits, Ruby was almost one hundred years old, living in a nursing home, almost deaf and practically blind. She was si tting in a recliner in her room, sound asleep. I pinched her on the toe and said, “Ruby… ” . She immediately jumped and said, “Is that you, Keith?” Deaf as she was, she had recognized my voice from a dead sleep. I stood there shedding a tear or two, recognizing that this was a crystalline re flection of Ruby’s love and affection for me.
However, it was Sandy who was the most devoted to her, and that devotion was reciprocated. During Ruby’s waning years, Sandy and Daddy looked after her every need. They visited her in the nursing home regularly. Daddy disposed of all her household goods and sold the house for her when she became too feeble to live there alone. Sandy managed all her personal affairs and kept her abreast of the local news.
When Daddy was disposing of Ruby’s household items, I
happened to be in town and went by to look over some of the things remaining in the house. I decided to take a small end table that still contained the set of dominos that Blue and I had played with when
I was a child. The next week, I visited Ruby at the nursing home and mentioned to her, “Ruby, I want you to know that I took a piece of your furniture down to my house. ” Without hesitating, she looked at me and said, “Oh, I’ll bet you took the domino end table. ” I have that little table, with the original dominos still inside, sitting in the living room of the Mallory House today.
Ruby died peacefully in 2008 at age one hundred. She had no close relatives. When her will was read, it came to light that she had left her entire estate to be divided equally between Sandy and
Daddy. Unbelievably, as her safety deposit boxes were emptied and her certificates of deposits collected and tabulated, she had a sizable estate. The big question in our minds at the time was (and still is),
where on earth did Ruby Cole get that kind of money?
She had lived practically as a pauper for as long as we could remember, a hardscrabble life pinching pennies and scrounging every day of her life. Sure, she was thri fty and probably saved every nickel she could, but one would have to save a huge slice of personal income for a lifetime to accumulate that much money.
The origin of the money is still a mystery. But the money was real and there to count. We had a theory or two, but those ideas were more speculations than anything else. When Daddy passed away a few years later, we transferred his half of Ruby’s estate over to Sandy.
Ruby’s bequest was a marvelous reward for Sandy’s dedication to her over so many years.
These are all terri fic memories. I am grateful for having known this wonderful couple for so many years and for the legacies they left for Sandy and me, both material and emotional gifts.
