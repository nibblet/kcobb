Houses, Cars and Boats
Many families de fine their identify by their assets —their houses, cars and toys. Materialism is one of the basic and powerful facts of human behavior. I suppose it has been somewhat of a factor for Dot and me as we have stumbled through the years. Therefore, I
likewise suppose a memoir would be incomplete without describing some of these important faceplates in our lives.
During our first couple of years of marriage, Dot and I rented apartments. The first was a tiny one-bedroom co ttage on Green
Street in Jackson, Mississippi, tucked behind the large Calvary
Baptist Church. The apartment was modestly furnished, but clean and neat. The rent was $75 a month. The location was convenient for us because the church had provided Dot a full scholarship at
Mississippi College in exchange for her service as a soloist on Sunday mornings. We later leased a more modern one-bedroom furnished unit in a complex on Je fferson Street. This complex was brand new,
and we were excited about our upscale surroundings. However, we were anxious and eager to own a home of our own, so much so that we rushed into it faster than we should have.
We pinched pennies until we accumulated about $1,200 in our savings account. Then in 1965, we found a three-bedroom home at 1249 Springdale Drive and made a deal for $19,000. The downpayment was $900, and we executed an FHA mortgage for
$18,100. Payments were $116 per month, including taxes and interest. It was a nice home in excellent condition, situated in a pleasant, tree-lined neighborhood. It was a typical tract house, with the only oddity being the unusual pink kitchen appliances. We took great pride in this home over the next six years.
We had no furniture for this new castle except for a TV and a bookcase. The $300 left over in savings after the downpayment was not going to reach far, we realized as soon as we began to set up housekeeping. Acute stress set in as I came to realize all the things we thought we needed—a washer and dryer, bedroom furniture, a sofa,
lawn mower, etc. For example, I remember coming to the realization that we needed a mailbox; then, after going to the hardware store and purchasing one, I realized I needed a shovel and cement to install it. And so it went, as we sank into debt and wound up with a stack of installment payment coupons. Only a year earlier, I had passed the CPA examination and was ostensibly a financial expert.
Now this self-acclaimed expert was finding himself up to his ears in debt and stretched to the limit financially. Ultimately, we hunkered down and waded our way through this crisis. But I vowed then and there to never again find myself in such a position.
We sold the Springdale Drive house for a modest profit when we moved to Orlando in 1971. There we settled on a nice brick home at
138 Variety T ree Circle in Altamonte Springs. This was a larger four-bedroom home and was listed for more than double the selling price of our Jackson home. More cautious this time, I remember calling
Norm White, the regional Peat Marwick partner in Atlanta, and asking what he thought about me spending that much money for a house. He actually laughed at me and said, “Go for it. Y ou can afford it. ” It was a nice, trouble-free house in a beautiful neighborhood with terrific neighbors. The property’s twelve Valencia orange trees and two tangerine trees produced tons of fruit annually. It was an idyllic place for our family at the time. We called it home for four years.
Leaving Orlando in 1975, we purchased a home at 3060 NE 47
Street in Fort Lauderdale. We once again upped the ante and paid more than double the selling price of our Orlando home. The Fort
Lauderdale home was a beautiful, four-bedroom house with a large
Florida room in back overlooking a swimming pool and a canal running off the Intracoastal Waterway. It was conveniently located in the prestigious Coral Ridge area inside the city. We loved this home and thoroughly enjoyed living there for fourteen years. Our three boys spent their formative years there and consider it their original homestead. It was a wise investment, too; when we left Fort
Lauderdale, we sold it for exactly three times what we had paid for it.
In 1989, we landed in Baltimore, Maryland. Before we le ft
Florida, I made some inquiries of the KPMG partners there, asking about what section of the city we should consider. Their answers were all over the page, mostly focused on tract developments in outlying suburbs. Not being satis fied with this guidance, I went to the Broward County library and did some research. I managed to find a listing of the fifty top executives in Baltimore. Still at the library, I located a Baltimore phone book. I then plo tted the home addresses of these executives on a city map. Voilà! I had my answer.
So we concentrated our search in the Ruxton/Malvern area, not far from the center of the city. We located a beautiful, two-story
Georgian home atop a steep hill, at 1428 East Joppa Road. The house was under construction and about 95 percent complete —the only brand new home we have ever owned. We had the chance to tailor most of the final details to our tastes, and the result was arguably the all-around best home we ever owned. It was meticulously built by a perfectionist contractor and was finely detailed in every respect. It had a huge 1,500-square-foot walk-in finished basement that we fitted out nicely with an oak bar, pool table and recreational furnishings. Another unique feature was a music room near the front entrance, where we placed our grand piano and some of our musical collections. When Dot finished decorating the home, it was a showplace. We used it liberally for entertainment. The only disappointment was that we had the opportunity to live there for only two years. Then, on to Philadelphia in 1991.
The Philadelphia Main Line has a well-deserved reputation as one of the most beautiful suburban communities in America.
Winding, tree-lined roads meander through hills and valleys, with large lots and a wide variety of beautiful brick and stone homes set back among lush vegetation. Dot and I scoured this landscape,
looking at fifty or more houses before settling on a beautiful property at 840 Mt. Moro Road in Villanova. This was a recently renovated and expanded house with a French motif, nestled on a well-landscaped one-acre lot. The renovation developer was living in the house at the time. It was a large home, with a combination of historic-type styling and modern appointments. It had terrific curb appeal. Interestingly,
practically all our furnishings fit the house to a tee. We had to acquire practically nothing to furnish the place. Again, we used the home liberally for entertainment. This was our home for four years.
In 1995, we returned to Fort Lauderdale. We had loved the
Coral Ridge area, where we had lived before, and were predisposed to return to that neighborhood. However, as we began our house search, we gravitated to the south part of the city, where some of our prior friends were now living. It was also a bit more convenient to downtown, the airport and major roadways. Following a thorough search, we landed at 2521 Del Lago Drive. The home was a
5,600-square-foot single-floor structure situated on a canal just off
Philadelphia home for four years
Home base in Fort Lauderdale, Florida the Intracoastal Waterway near the port. Reportedly, the home had once been lavishly decorated in Laura Ashley style, but after a bank repossession it had been neutralized in order to facilitate a sale. Therefore, it was plain vanilla and required some extensive remodeling. We hired an interior decorator, and she and Dot did a beautiful job of converting this terri fic house into a showplace. We have lived in this home now for twenty years. We have continued to update and maintain the property over the years, but the essential design, look and feel is much the same as it was following our initial occupancy.
And then there is the Mallory House, our respite in Calhoun City that we have enjoyed for the past many years. We purchased the home in 1998 from our friend Fred Nabors, a local developer who had meticulously restored the property. Our beloved high school English teacher, Miss Frances Mallory, had lived in the home practically her
The Mallory House, Calhoun City entire life, so we named it after her. Built in 1905, it is the oldest house in Calhoun City and exudes the harmony of the history of the community. The Mallory House has been our home away from home, where we visit about four times each year. It is fully decorated and stocked, and is just as comfortable as our home in Florida. James
Pittman has been our caretaker since we have owned it, and takes great care to keep it in top flight condition. He takes enormous pride in the place and treats it as his personal asset. We will be eternally gratefully to James for his loyalty and his excellent service at the
Mallory House.

James Pittman, caretaker

Shifting from houses to automobiles, Dot and I started our marriage with two cars—a 1957 Ford Tudor Sedan and a 1961 Ford
Falcon carried over from our college days. Neither was in great shape,
but the 1957 Ford was particularly dilapidated. After struggling with it for a couple of years, I eventually parked it in our driveway and replaced it. Dot, ge tting tired of seeing in there, sold it to a scrap dealer for $50. When we later had serious engine problems with the
Falcon, we traded it in for a brand new maroon 1965 Ford Fairlane.
We bought the new car from Dot’s brother Charles Hill, who was working as a salesman at Hawkins Ford dealership in Calhoun City.
It was a beautiful car and we were justifiably proud of it.
In those days, cars were not nearly as well built as they are today,
and their designs changed o ften. Therefore, we found ourselves trading one of our cars every couple of years. Over the next few years,
we owned a yellow Pontiac, a two-tone brown Oldsmobile and a white Buick Riviera. In about 1972, I surprised Dot at Christmas by driving home a gorgeous white and maroon Cadillac and parking it in the driveway. It was our first of several Cadillacs, and we were both excited. Later on, we shi fted our tastes to foreign models. I
purchased a snazzy BMW 633 sports car and got Dot a beautiful dark green Jaguar sedan. We then shifted to Lexus and owned both sedan and sports models of that luxury brand.
When we returned to Fort Lauderdale with Alamo, we dumped our cars and began driving company-furnished Cadillacs for the next two years. Because of our General Motors connections, we traded these cars for new ones about every six months.
Upon the sale of Alamo, Mike Egan arranged a dinner and awarded me several gi fts in recognition of my leadership of the company. One of these gi fts was an option to pick out any car
I wanted and send the bill to him. So, I chose a sleek BMW 840
CI. This was one of the coolest cars ever built. It retailed for about
$85,000 in 1997. BMW only sold about seven thousand of this model over a five-year period. People gawked at it everywhere I
went. I loved the look and feel of this car and kept it for about ten years, before I eventually sold it on eBay to a retired executive in
South Carolina for $24,000.
For our family car during this time, we had Mercedes, BMW
and Lexus sedans at various times. Our latest Lexus was a hybrid,
a new and di fferent twist for us. For a while, we owned three cars.
Somehow, we reasoned that we needed a convertible (since we lived in South Florida), so we started this craze with a BMW Z3, a cute sports car that we used for tooling around town. We kept it for ten years as well, and also sold it on eBay. Continuing the convertible interests, we replaced the Z3 with a Lexus 450 hardtop convertible and then replaced that with an Infiniti G37 hardtop convertible.
While at Alamo, I became interested in electric automobiles and placed an order in 1996 for ten General Motors experimental electric cars, to test them in the rental market place. Following hybrid and electric car developments over the next several years, it seemed to me that T esla was cracking the code. So, in 2014 I took the plunge and purchased a new T esla Model S sedan. It is clearly the most remarkable vehicle I have even seen, much less driven. It is as close to perfect as one can imagine for an automobile. It is fast, quiet,
technologically advanced and simply fun to drive and own. I have been significantly impressed and have enjoyed owning it.
Looking back, we have owned over thirty different automobiles over the past fifty-plus years. I have enjoyed each and every one of them (except for the starter Fords) and never was so unfortunate to draw a lemon.
We have owned not as many boats. But the ones we did own were a source of great pleasure and fun. We acquired our first boat in the early 1980s, a thirty-two-foot T rojan sedan that I bought from the estate of a deceased partner in KPMG’s Jacksonville office. I
knew little about boating at the time and hired a captain to assist me in bringing it three hundred miles down the Intracoastal Waterway.
I supplemented this experience with a course sponsored by the
U.S. Power Squadron, and before long became adept at navigation,
operation and maintenance of our boat. We renamed it “Happiness” ,
thinking the name might prove providential for our family, and we thoroughly enjoyed our many marine outings together. A fter a few years of wear and tear, I decided to sell it and look for a better one.
As it turned out, another Jacksonville partner had a thirty-six-foot Chris Cra ft sports fisherman that he was interested in selling.
He had acquired it following a heart a ttack and had spent three or four years tinkering and improving the vessel as therapy for his health condition. It was in pristine condition—better than new—so
I jumped on the opportunity and made another three-hundred-mile journey down the Intracoastal. We renamed this boat “Happiness”
also. It was a significant improvement over the T rojan, and our family enjoyed it even more. We took several extended trips on it —to the
Bahamas, through the cross-Florida waterway, and down into the
Keys on several occasions. I also used it o ften for deep-sea fishing.
Paul and John were teenagers at the time and helped me maintain
“Happiness” and keep it in top condition. Eventually, as the boys got older and their interests changed, our usage of the boat diminished.
So, we sold it shortly before we moved to Baltimore. I remember the sad day when we all stood on the dock and watched the new owner head away down the canal.
Our family thoroughly enjoyed these boats. They were a catalyst for family unity during some important years for our children. And we had some unique experiences as we vacationed and travelled around Florida and the Bahamas. Once, on our way to an outing in the Keys with my good friend Allan Arch and his family, we came upon a dead man floating in the Intracoastal Waterway near the
Sunrise bridge in Fort Lauderdale. Allan and I reported the sighting to the Coast Guard and waited for help to arrive. Unfortunately, we then spent most of the rest of that day in interviews with the local police. We never learned the man’s identity or the reason for his death. On another occasion, our family was in the Bahamas when we encountered two fishermen stranded in open waters after their boat had sunk. One was floating out of sight of land, clinging to a red gasoline can. The other was spo tted about a half-mile away,
swimming in the wrong direction. We literally saved these guys’
lives.
Along the way, we owned three Boston Whalers. Back in the 1980s, we had a fifteen-foot Super Sport that we kept on a li ft behind our house. This boat was used almost exclusively as local transportation around Fort Lauderdale’s miles of waterways. I can’t begin to estimate how many hours John scooted around town on it with his friends and enjoyed water sports with it. By the time we sold it, it was almost worn out! After returning to Fort Lauderdale in
1995, I felt like we had to have a boat, so I went back to Lauderdale
Marina and purchased a twenty-four-foot Boston Whaler Outrage.
It was a great boat. As time went by, however, it became a little dated,
so I traded it for a somewhat similar twenty-three-foot Boston
Whaler Dauntless. This essentially became a local water taxi for us,
and we used it primarily to drive around town sightseeing and going out to dinner at waterside restaurants. As our usage diminished, I
finally decided to bite the bullet and become “boatless” for the first time in many years.

## Principles

- Learn from financial mistakes and take corrective action.
- Do your own research when external advice is vague or inconsistent.
- Use major assets (homes, boats) to create family experiences and social value.
- Seek trusted counsel before making large expenditures.
- Mitigate operational risk by hiring expertise and acquiring skills when entering a new domain.

## Decision heuristics

- If expert local guidance is inconsistent, collect primary data (phone books, maps) and plot target peers to identify desirable neighborhoods.
- When lacking operational experience, hire a knowledgeable professional and undertake formal training before full responsibility.
- Before making large discretionary purchases, consult a trusted senior colleague for perspective on affordability.
- After experiencing financial strain from overextension, 'hunker down'—prioritize paying obligations and avoid repeating risky purchases until stabilized.
- When evaluating new technology for business or personal use, pilot/test in a controlled way before full-scale adoption.
