KPMG—Philadelphia
We arrived in Philadelphia in August 1991. After an exhausting search for a home, we landed in Villanova in the storied Main Line area of the city. We purchased a beautiful home on Mt. Moro Road that was well-suited for all of our furnishings and belongings; they seemed to fit perfectly. We enrolled Mark at the nearby Shipley
School, Dot got busy with the house and the neighborhood, and I
went to work.
The Philadelphia office suffered from much the same maladies and sluggishness of Baltimore, except on steroids. It seemed as if that was my destiny these days, fixing problems in the firm. In addition to the Baltimore-like insularity, the partners in Philadelphia were a rambunctious and ornery group whose members loudly objected and pushed back on much that was going on within the firm. The managing partner, who had been ousted and forced into retirement just prior to my arrival, had been cut from the same cloth. Prior to his departure, he had fomented their open grumbling and rebellious mood. Some leaders inside the firm referred to him as the Pied Piper of Pennsylvania.
Philadelphia was one of KPMG’s larger offices—about thirty-five partners and over three hundred fifty sta ff members. It had a rich heritage that went back to the earliest days of the firm. Some of the brightest technical professionals in KPMG’s history had passed through there over the years. Like the partners in Baltimore,
the partners here were exceptionally competent technically. They basked in the notion that they were the smartest professionals in the city. At least that’s what they told each other—regularly. Because of their extreme inward focus and the time they devoted to grumbling and moaning, it was clear to me that they had lost most of their objectivity. And although the office served numerous major clients,
I would characterize many, or most of the relationships between clients and our partners as tenuous. I had my work cut out for me. But
I had the unanimous support of the firm’s management committee,
and I had the Baltimore experience in my back pocket.
So I plunged in with all of my energy. My game plan was much the same as in Baltimore—get to know the partners, meet the major clients and then hit the streets. The only difference was that, having learned from the past, I moved more aggressively. I made bold moves, and I made them rapidly.
It didn’t take long to see how difficult it would be to cut through all of the noise and accurately assess the Philadelphia partners’ skills.
Luckily, I stumbled upon the firm of RHR International, a group of corporate psychologists with a fifty-year reputation of counseling
Fortune 500 companies on executive talent. I hired RHR to conduct assessments of the key partners and assist me in coming to grips with the troublesome cultural issues. RHR was nothing short of terri fic.
Their insights were eye-opening. Their work and recommendations led to a sweeping realignment of responsibilities within the practice,
changes that quickly resulted in a ttitudinal adjustments and productivity enhancement. The mood changed almost overnight.
I can’t say enough about the value RHR brought to this KPMG
segment in my early days in Philadelphia.
Getting to know the major Philadelphia clients was a bit like a Baltimore makeover. The partners dragged their feet and procrastinated on making introductions. I soon realized that this sluggishness was due to the fact that they actually didn’t have relationships with the top level of their clients.
One dramatic example deserves mention. Our largest client was Rohm and Haas, a Fortune 500 chemical company. In the first few weeks, I gathered together three Philadelphia office partners to discuss our client service. When I suggested, among other things,
that we make arrangements for me to meet the Rohm and Haas
CEO, Larry Wilson, they said that first I had to meet the controller,
who was their primary contact point. Then, and only then, would it be appropriate for them to introduce me to the chief financial officer.
Protocol, you know. As for the CEO, they demurred, saying he was a chemical engineer and not really interested in “accounting stu ff” .
I challenged them on this point, thinking it unlikely, but they stuck to their guns, saying that Wilson wanted nothing to do with us; the truth of the matter was that they didn’t know him at all. Undaunted, I
tapped my phone and asked my secretary to place a call to his office,
and darned if she didn’t get him on the line. My partners cringed in their chairs, seemingly on the verge of nervous breakdowns one and all. I introduced myself over the phone as the new KPMG managing partner, and he said, “Y eah, I read about that in the newspaper. From
Baltimore, right? Welcome to Philadelphia!” I told him I’d like to meet him, and he immediately replied, “Hell, yes! I’d like to meet you too. ” He asked me a couple of questions about where I was living and then invited me to dinner with him and his wife that weekend.
The first time I met Larry was when he rang the doorbell at our home that Friday evening, followed by a nice dinner together at a Chinese restaurant in Villanova. From that day forward, Larry Wilson was a fine professional friend. He came to the next audit commi ttee meeting and never missed one afterwards.
In addition to making a friend, this experience underscored a valuable lesson: Never sell yourself short and never be bashful about establishing relationships. Also, never allow yourself to be a victim of immature palaver on the part of your own associates.
Not long afterwards, I went out with two partners for a luncheon meeting with another major client, Pete Musser, the chairman and CEO of Safeguard Scienti fic. Pete was a delightful, charming executive with an ingratiating manner. I remember with amusement that he brought his dog, an Irish se tter, to lunch and sat him next to his place at the table. As I was leaving, he said rather matter-of-factly,
“By the way, Keith, I need to get a $10,000 check from you. ” I’m sure
I looked puzzled. He added, “That’s for the United Way. A guy like you can’t be the managing partner of KPMG and not be a member of the Alexis de T ocqueville Society!” Immediately recalling Larry
Horner’s advice those early days in Fort Lauderdale, I sent him a check that afternoon. And thus began my twenty-plus-year journey with the Alexis de T ocqueville Society. A few days later, Pete called me and said he admired my reaction to his admi tted pushiness.
He said he had arranged for me to be appointed to the board of directors of the Alexis de T ocqueville Society. That move launched a series of new relationships for me in the city. I won’t forget that act of kindness. Pete also later arranged for my appointment to the
Philadelphia Boy Scout Council. What a guy! He is another member of my Hall of Fame.
Pete Musser reminds me of another valuable business fundamental: There are all sorts of quid pro quos in life, and they can contribute to success if one plays his cards smartly.
I found the Philadelphia business community to be friendly and welcoming. During my first month there, I received a visit from Ralph
Roberts, the distinguished chairman of Comcast Communications,
along with his son Brian. Comcast was not a client and there was no other official relationship with KPMG. The visit was merely a gesture of good will and welcoming to the city by a genteel gentleman from the old school. I also had the distinct impression that Ralph was conducting a relationship training exercise for Brian, who was about thirty years old at the time. Ralph passed away this year at age ninety-five and Brian of course is now chairman of Comcast, the largest communication company in the world. I have remembered this anecdote with fondness and have a ttempted to emulate Ralph
Roberts’ initiative on numerous occasions since. Shortly after my arrival in Philadelphia, a client executive stopped by my office and put the arm on me for a political contribution for Ed Rendell, a
Democratic candidate for mayor. I politely demurred, saying that I
rarely dabbled in politics and, moreover, had never met Ed Rendell and didn’t even know anything about him. Early the next morning,
my office phone rang. The voice on the other end said, “Keith, this is Ed Rendell. I understand you’re new to town and know nothing about me. I’d like to fix that. How about joining me for breakfast tomorrow morning?” I agreed and met Ed in the restaurant of the
Rittenhouse Hotel, overlooking a beautifully picturesque, snow-blanketed Ri ttenhouse Square. I felt a bit awkward at first, so I
explained my established abstinence from politics and how I was a Republican, anyway. He waved a dismissive hand and said, with his trademark broad, toothy smile, “Not to worry. I have plenty of campaign money anyway. I just wanted to meet you. ” We spent two hours together that morning in a wide-ranging discussion, seemingly hitting it o ff for some odd reason. Perhaps it was because we were both so plainspoken.
From that point forward, Ed always made a distinct point of speaking to me at functions or even walking along the streets.
Shortly after he was elected mayor, he personally called me again and asked me to serve on an elite management and productivity board he established to help curb city expenditures. I agreed, of course, and through this vehicle was able to meet many of the key movers and shakers of the Philadelphia establishment. Ed served two incredibly successful terms as mayor. He was fondly labeled
“ America’s Mayor” by the media. His popularity surged. He later went on to serve two terms as governor of Pennsylvania and then was chairman of the National Democratic Party. T oday, he is a frequent political and sports commentator on television. Every time I see him on TV , my mind races back to that initial meeting and I re flect on what an impressive individual he is. So genuine. Ed’s initiative in this interface taught me some other great lessons. One was to go out of your way to initiate relationships and then to take the time and make the effort to assure they are meaningful. I have used that technique liberally ever since that snowy, blustery morning on Ri ttenhouse
Square. Thank you, Ed Rendell.
The Philadelphia practice had three satellite offices—
Allentown, Harrisburg and Wilmington. They were outposts,
operating much like small-town CPA firms, but with all the liability of a huge international firm. These units consumed an inordinate amount of my time, as I tried to harness them and drag them kicking and screaming into the KPMG mainstream. I also had added Pennsylvania and Delaware to my regional responsibilities and was spending quite a bit of time on trains and planes overseeing those practices. Washington, D.C. was particularly onerous and burdensome, if for no other reason than it was simply Washington,
with all the a ttendant risks and issues. And then there were the monthly two-day meetings of the KPMG Management Committee,
held in venues scattered around the country.
On top of this activity, I was tagged by the management committee to spearhead a project to completely overhaul the partner compensation system for the entire firm. The income distribution system in place for a couple of decades had been loosely administered and reeked of favoritism; it was producing partner compensation that ranged over a wide spectrum of unfairness. It was the single most controversial subject within the KPMG partnership.
And just as controversial, of course, was any change to the system!
I plunged into this task with my handy-dandy Macintosh computer and proceeded to realign the partner salary and profit participation of the 1,500 partners. Following iteration after iteration, and revisions upon revisions, I finally arrived at a process and income allocation that was a dramatic improvement. The management commi ttee unanimously approved my recommendations and implemented the process immediately. This was an unbelievably difficult undertaking,
but I think it was probably the single best thing I ever did for KPMG
over my thirty-two-year career.
Considering all of these responsibilities and the desperate need to right the ship in Philadelphia, I was a very busy guy.
Our social life in Philadelphia was much di fferent than it was in any of the other places we had lived. Establishing meaningful relationships seemed more di fficult for some reason. Perhaps that phenomenon was related to the city simply being much larger than the other cities. Or maybe it was the reputed cliquishness of the Main
Line establishment. Whatever the root cause, we were undaunted.
We loved our picturesque home in Villanova and we used it liberally for entertainment.
In the midst of all the hustle and bustle, travel, long hours at work, parenting and social activity, Dot developed breast cancer.
What a sad day! But she had wonderful doctors and we treated this setback much as we handled everything else—aggressively. She had surgery almost immediately and then a course of radiation that lasted a couple of months. Despite our positive outlook, the psychological impact was as serious as the disease itself, it seemed. Only those who have faced the life threat that cancer portends can full understand the emotional struggle the victim endures. Dot struggled mightily,
but you’d never have recognized that from her upbeat, positive approach to life itself. What a trooper in the face of that adversity!
Then my professional environment changed dramatically once again in 1993. KPMG embarked on a massive reorganization with major changes to the traditional functional and geographic organizational structure of the firm. For the entire existence of the firm, the business had been managed in geographical units—cities—
and further structured along the lines of audit, tax and management consulting. Two years into their administration, Jon Madonna and
Jim Brocksmith pushed through a major restructuring that resulted in enormous cultural changes in the firm, touching every nook and cranny. In one of the biggest surprises of my business career, I was named national managing partner for the new financial services line of business, the largest of the five new national practice units. I was stunned. Where did that come from?
So, I unhitched my Philadelphia managing-partner and area managing-partner harnesses and signed onto two of the most challenging years of my entire career. As I was leaving the Philadelphia managing partner position to take on the national responsibility, the local partners arranged an elaborate dinner to pay tribute to my two years of leadership there. The highlight of the evening, for me, was the local partners’ presentation of the Philadelphia Bowl, a limited-edition china bowl manufactured in 1932 for Bailey Banks & Biddle.
Copies had normally been reserved for high-level dignitaries visiting the city —presidents, supreme court justices and the like. This priceless memento now sits prominently in my office.

## Principles

- Use independent, professional assessment to diagnose talent and culture when internal objectivity is compromised.
- Initiate relationships directly; don't let gatekeeping or protocol prevent access to senior client executives.
- Move decisively and rapidly when leading a turnaround; bold, timely action can accelerate change.
- Leverage civic and philanthropic engagement to build networks and open doors in a new community.
- Address systemic unfairness with iterative, data-driven redesigns to gain credible, implementable solutions.

## Decision heuristics

- When internal bias and noise obscure talent assessment, engage established external experts to provide objective evaluations.
- If local gatekeepers block access to senior stakeholders, contact the stakeholder directly to test assumptions and build the relationship.
- Prefer bold, rapid interventions over slow incrementalism in turnarounds: act quickly and iterate based on results.
- Use visible philanthropic or civic actions to create introductions and credibility in a new community.
- For politically sensitive, large-scale redesigns, iterate models repeatedly until you reach a defensible, improved allocation and seek unanimous governance approval.
