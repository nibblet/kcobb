Famous People I’ve Met
(None of Whom Would Ever
Remember Me)
I have had the pleasure of meeting many celebrities over the years. Most of these encounters were brief and many were not particularly substantive—surely, they were memorable only to me,
not to the people I met. Y et they le ft indelible impressions on me,
life lessons that have lighted my pathways for years and probably will continue to do so in years to come. A summary of a few of these follows.
My earliest recollection of meeting a celebrity was an encounter with Elvis Presley in about 1956. This is when I was a fifteen-year-old clarinet player in a Dixieland jazz band that won a spot in the annual Mid-South Fair T alent Competition in Memphis, T ennessee.
Elvis was twenty-one years old and at a break-out point of his career,
having just recorded some his first songs and signed a contract with
RCA. Colonel Parker, his agent, was managing part of the talent contest as well as promoting Elvis at the fair. He tried to get Elvis out on stage to showcase him to our audience, but for some reason,
Elvis refused to make an appearance. I vividly remember him out back in the alleyway behind the stage, throwing a wristwatch back and forth to a friend. He chatted it up with the five of us in our band as we stood and watched them. He bragged about winning a teddy bear as a prize at one of the carnival booths and ridiculed Colonel
Parker for being an unbridled promoter. I also remember being struck by his obstinacy, and thinking what a fool he was to miss such a publicity opportunity. And, of course, I remember the numerous times I have recalled this miniscule incident and thought about what little difference it made to his spectacular career. T elling people that
I once met Elvis Presley has been fun. More importantly, I learned a lesson from the encounter—that minor blips do not sidetrack the momentum of a career trajectory.
Somewhere near the same time, I had the opportunity for a trip to the National Boy Scout Jamboree at Valley Forge that I
wrote about earlier. The itinerary included a side trip to New Y ork,
where our scout troop attended a Brooklyn Dodgers-Chicago Cubs baseball game at Ebbets Field, truly a memorable experience. I was a diehard baseball fan and this was my first Major League game. I
still remember the score: Dodgers 6, Cubs 3. Even more memorable was a locker room visit after the game. Our small group had the opportunity to talk with Roy Campanella, the legendary Dodger catcher. He was pleasant and patient with us, answering all of our questions and encouraging us to diligently practice whatever interested us. He showed us his left hand, which seemed to me at the time to be the most gnarled example of human flesh I could imagine.
The hand re flected years of ba ttering behind the plate. He talked about the constant pain, but how it was worth all of it for the glory of Major League baseball. His grit inspired me. Many times over the years since then, I have pictured that gnarled hand as I contemplated interferences with my own ambitions.
In about 1990, I had a chance to meet and talk with T om Clancy,
who by happenstance was seated next to me at a business luncheon in his hometown of Baltimore. He was at the early stages of his notoriety and naturally was peppered with questions. He told the people at our table about how he managed to write The Hunt for Red
October, his first blockbuster novel. He described his school days at
Loyola University and his earlier career as a humble State Farm agent.
He related his numerous misguided attempts at writing and then his fortuitous meeting of some retired admirals at an Annapolis pub.
He confessed how he surreptitiously played their stories one against another and eventually pieced together the intricate submarine details contained in the book. He then described how he gave a copy of the book to an Argentine lady who had been seated next to him on an airplane and how she, in turn, passed it on to her friend President
Ronald Reagan. The president was interviewed on TV not long after and was asked what he might be reading for fun. He turned, picked up the book and said, “Well, I’m reading this excellent book called
The Hunt for Red October. ” After that little slice of free promotion, no further marketing was necessary! This anecdote has often reminded me how serendipity, coupled with a little skill, can dramatically change one’s life.
During the 1990–1995 era when I was with KPMG in
Philadelphia, I was responsible for the firm’s Washington, D.C.
office and travelled there regularly on business. I would take the early-morning Amtrak train to D.C. ’s Union Station and, usually, a late-afternoon train back; there was a stop in Wilmington, Delaware each way. Occasionally, Senator Joe Biden would be on the train,
and I had a chance to sit with him several times and talk (or more accurately, listen!). He had recently su ffered a brain aneurism,
similar to my close friend Hy Indowsky, from the Fort Lauderdale
Peat Marwick office, so we had at least a common starting point for a conversation. I learned from Senator Biden that he chose to commute to Washington from Delaware daily, rather than take up residence in the Capitol. He had lost his wife in an automobile accident and was committed to being home with his family whenever possible. He also told me why he was a teetotaler and had resolved to never drink, as well as stories about how a stuttering impediment had impacted his youth. I told him about my son Mark, who was dealing with speech issues. He struck me at the time as an everyday
Joe—almost a blue-collar poster boy—and I had to pinch myself to realize that he was a powerful United States senator. It never crossed my mind that one day he would be vice president of the United
States. Along with his discourses about political philosophies and personal journeys, his family devotion le ft a big impression on me,
influencing my thoughts about family and commitment for the next twenty-five years and more.
In Philadelphia in the early 1990s, General Colin Powell spoke at a large business banquet; it was shortly after his resignation from the military. KPMG was one of the sponsors for the event, and Dot and I represented the firm at a private pre-banquet meeting of about a dozen community leaders with him. General Powell spent almost an hour in a question-and-answer session with this small group. I
was in awe listening to him talk extemporaneously on a wide range of subjects. His command of the world stage was nothing short of astonishing. It seemed as if he was intimately familiar with every place on the globe. Moreover, the name of every world leader, regardless of how remote or obscure, was on the tip of his tongue. I asked him a couple of questions. In his responses, he looked me straight in the eye and gave me an eerie feeling of sincere connection. T ruly a most impressive man! I watched him carefully as he displayed a genuine respect for the country and its government, and proposed solutions for world problems—all with the calm aplomb of a world-class professional. He caused me to yearn for a more comprehensive understanding of my world and con firmed my respect for the professionalism of our military. I really liked this man and will never forget this special hour of enlightenment. A photograph of this event sits in my study today.
In about 2005, Dot and I travelled to London for an RHR
International board meeting. Our hotel was sponsoring a classical concert and offered us some complimentary tickets, which we gladly accepted. As it turned out, the concert was a highbrow event at an old Christopher Wren church in the Chelsea district. As we sat all prim and proper in the small audience, only two hundred people or so, Dot pointed out Margaret Thatcher in a seat just across the center aisle from us. Following the concert, we attended a nice reception in another part of the church. We again noticed Margaret Thatcher.
She was standing along the perimeter of the crowd, alone except for her bodyguard, and on an impulse, I walked over and struck up a conversation with her. Although I should have logically expected her to be an engaging conversationalist, I was nevertheless surprised and stricken by her charm and the ease of the conversation. I spent about twenty minutes talking about everything, from Boynton Beach,
Florida to the French rioting in Paris. One of my curious friends later asked, “Just what the hell would you have to talk about with
Margaret Thatcher for twenty minutes?” I don’t recall my answer, but
I do recall how many times since then I’ve been reminded that there is no good reason to be intimidated by anyone. My self-assurance,
admittedly never altogether in the background, was rea ffirmed by this experience.

And then there was Miss Oceola McCarty. While perhaps not as famous as some of the others, Miss McCarty gained notoriety and national publicity in the late 1990s. She was a poor African-American washerwoman in Hattiesburg, Mississippi, who at age eighty-seven had donated her life savings of $150,000 to the University of Southern
Mississippi for a scholarship fund. The national media latched onto her story, and she became a national emblem of sel fless charity.
She was awarded the Presidential Citizens Award by President Bill
Clinton in 1998. Around that time, the USM Foundation escorted
Miss McCarty to New Y ork to visit potential donors who might be interested in matching her generosity. Knowing that I had business contacts in the New Y ork financial community and was an alumnus,
they invited me to accompany them. The University hosted a nice luncheon at the Metropolitan Club, and I had the privilege of sitting at the head table with Miss McCarty, the USM president, Senator
T rent Lott and his wife, Dan Rather and a couple of other journalists who had covered her story. Of course, this remarkable little lady was the star of the show at our table. Even in the midst of the splendor of the New Y ork glitz, her humility and selflessness matched the bright lights of the city. For an uneducated and lowly Southern lady, she displayed remarkable wit, self-con fidence and clever conversation.
Senator Lott asked her if she had any secrets about ironing a man’s shirt, to which she responded by explaining in great detail how one should start with the cu ff and finish with the collar. He replied,
“Well, that’s interesting. I always start with the collar and end with the cuff. ” She quickly quipped, with a wry grin, “Well, Mr. Lott, it’s just like some of the things you folks are doing up in Washington.
Y ou’re just doing it wrong!” I loved this little lady. She’s now gone,
of course, but I think of her o ften, often when they pass around the offering plate in our church. What an inspiration!
Over the years, I have met numerous other well-known personalities, but the above stories illustrate some of the timeless inspiration and guidance that I have gleaned from these brief experiences.
Many people tend to either worship or despise famous people.
I have tried to parse out some of the characteristics that make celebrities special and to learn from these people without wholesale adulation, judgment, or contempt. And I indeed have learned quite a lot from them. I am, in part, a product of these experiences; they shaped my thinking for years to come.
