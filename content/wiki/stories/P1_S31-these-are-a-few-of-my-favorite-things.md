These Are a Few of My
Favorite Things
Study hall periods for seventh and eighth graders at Calhoun
City School were monotonous and uncomfortable. They were held in the auditorium on the second floor of the old school building.
On either side of the stage was a small dressing room that was used for piano lessons —one, on the le ft, occupied by Libba Jane
Harrelson for beginning levels and the other by Freddie Cox for more advanced students. T eachers proctored the aisles of the study hall with stern expressions, trying to maintain discipline and ensure that “studying” was taking place. However, there was no keeping the noise level down, due largely to the constant competing cacophony penetrating the thin walls of those little dressing rooms. There was much hunting and pecking, repetitious musical scales, dissonant chords and fumbling around with strange melodies. Y et in the midst of all this monotony and noise, there were occasional bursts of beauty. A few talented students practiced and learned musical scores that radiated sonorously throughout that small auditorium.
My young ears always perked up when these exceptions seeped through the walls. My love of music was hatching.
Music has played a pivotal role in my life for as long as I can remember. The impact of music is difficult to explain, but it has had a profound influence on my personality and my cultural foundation.
Music has served as a trigger for memories of key events. I have looked back over the years and tried to formulate a list of my T op
T en songs. That task has proved daunting because there are so many candidates, in such a wide variety of styles and genres. Nevertheless, I
have struggled and constructed such a list. The musical compositions described in the following nine paragraphs have special places in my heart—places occupied for a variety of reasons. A brief overview of each follows, ranked in no particular order.
“Somewhere Over the Rainbow” is an incredibly beautiful song that belongs near the top of the list. Harold Arlen and E.Y. Harburg wrote this song in 1939 for The Wizard of Oz movie starring Judy
Garland as Dorothy, who sings it to her little dog, T oto. The melody and lyrics are equally touching. I was first drawn to the melody, which left an unforge ttable tune plastered inside my brain, as a teenager.
Then I began to focus on the lyrics, which somehow echoed much of my yearnings in those quixotic days —“Somewhere over the rainbow, blue birds fly, and the dreams that you dreamed of, dreams really do come true. ” I loved that line, which seemed to li ft me up and take me out of Calhoun City. It also seemed to me to express a universal hope. Then I realized that the music and lyrics matched perfectly. I learned to play “Somewhere Over the Rainbow” on the piano, and I have played it probably more than a thousand times over the past fifty years. It still raises goosebumps on my arms, and I
love it as much now as I did the first time I heard it.
There were times when Freddie Cox would fill in gaps in his lesson schedule by playing the piano himself. Identifying those occasions was easy. Freddie, a somewhat odd character otherwise,
was an absolute piano virtuoso with truly unique talent and skill.
He played “Claire de Lune” like no one I have heard since. Now,
bear in mind, I was twelve or thirteen years old at the time and knew nothing whatsoever about formal music. So perhaps my views might have been a bit exaggerated. However, I came to simply adore
“Clair de Lune” , the Claude Debussy masterpiece. The mathematical precision, the hills and valleys of the melody, and the odd chords all blended to create a haunting theme that resonated in my soul and has lived with me for a lifetime. It’s been said that Debussy was a musical Impressionist; he and his interpreter Freddie Cox most definitely left an impression on me. I never learned to play “Clair de Lune” on the piano; it is above my skill level. But the everlasting impression this magnificent work left inside me is the reason I now place it in my litany of top favorite musical compositions.
Van Cliburn skyrocketed to international fame in 1958, when he won the first T chaikovsky International Piano Competition in
Moscow, playing with impeccable perfection T chaikovsky’s Piano
Concerto No. 1 in B Flat Minor, a classic composed in 1875. Somehow
Libba Jane Harrelson knew about this remarkable and historical event and presented me with a gi ft of the soundtrack. I still have it stored away with my other relics. Of course, it’s hardly playable now, since the format is the old 33 1/3 rpm disc, but I managed to download it into my iTunes library and still play it from time to time,
along with other artists’ renditions. In my view, the concerto is one of the most powerful pieces of classical music ever composed. I love it, and I love the memory of Libba Jane, who surely had no idea how much I would enjoy it for the rest of my life.
Shifting to American in fluences on the classical scene, while in high school I became addicted to George Gershwin’s Rhapsody in
Blue. Less structured technically than the European classical works,
Rhapsody is probably be tter characterized as classical jazz. The melodies and pace of the music demand one’s full a ttention from beginning to end. It has demanded my attention for more than fifty years, from the moment it opens with the clarinet glissando to the haunting blues melody throughout.
The Eddy Duchin Story , filmed in the mid-1950s, starred
Tyrone Power and Kim Novak, with piano music performed by
Carmen Cavalaro. I saw the movie and absolutely adored the music,
particularly the theme song, “T o Love Again” . This theme was based on Chopin’s “Nocturne in E Flat” , another one of the beautiful pieces from the golden age of European composers. This one song has also remained by my side for more than fifty years. I now have the original soundtrack of The Eddy Duchin Story in my iTunes library and play it regularly (over and over again) on my iPhone and home Sonos system. The “Nocturne” has essentially become my personal theme song; it effectively defines my love for piano music.
When I played clarinet in the Dixieland band in high school and came to love Dixieland music, I tried my best to emulate Pete
Fountain, the eminent clarinetist of that day. Pete had a fluid and woody tone that I simply could not replicate, no ma tter how hard I
tried. Anyway, the most famous song among the almost one hundred albums he recorded over the years was “Just a Closer Walk With
Thee” . It was his theme song, played at virtually every performance over a five-decade career. I loved it as a teenager and I still do. It defines New Orleans music and a segment of Dixieland jazz for me.
“Just a Closer Walk” is an easy member of my T op T en.
My music education and familiarity basically originated in the church. Dozens of favorite hymns and religious compositions have touched my soul over the years. However, one stands out singularly—“The Lord’s Prayer” , written by a little-known composer named Albert Malo tte. My first recollection of this masterpiece was a stunning performance in the First Baptist Church by Ada
Gwyn Prior, a remarkably talented soloist, in Calhoun City. It still stirs incredible emotion within me whenever I think about it.
Absolute beauty! Probably the best contemporary rendition of this magni ficent music is a recording by Andrea Bocelli with the
Mormon T abernacle Choir. It can’t be beat!
Moving along, the 1950s and 1960s decades were a time of inspiration and experimentation in the world of music. My choice of the best song of that era is “Y esterday” , performed by the Beatles with Paul McCartney as the lead singer. While the music is unique and exquisite, the lyrics spoke to me in the 1960s. “Y esterday, all my troubles seemed so far away… ” . Like a mirror, they re flected a lot of my inner thinking at the time. That thinking, interestingly, has now morphed more into “tomorrow”! “Y esterday” stands out as one of the most famous songs in music history. I fully understand that notoriety.
Broadway musicals and the movies have produced dozens of candidates for my T op T en. The magic works of some of the great composers live through the decades —Richard Rodgers, Oscar
Hammerstein, George Gershwin, Cole Porter and on and on. From this long list of worthy candidates, I have chosen Burt Bacharach and his flagship song, “Raindrops Keep Falling on My Head” . Bacharach’s music has often been classified as perhaps the most complex among the works of American composers —dizzying arrays of melodies,
chord changes, modulations and key changes, making the songs extremely difficult for musicians to play. Y et, the tunes and rhythms are catchy and seem to stick like glue in one’s memory. “Raindrops”
is like that —complicated and infectious. This might seem an odd choice of a favorite from this genre, but it reflects my admiration for a true musical genius.
My last Top Ten selection is an American tradition for the past century. It’s “ The Stars and Stripes Forever”, composed by
John Philip Sousa in 1896. It’s the o fficial march of the United
States. It brings people to their feet and triggers applause whenever and wherever it is performed. The composition features the entire marching band, from tuba to piccolo. Its brisk pace imbues the space with a powerful sense of patriotism. I am inspired every time I hear it. It’s an easy T op T en pick.
I can think of dozens of others —perhaps hundreds—that stir up my emotions. “ Are Y ou Lonesome T onight” (Elvis Presley),
“That’s What Friends Are For” (Burt Bacharach), “How Great
Thou Art” ( The Gaithers), “I Will Always Love Y ou” (Whitney
Houston), “Battle Hymn of the Republic” (Mormon T abernacle
Choir), “Hey Jude” (The Beatles), “Hallelujah Chorus” by George
Frideric Handel, “Y ou Light Up My Life” (Debby Boone), “What A
Wonderful World” (Louis Armstrong), “The Way We Were” (Barbra
Streisand), “Moon River” by Henry Mancini and Johnny Mercer,
“White Christmas” (Bing Crosby), “1812 Overture” (T chaikovsky),
“Don’t Cry For Me Argentina” by Andrew Lloyd Webber and Tim
Rice, “ Amazing Grace” and on and on. Heck, maybe I should have set out to list the T op 100! I can’t seem to stop.
The common theme running through all these compositions and recordings is how they have touched me over time. They all fall into a category that I think of as magic potions, casting all sorts of different spells. Music has incredible power. It has brightened my bleakest days. It stirs up wonderful memories in my life and can change my mood in a flash. So, I rely on my favorite music to keep my spirits alive and healthy. As Julie Andrews sang so beautifully in
The Sound of Music: “I simply remember my favorite things, and then
I don’t feel so bad. ”
