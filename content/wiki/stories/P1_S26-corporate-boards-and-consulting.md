# Corporate Boards and Consulting

> After leaving his executive role in 1997, the narrator built a third career as a corporate board member, consultant and adjunct professor, through a sequence of board appointments, entrepreneurial investments, consulting engagements and teaching. He experienced notable successes (Alliance Data, Fed board, consulting retainers) and failures (Laundromax), and learned governance, risk and professional lessons.

Corporate Boards and Consulting
When my Alamo gig came to an end in early 1997, I was fifty-six years old. For a short time, I mused about what I might do with the rest of my life. Then the phone began to ring, and I found myself gradually drifting into a third career. I soon learned that there was a demand for my services, and I slowly morphed into a professional corporate board member, strategic consultant and college professor.
My first call came from the Federal Reserve Bank. I was invited to join the board of the Miami branch of the Fed and served there for six years—two three-year terms—before being term limited out.
I never learned exactly how I was selected for this post. I asked the bank president who had called me and he said, “Beats me, I just got a call from Washington and was told that you’re the man!” I suspected it had something to do with my previous position as head of financial services for KPMG.
Without a doubt, the Federal Reserve Bank is the most professional organization I was ever associated with over the many years. Serving on its Miami board was one of the more enjoyable experiences of my entire career. I rubbed shoulders with some great people, got a practical education about economics and monetary policy and felt that I made a genuine contribution. I attended many meetings of the board at the Atlanta bank, as an emissary for the
Miami bank, and once attended a Board of Governors meeting in
Washington, D.C. I actually had dinner once with Alan Greenspan.
In addition to all of those positives, the board membership carried with it a distinctive cachet; I became recognized throughout the
South Florida business community as some sort of important super economics guru. This reputation was overblown, of course,
and undeserved. But I enjoyed it nevertheless. There are still some remnants of that celebrity hanging around today, more than ten years after I left the board.
I was also approached early on about joining the board of RHR
International, Inc., a well-known international consulting firm,
comprised of corporate psychologists, that served the Fortune 500
community. I had used the services of this firm with human resource challenges at both KPMG and Alamo, and was well familiar with their work. I was a friend of T om Saporito, one of its senior partners who had provided valuable counsel to me over the past few years.
T om recommended me to their board chairman. All of the partner/shareholders in this firm were psychologists. They were fantastic at what they did, but not particularly well trained in business ma tters; engaging a board of independent directors was a wise decision for them. I, on the other hand, had worked with consultants at KPMG for more than thirty years and knew that discipline through and through. For this reason, I think I made a signi ficant contribution to the operations of RHR over the ten years that I served on their board. The firm was a private company owned by the consultants and our board compensation was modest.
Nevertheless, I thoroughly enjoyed the experience.
Another early board experience was with a couple of young guys in South Florida who smoked me out of the woodwork in 1999 and asked me to sign up with a new mutual fund they were organizing. I met with them and was impressed with their enthusiasm and unbridled commitment. I especially was taken with the intellect and energy of David Zwick, their leader. I thought their idea was ambitious and was dubious about their ability to pull it o ff, particularly with their youth and inexperience. Think about it. Two young fellows in their early thirties trying to launch from scratch a highly regulated mutual fund specializing in federally mandated funding for low-income properties. How likely is that to be successful? However, I was intrigued with the concept for some reason and agreed to do it. So, I joined the board as an independent director of CRA Qualified Investment Fund, along with an eclectic assortment of other trustees. There were certainly challenges along the way for both management and the board, but the venture was quite successful. They are still alive today with about $2 billion in assets and a pristine reputation. I served on the board for several years before dropping off to take advantage of other opportunities.
Actually, I became weary of dealing with a few board members who, I felt, were being small-minded, and resigned to move on to something more interesting for me.
In the same general time frame, I was approached by two other young entrepreneurs, Alan Haig and Eric Lambert. They had a bold plan that I found intriguing. Both guys were about thirty years old and had excellent academic and professional backgrounds. Their idea was to build out six hundred modern coin laundries across the nation. I processed these guys through my tried and true formula for judging people —intelligence, energy and integrity —and they passed the test with flying colors. So, I agreed to invest in the start-up enterprise and serve on the board of directors of Laundromax.
Before long, I found myself as chairman. A fter two years of feverish activity, the business failed and the investors lost all of their money.
What went wrong? The founders were bright, capable professionals with unusual energy and drive. They had done extensive research and carefully modeled every aspect of the business before launching the venture. A highly experienced and diversified board of directors supported them. The directors all had their own money at stake.
Approximately eighty-five sophisticated investors had invested $17
million after studying the business plan. Banks were extending credit totaling $24 million. Among these groups, no one had questioned either the soundness of the business strategy or the progress of the concept development. In the end analysis, the answer was simple.
It was fundamental Economics and Financial Management 101.
The capital markets for entrepreneurial businesses completely dried up, quite suddenly, in the year 2000. We could not raise the money necessary to expand the business to the breakeven point.
The investors lost their entire investment —all $17 million! I had invested $400,000 and, of course, lost it all.
Laundromax was a collection of harsh lessons for me. One of those lessons was that some things in life are just not controllable, no matter how much effort is expended. Other lessons are documented in a business case study that I wrote at the request of the graduate
School of Business and Entrepreneurship at Nova Southeastern
University. Most university case studies pivot around stellar business successes. Nova Southeastern reasoned that their students needed to be schooled in failures as well. Over the last several years, I have given lectures on this case study to over four hundred Nova MBA
students. Alan Haig, one of the two young founders, has remained a close friend and has participated in a couple of those lectures.
My board experiences continued to diversify. My good friend
Ned Allen sold a mobile home property business in the late 1990s to Chateau Communities, Inc., a sizable real estate investment trust
(REIT) based in Denver. As a result of receiving stock in connection with the sale, he had a signi ficant investment in the company and was a member of its board of directors. Ned thought I could make a contribution and convinced the board to invite me to serve as an independent director. I agreed and immediately began working on audit committee issues there. It was my first experience as a director of a company listed on the New Y ork Stock Exchange (NYSE). The board membership was a relatively short-lived experience —about two and a half years. The REIT became the victim of a hostile takeover a ttempt by Sam Zell, a well-known corporate raider.
That unfortunate event put Chateau Communities in play, and we ultimately sold it to the State of Oregon pension plan. I enjoyed my short term on this board, especially all the shenanigans associated with the ultimate sale of the business.
One day in 2002, I received a breakfast invitation from Alan
Levan, chairman of BankAtlantic Bancorp, a $5 billion company that owned Bank Atlantic, a community bank with operations throughout South and Central Florida. I didn’t know Alan well.
He had been an investor in Laundromax and had served on the board with me there, though he had dropped o ff when the going got tough. Over breakfast, he told me he had admired my skills as a Laundromax director and particularly the way I had handled the wind-down (when he had ducked and run). He invited me to join the BankAtlantic Bancorp board. I agreed. A year later, I was also appointed to the board of BFC Financial, a related company. Both companies were listed on the NYSE at the time and were intertwined in their ownership of the bank, their primary asset.
I served on these boards for ten years and made some significant contributions along the way. As time passed, a weakening economy,
management issues and vexatious federal regulations made for a dismal scenario and declining fortunes. I became somewhat of a contrarian director as the bank ’s fortunes dwindled. The other directors seemed pathetically passive and generally agreeable with whatever Alan proposed. Despite his controlling nature, Alan seemed to appreciate my independence and respect my insights. The economic and regulatory pressures forced the sale of BankAtlantic in 2012 to BB&T . After the sale, both of the remaining companies were a mere fraction of their earlier size and not nearly as interesting or challenging for me. Moreover, I had a serious disagreement in early 2013 over what I thought was an outrageous $30 million executive compensation package proposed for the four top officers of the surviving companies. I voted against the compensation and then resigned as a director of both companies. I handled this disagreement and resignation professionally and without fanfare,
and I am still on friendly terms with Alan and other management.
In fact, at Alan’s suggestion, they agreed to pay me a $5,000/month retainer for eighteen months in case they might need my advice and counsel after I left the board. That’s good work if you can get it!
I served on several other boards along the way. Steve Swink,
a personal friend since childhood, got himself involved and wound up as the CEO of a delivery company in New York. The company was Dispatch Management Services Corp., a public company with some impressive logistics technology. At Steve’s request, I joined the board. I also persuaded two of my friends,
Ned Allen and Tom Saporito, to join me there. The business was troubled from the start, and Steve’s attempted turnaround was unsuccessful. Steve resigned, and then Ned, Tom and I all left the board ourselves. This was a fairly miserable experience for us all.
On a more positive side, I served for several years on the board of First Fleet Corp., a truck-leasing company privately owned by my friend and neighbor John Flynn. First Fleet was an interesting and complex business that we eventually sold to PHH Corporation (a
KPMG client when I was in Baltimore) for $30 million. I also served for two years on the board of Renaissance Cruises, a prominent cruise line headed by Ed Rudner, an old friend from my early Alamo
Rent A Car days. As a result of all these varied experiences, I learned a lot about corporate governance —valuable experience in today’s complex and litigious business society.
In about 2005, Craig Farlie and Mike Turner approached my good friend Jim Blosser and me about serving as a board of advisors for a new entrepreneurial company they were planning to launch.
We agreed and for the next three years served as a couple of gray-haired consultants as these two young guys embarked on their ambitious venture—starting a new investment banking firm from scratch. Farlie Turner & Co. has emerged as a highly successful middle market investment banking firm and is widely recognized as a quality service provider in the financial marketplace. Watching this success story take place from its embryonic stage to today, and being a part of its development in the early days, has been quite interesting.
The most signi ficant of all the boards I have worked with is
Alliance Data Systems Corp., an incredibly successful company operating in the private label credit card and data marketing space. The company ranks as one of the top five performers of all companies listed on the New Y ork Stock Exchange over the past ten years. Roger Ballou joined their board in about 2001. In
2004, the company sensed a need for additional board talent, and
Roger recommended me. A fter a lengthy interview process, I was appointed to its eight-member board and have served there for the past ten years. Without a doubt, this has been the most sophisticated assembly of individuals I have ever worked with. Every one of the board members is incredibly smart, works diligently, is ethical and behaves responsibly in all situations. It has been a pure pleasure for me. Moreover, it has been financially rewarding, as I have received a significant portion of my board fees in company stock, and the stock has increased eight-fold since I joined.
In the midst of all this corporate board activity, I have been called on for consulting services from time to time. I never sought out any of this activity, but it seemed as if every time I turned around, I saw a door opening.
In 2002, Roger Ballou had moved to Philadelphia as the CEO
of CDI Corporation. For several years CDI had owned Modern
Engineering, an automotive design company in Detroit serving the Big Three automotive companies with 1,200 engineers. CDI’s board began to reason that the business was not synergistic with
CDI’s other contract staffing business. CDI had been trying to sell the company for more than a year, without any success. Roger called and asked if I would take on a project of disposing of the subsidiary.
We worked out an agreement whereby I would be paid a per diem fee for my services with a success fee of $100,000 if I could sign a buyer within six months. I booked a flight and headed to Detroit to review the business. Fortuitously, on this first day of the assignment
I accidentally stumbled upon a guy who expressed a keen interest in acquiring the company. Just dumb luck! We hammered out the details over the next few weeks, and within six months the sale was closed. I never talked to a single other prospect! I of course collected my success fee.
Roger called me again, about three years later, with another problem. CDI’s external auditors had issued their audit report for the year 2004 and reported a material weakness in internal control.
This was in the early days of Sarbanes-Oxley regulations. “Material weaknesses” were considered extremely serious matters, both by the
Security Exchange Commission (SEC) and the company’s board of directors. Roger was disappointed in his CFO and accounting staff, and was frustrated to no end. He was reaching out to me to say, “Help!” I agreed and made several trips to Philadelphia to design remedial measures and monitor corrective action by the financial staff. I also served as an intermediary with CDI’s external auditors,
inasmuch as I had known the engagement partner Jerry Maginnis at
KPMG for many years. I had in fact nominated him for partnership in the firm more than ten years earlier —a convenient coincidence.
In this instance, both financial expertise and relationships combined to result in a happy ending.
Shortly after leaving Alamo, I was contacted by T om Shea, CEO
of Right Management Consultants, who wanted to engage me as an independent consultant. He and his wife, Maureen, jointly owned and operated the Florida/Caribbean franchise for Right Management.
We worked out an arrangement where I would meet with them for four hours each month and advise them on a wide range of matters.
They agreed to a monthly fee for these services. T oday, eighteen years and over two hundred meetings later, I am still serving them in this capacity—at the same fee. Over the course of those years, I have advised them on the complete panoply of their lives —all aspects of their business, their personal investments, managing their sons’
affairs as they matured, community involvement and a host of other matters. They consult with me on virtually every significant decision they make. It’s not that I have any special wisdom or insight. I don’t.
They simply desire an independent sounding board and a resource to test their judgment against. And they are willing to pay for that.
This has been a pleasurable experience for me (or else I wouldn’t do it). I think it has been productive for them as well (or else they wouldn’t do it.). T om and Maureen have becomevery special friends and people who I admire greatly. I like to think I have been helpful to them over the years.
From time to time, I have taken on a variety of other projects as a consultant. I did some work for Bear Sterns in New Y ork a few years back, advising them about the rental car industry, which had piqued their firm’s interest. I did some limited strategic consulting for a sizable interior design firm in Naples, Florida. I negotiated the acquisition of controlling interest in an insurance company in Pennsylvania for an old friend, who paid me with a 5 percent interest in the company. I did expert witness testimony on a couple of occasions. One other interesting project was the negotiation of a complex, high-pro file divorce se ttlement for Pam and Steve
Berrard. These and other projects have run the gamut of financial matters, industry expertise, negotiation and, in some cases, just plain common sense. All have been enlightening and rewarding for me personally.
The third leg of my post-Alamo career was the role of college professor. In about 2003, Randy Pohlman, dean of the business school at Nova Southeastern University, approached me about teaching in the graduate school as an adjunct professor. I really had no interest in such a thing and politely declined. I was later talking with
T om Tworoger, a long-time friend and a full-time professor there.
T om leaned on me hard and insisted that I just try it. He promised I
would love it. I consulted with Dot, who encouraged me. So I took the plunge and agreed to teach one course in Entrepreneurial Finance.
After one semester, I was hooked —just as T om had predicted. I
began teaching the same course twice a year and continued that for ten years. It was one of the more enjoyable things I ever did, much to my surprise. I loved it! I was at home in the classroom, delivering to a diverse cadre of students a combination of academic knowledge and practical experience from a lifetime of business activity. And diverse the students were! They came in all shapes and forms. Over the years,
I had two dentists, two pharmacists, a 747 pilot, a minister, an Army colonel, a Pratt & Whitney plant manager, a farmer, a Saudi prince—
you name it—all ages of men, women, many nationalities and ethnic groups. The common thread among this melting pot was a desire to own and operate one’s own business. Most were paying for the course with their own money (well, maybe not the Saudi prince!)
and were genuinely interested. Over the ten years, I taught over five hundred students. I received plaudits and excellent ratings from the students, and several have stayed in touch with me. A few have become good friends, and I have watched them become successful business people. I have now retired from teaching regularly, but have agreed to serve as a guest lecturer at Nova from time to time.
Throughout my career, I have been a staunch believer in continuing education to keep “sharpening my saw” , as Stephen Covey put it in his best-selling book, The Seven Habits of Highly E ffective
People. I have attended as well as led numerous KPMG seminars throughout the years; have attended several concentrated executive leadership programs at Harvard University, Stanford University and the Wharton School of Finance; have attended numerous corporate governance seminars and programs; have served on panels for
Corporate Boardroom programs; have attended NYSE-sponsored peer exchange programs; and have participated as an expert panelist on programs sponsored by the National Association of Corporate
Directors. Invariably, I have learned something every time I
participated in such programs.

## Principles

- Judge founders and leaders by intelligence, energy and integrity.
- Accept that external market forces can make outcomes unmanageable despite sound planning.
- Maintain professional conduct when disagreeing or exiting roles.
- Use failures as teaching tools and case studies to pass lessons to others.
- Leverage personal and professional networks to create opportunities and solutions.

## Decision heuristics

- Screen potential founders/partners for intelligence, energy and integrity before committing.
- When market liquidity disappears, recognize limits of control and stop assuming expansion will continue.
- If a compensation or governance decision conflicts with personal principles, vote your stance and be prepared to resign.
- Accept short-term, fee-based consulting assignments with a clear success metric and act quickly to exploit serendipitous leads.
- Serve as an independent sounding board for clients who want objective testing of judgment and be willing to maintain long-term retainer relationships.
