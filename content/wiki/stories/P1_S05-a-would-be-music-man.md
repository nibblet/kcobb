## Full Text

A Would-be Music Man
The story of my musical career is a composition without a finale,
an aspirant’s career that actually never happened. I had grand visions of such a career at an early age, but musical mastery never developed.
Based on the evidence, it was not because of a lack of talent. More likely, it was because of a lack of discipline. Was I just lazy? Or was it all the distractions, fueled by my curiosity about so many things?
Whatever the cause, it is clear that I had some level of talent that I
never fully developed. Although mastery simply didn’t seem to be in the scheme of things for me, I have tinkered with music and musical instruments all my life, and have developed an enduring love for music.
My earliest musical experimentation began with a harmonica at six or seven years old. Blue Cole, our next-door neighbor and self-appointed surrogate father, bought a small Hohner Marine Band harmonica, gave it to me and challenged me to learn to play “Old
Black Joe” . I was fascinated with the instrument and sat under the big oak tree in our front yard for hours playing with it. Before too long, I figured out the basics and learned to play the melody. Blue was ecstatic. When I would play a tune, he would roar with a loud belly laugh and slap his legs. He then put me on display, taking me down to his Standard Oil depot and asking me to play “Old Black
Joe” for his friends and customers. I became his personal monkey and organ grinder!
I do not remember when piano lessons began and I honestly do not recall how many years I took lessons. Not too many, consistently,
I think. My best guess would be that I started in about fourth or fifth grade. My mother recognized that I might have some talent. She enrolled me for private lessons and bought a new Bradbury piano on a rental/purchase plan, a big financial commitment on her part. I
was in and out of piano lessons for a few years, but I never performed well—or even moderately well. Piano lessons were a painful experience for me. I floundered around in a world of dichotomy. I
loved the piano, but I despised practicing all the scales and required exercises in the John Thompson’s method books. When it came to piano practice, I was a certi fiable goof-o ff. My instructors would always report my dalliances to Mother and o ffer up comments like,
“It’s such a waste. He clearly has the ability. ” Mother was distraught,
having sacri ficed financially to buy the piano and then me not performing up to her expectations. There was one embarrassing time when I failed to learn my recital piece and was not allowed to participate, even though my name had been printed in the program.
Mother was humiliated and said, “That’s it. No more piano lessons. ”
Under my breath I said, “Thank you God!”
An interesting development occurred along the way, however.
As I had dutifully sat on the stool supposedly practicing, I began to learn that I could play by ear many of the songs I actually liked. So,
I would spend all the allo tted practice time tinkering with familiar melodies and chords. I have been tinkering ever since.
The Calhoun City schools decided to form a band the year I was in the sixth grade. In order to gin up some interest and populate the band, school officials commissioned an aptitude-testing program for all students above the fifth grade. Approximately a dozen students popped out of this study as having exceptional natural music ability,
and a campaign was launched to recruit them for the band. I was the youngest among that group. Others I recall being singled out included James Bailey Caviness, Hal Jr. Reese, Jerry Sue Stewart,
Martha Ford Harrelson, Jan Cutrell and Nancy Wall. James Bailey was a phenomenal find. He was a macho guy and a popular football player who considered all male musicians sissies. He vigorously resisted the pressure to become a member of the band. The school authorities eventually prevailed on his parents, however, and he reluctantly began playing the trombone. His progress was nothing short of astonishing. Within a year, he was the first chair trombonist at the prestigious Mississippi State Band clinic. It was later determined that he was one of those rare individuals possessing perfect pitch.
The clarinet was my instrument of choice, and I spent the next six years as an active member of the school band. The band was a thoroughly enjoyable experience. Much like my piano experience,
I didn’t spend much time on technical practicing and sight reading,
so I was extremely slow at it. But over time, I sounded good, and after Jerry Sue graduated, I moved up to first chair clarinetist. I was selected to attend the State Band clinic on the Gulf Coast, ostensibly because of my musical talent. Predictably, I had great difficulty with the sight-reading portion of the tryouts upon arriving at the clinic and I was relegated to the last chair in the clarinet section. I was embarrassed to no end, certain that they all regarded me as a fraud and were wondering how I had managed to qualify for the clinic in the first place. I managed to stumble through the first hours of the clinic, memorizing my part as we moved along. Before the clinic ended, I was back on a par with the other clarinet players and felt much better about myself.
Throughout high school I was a regular participant in all sorts of organized music activities. I sang in the Glee Club and the church choir and was a regular in school musical productions. My favorite production was Lelawala, where I had a singing and acting role as an
Indian brave.
When I was in the tenth grade, a small group from the band formed our own Dixieland jazz band. In addition to me, the members included Hal Jr. Reese on trumpet, Phil Ray on trombone, Sonny
Y oung on bass and John Edmondson on drums. Given our meager backgrounds and dearth of formal training, we were actually pre tty good! We began playing our Dixieland gig at various events and talent shows. In due course, we found ourselves competing in the
Mid-South Fair T alent Competition in Memphis, where the winner was promised a spot on the T ed Mack Amateur Hour in New Y ork.
We didn’t win, but we were featured on a local Memphis TV show.
An even more important highlight of this event was the opportunity we had to meet Elvis Presley!
Dixieland band at Mid-South Fair Talent Competition
We also formed a ten-piece dance orchestra in which I played both the clarinet and saxophone. We actually played for a few local dances.
During my junior year, Luther Castleberry, our new band director, suggested I should try out for the Lion’s All-State Band,
a well-known hallmark of success among the music cognoscenti. I
agreed, so he prepared the application for me. The organization sent copies of the required music and requested a tape recording for the audition process. When I first looked at the music, I was staggered by the complexity and tried to beg off. Mr. Castleberry, who played the clarinet fairly well himself, sat down and played the music for me. I
listened. And then he played it again. Before long, I was saying, “Hey,
that’s pretty music. I can do that. ” I learned the pieces from listening to him, without any meaningful reference to the sheet music. With his supervision, I then made the recording. He submi tted the tape,
and a couple of weeks later —Bingo! I was in! That is the good part of the story. When the band assembled at the University of Southern
Mississippi for rehearsals before a trip to New Y ork City, we all sat down for seating tryouts. Once again, I was an abject failure because of my pitiful sight-reading ability. Nevertheless, I managed to learn the music fairly quickly and ultimately emerged as a respectable member of the band.
There is an interesting sidebar to this anecdote. Because of my inadequacies, the director of the All-State Band sent me over to the
U.S.M. fine arts department and stationed me in a practice room with instructions not to come out until I learned all the music. As
I was slaving away in my solitude, I heard a knock on the door. The visitor was Dr. Gomer Pound, professor of clarinet at U.S.M. He approached me and said, “Who are you?” I told him and he said,
“Where did you learn to play like that?” I explained my situation and my awkward predicament and he remarked, “Don’t worry about the sheet music—would you please just play that piece again for me?”
He then asked me to play something —anything—from memory. I
fumbled, then conjured up one of my Dixieland pieces. When I had finished, he said to me, “Y ou are a very talented young man. Surely you know that. But you obviously are not applying yourself very well. Y ou need to come down here to this college so I can teach you. ”
When I returned home from the New Y ork trip, there was a le tter waiting for me from Dr. Pound offering me a $300 scholarship. And that’s how I first began considering a ttending U.S.M.! I never used the scholarship, because it required playing in the college marching band, which I considered too much work.
I had terrific guidance in all my high school music experiences.
Freddie Cox didn’t impart a lot of piano or music theory, but I
learned a lot about music appreciation from him. I absolutely loved to hear him play the piano. He was a true virtuoso. I tried to imitate him, but found it impossible to replicate his virtuosity. Despite his many unusual personality quirks, Freddie was a great mentor.
Libba Jane Harrelson, the church organist, was also a dedicated professional who gave us unusual a ttention and instruction. When
I was sick one summer, she came to my home to visit me and brought a gi ft, the Van Cliburn stereo recording from the famous
Rachmanino ff piano competition in Moscow. I cherished that album and played it hundreds of times. I did have a beef with Libba
Jane, however. She kept the church organ locked, with the key on a ribbon around her neck. I desperately wanted to play the organ and see how it worked, but she was resolute in not le tting me touch it. I
suppose I should forgive her for that!
Perhaps the best music leader in town was Mrs. Minnie Lee
Pryor, the consummate sophisticated lady who made sure everything we did at the church, music-wise, was first class and professional in every respect. I learned a lot from her.
I am indebted to all these people for their leadership.
The outgrowth of all these high school experiences was an intense love and appreciation for music. I liked all types —classical,
folk, rock & roll, blues, gospel, Broadway, marches, jazz, et al. Thanks to the mentioned mentors and others, I did indeed learn about all styles of music. I played in bands, sang in the choir and glee club,
listened to the radio, collected albums and generally centered much of my life in musical themes. That love and appreciation has remained with me for the rest of my life. We have both a grand piano and a Yamaha keyboard in our home today, and I still tinker —just for my own personal enjoyment.
In addition to the activities and mentors, I am grateful for the natural gifts that God gave me. I clearly don’t have perfect pitch or any other virtuoso talents, but I have been blessed with an unusual ear for music. Throughout my life I have failed to develop certain gifts I was blessed with, and regret not being more diligent in mastering musical skills at a young age. Perhaps there was too much going on or perhaps I was lazy insofar as musical development was concerned.
I still have a Hohner Chromatic harmonica today. I pull it out from time to time and play a tune or two, which o ften will include a stanza of “Old Black Joe” . When I do, I always think of Blue Cole,
slapping his legs, pa tting his feet and laughing. I won’t ever forget that he was the one who set me off on this long sentimental journey.

## Principles

- Lifelong engagement can come from enjoyment even without technical mastery.
- Mentor demonstration and support can compensate for formal-skill gaps and create opportunities.
- Be honest with yourself about willingness to invest time; declining commitments may be preferable to half-hearted participation.
- Regret follows neglected natural gifts when discipline is lacking.

## Decision heuristics

- When written music is overwhelming, learn by listening and rehearse with a knowledgeable mentor to create an audition-quality performance.
- If an opportunity requires a commitment you are unwilling to sustain, decline it rather than participate half-heartedly.
- Use allotted practice time to focus on personally motivating material (play by ear) to maintain engagement when technical drills are aversive.
