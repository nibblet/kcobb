New Roots in Fort Lauderdale
We arrived in Fort Lauderdale in the summer of 1975. A fter searching the various neighborhoods and surrounding suburbs, we decided on living inside the city itself and purchased a house in the
Coral Ridge area. The neighborhood was nice, probably a notch above our means at the time, but it appealed to us for a number of reasons. Having made the plunge from Jackson to Orlando just four years before, this move was smoother and easier. The people in Fort
Lauderdale were friendly and the business was well established. We settled in quickly, enrolled Paul in public school and began to find our way around town.
We joined the Coral Ridge Presbyterian Church, where Dot becamevery active over time. We joined the Lauderdale Yacht
Club, which seemed to be the social center of the community. After a year, we moved Paul to Pine Crest School, which lived up to its reputation as one of the country’s finest educational institutions—
and turned out to be another valuable connection point for us in the community. We became quite comfortable and enthusiastically made For Lauderdale our new home. We were to stay there for fourteen years.
From a business point of view, the next fourteen years was an unquestionable success in almost every respect. Looking back on those days, I a ttribute our business success to two basic things:
recruiting outstanding professionals and cultivating valuable relationships.
I was the newly appointed managing partner of the Fort
Lauderdale Peat Marwick office. There were two other partners —
Bob Happ and Hy Indowsky —and a total sta ff complement of twenty-four professionals. The firm’s practice was an emerging business, as contrasted to an “establishment” mainstream business.
The offices were on Sunrise Boulevard, outside the main drag of the city and its commercial center. Real estate and construction clients, bustling industries at the time in South Florida, largely dominated the clientele. Our clients were also tightly tethered to the
Jewish community, re flecting the in fluence of Howard Miller, the former managing partner, and Hy Indowsky, who were both active personalities in that segment of society.
Realizing that I needed to establish my own identity in the community, I set about to build a network. I recalled my success in
Orlando, reaching out to others with initiative and boldness. I asked a lot of questions of everyone I met and sought advice liberally. I
invited Larry Horner to lunch and sought his counsel. Larry, the future chairman of the firm, was the managing partner of the Miami office at the time. He had been incredibly successful networking as a newcomer and an “outsider” in the tight-knit Miami community.
Larry had some wonderful advice for me, much of which became a fundamental part of my relationship strategies for the balance of my career. For example, he advised me to use some of my personal funds to invest in my own personal success, as contrasted to waiting for the firm to make a move. That was something that had never explicitly occurred to me. He told me how he made a personal contribution to the University of Miami and was immediately appointed to the board of directors. I began doing that sort of thing, both financially and with my time, and I learned how people recognize and respect those personal commitments. I recently saw Larry, who is now in poor health and con fined to a wheelchair, and I thanked him again for his sage advice forty years ago. He smiled and looked pleased that
I remembered.
I also recall receiving a cold call from Ellio tt Barne tt, who managed a successful law firm in the city but was also somewhat outside the mainstream in those days. Ellio tt welcomed me to the city, invited me to lunch and o ffered to help in any way he could.
That one lunch triggered a close friendship with this brilliant and influential man, a friendship that lasted for twenty years, until he died. One ironic piece of advice from him stuck with me. He said,
“Keith, you need to steer your firm toward the mainstream business community and not be so reliant on the Jewish community. ” A
strange comment, I thought, coming from the managing partner of a
100 percent Jewish law firm. Nevertheless, I began watching his firm and saw how he was diversifying his own practice. So I set about to emulate his behavior. I absorbed and cataloged Elliott’s advice, along with all the others.
I learned a lot from these two guys and I grew up a lot in the process.
I did considerable research on the makeup and pro file of the business community and worked hard at trying to understand the demographic landscape. Admittedly, it was all a little foreign to me.
During this process, I identi fied two individuals who appeared to be among the best-connected and most respected businessmen and professionals in Broward County. They were David Rush, the owner and CEO of ACR Electronics, and Hank Watson, a well-known lawyer, banker and political hack. Both were twenty years or so older than me, and I had no connection points with either of them. Working o ff visceral intuition, I picked up the phone and called them both. I told them that I would like to meet them and to seek their counsel. I suppose they were curious about such boldness,
so each of them agreed. I laid out my situation and openly asked,
“Would you be willing to help me get established in the business community?” Both of them jumped at the opportunity and went out of their way to assist. Even today, I’m not sure why. I remember with fondness how Hank said, in our first meeting, “I’ll tell you what. My wife is chairing the Easter Lily Ball in a few weeks, and she’s short on sponsorships. If you’ll write me a $500 personal check, I’ll stand at the door and introduce you to every person who walks through it!”
So, of course I did. And he did!
These two encounters developed into cherished relationships that lasted for decades. I cannot begin to recite the ways these two men were helpful to me over the years. I tried to reciprocate from time to time, but I’m afraid I still have a dangling liability on my relationship balance sheet. And to think, it all began with a cold call to two strangers. Dave Rush passed away a few years ago. I sat somberly at his funeral at T emple Beth T orah and felt big tears streaming down my face. Hank is living at a retirement home in
Pompano Beach. Even at his advanced age, he is still wheeling and dealing among the elderly residents there. I see him from time to time at church, and he speaks to me much the way a father might address a favorite son. What a couple of guys!
Gradually, the business began to get traction in the broader Fort
Lauderdale business community. A fter five years, we moved the office to a new downtown building at 110 East Broward, just one floor below the Ruden Barne tt law firm, which relocated there at the same time. Symbolically, we all felt as if we had arrived. By that time, the City of Fort Lauderdale was a flagship audit client, along with Century Banks, a long-time Broward institution. We began working with Alamo Rent A Car, which initially was owned by the estate of John D. MacArthur. The Fort Lauderdale staff had grown to about forty professionals, and the business steadily broadened and diversified as it grew. Real estate and construction clients remained in the portfolio, but clients in government, financial services,
manufacturing, health care and service industries eventually overtook them in dominance. Elliott Barnett’s advice was spot on.
Over time, Alamo Rent A Car developed into our largest and most important client. Michael Egan and a group of investors acquired the company from MacArthur in the late 1970s, and they retained our firm as independent accountants. I first met Mike around 1979, when our firm found ourselves in a dispute with the company over some accounting matters. I was called in to help settle the dispute and ended up in heated negotiations with Mike, whom
I met for the first time in the process. That initial confrontation and ultimate settlement resulted in a mutual respect between the two of us that forged the basis for a long and wonderful relationship. As part of our deal, Mike insisted that I assume personal responsibility for their account, which I did for the next ten years. Over that time
Alamo grew from a small car rental company tucked away in South
Florida to a nationwide presence commanding more than 13 percent of the industry market share. Our services grew right along with the company. I have dozens of stories about the variety of services we performed and the company crises we helped Mike navigate.
One signi ficant event occurred in 1986, when I worked side by side with Mike as we negotiated his personal acquisition of controlling interest in the company from the Hanley Dawson family of Chicago. I worked with him every step of the way through the negotiations. T ogether, we landed on a deal valuation of $50 million.
There was a problem, however. Financing was extremely tight. Near the end, we were $3 million short on cash and it looked as if the deal was falling through. As a last ditch measure, Mike and I went over and met with Gordon Oliver, the president of C&S bank, and worked out a $3 million unsecured loan. No collateral, just personal persuasion with an old-fashioned banker who was willing to place his faith in his intuition. This was a seminal event for Mike, and the quality of my personal services in the transaction did not go unnoticed by him. He was profusely grateful.
I can’t say enough about Mike Egan as a client. He was the consummate client. He had absolute con fidence in me and rarely made an important strategic move without consulting me. He is an exceptionally bright man and was extremely demanding, keeping me on my toes constantly. But he was always willing to pay top dollar for quality service. In all those years, while other clients whined incessantly and wheedled about fees, I never discounted our standard rates to Alamo by as much as a dime. And I’m convinced that even today he feels he got his money’s worth. Interestingly, Mike and I
were never close social friends or what one might describe as buddies.
We didn’t golf, have casual lunches or socialize together. Our relationship was strictly a business one, across a desk or conference table dealing with strategic and tactical issues. When I told him I was moving to Baltimore, he expressed frustration and offered to match my compensation if I would decline. I demurred of course. So he made a counter proposal. He offered to pay me a retainer to remain on call for consultation on important matters. I accepted, and several times over the next six years I found myself absorbed in long phone calls discussing General Motors contracts, financing agreements,
legal matters or whatever. I distinctly remember one Saturday night in Baltimore when I was all dressed up in a tuxedo on my way to a black tie social event, and Mike called. I spent an hour and a half on the phone with him while Dot grumbled in the background that we were late for our event. My explanation: Mike came first.
In the mid-1980s, we opened a satellite office in West Palm
Beach and transferred a New Y ork partner, Charles Hurty, to expand our practice in that area. Around the same time, we inherited a small office and practice in Vero Beach. The Vero office had been a part of the W.O. Daley practice, merged into the newly reestablished office in Orlando (about ten years from the Orlando closing, just as I had advised Walter Hanson in 1975).
In 1987, Peat Marwick entered into a large international merger with KMG Main Hurdman, a respected Europe-based firm with a second-tier U.S. branch that did not enjoy the same respect;
the merger was strategically important for Peat Marwick’s global operations, but many of our U.S. offices suffered. I was not fond of the idea for a number of reasons. Peat Marwick chairman Larry Horner organized regional partner meetings beforehand to explain and garner support for the merger. When he announced at the Atlanta meeting that the proposed new firm name would be “KPMG Peat
Marwick” , I stood up and said that it seemed to me that “‘KPMG’
means ‘Kiss Peat Marwick Goodbye’” . And that is what happened.
“Peat Marwick” was dropped from the name and the firm became known as “KPMG” less than two years later. For years, partners teased me about my prophetic remark! At first, the merger boosted the size of our local office by about 20 percent. But Main Hurdman clients and personnel proved not up to our standards and it was not long before much of this new business evaporated.
This was a busy, stressful and productive time for me, trying to make the merger work but struggling with the quality of their people and clients. I had my hands full managing two burgeoning satellite offices, a merged contingent from Main Hurdman, and Alamo.
During our time there, Fort Lauderdale was often referred to as the fraud capital of the country. And for good reason. Inexplicably,
it seemed as if South Florida was a breeding ground for criminal behavior. And we saw more than our fair share of it. In the mid-1980s,
we were called in to investigate a fraud at an outfit called Flowerland,
which, it turned out, had bilked investors out of approximately
$40 million. With the expert skills of Don Dawn on our sta ff, we were eventually able to unravel the unbelievably complex scheme.
One Saturday morning during the same time period, a friend and board member at Commonwealth Savings and Loan in Coral
Springs approached me about doing some forensic work at their institution. I assigned Mary Wood, a senior manager on our staff, to the engagement. After about two months of digging, she managed to identify approximately $45 million of bogus investments that had been camouflaged by the chairman’s brother, who had lost it all in the bond market. I was personally involved in both the Flowerland and Commonwealth engagements, as well as in a high-pro file case with Sahlen Associates, a public company we inherited as a client with the Main Hurdman merger.
On the eve of a proposed $100 million public o ffering by
Sahlen, I was called in to help resolve some problems. Meeting with the Sahlen executives in our conference room, I sni ffed out that something serious was wrong. I began grilling them. The more questions I asked, the weaker their answers were. I finally turned to
Harold Sahlen. It was bold of me, but I said, “Harold, I think you’re lying to me. I’m going to ask you to leave my office, and I should tell you that I’m placing a call to the chairman of your board to report my suspicions. ” My partners cringed; they were afraid of what would happen and were probably embarrassed by the sharpness of my accusation. Harold was stunned, but obediently left, and I made the call. I was right. The entire company was a wholesale fraud and soon became totally unraveled. The next day, I met with Richard
Rainwater of the Bass Brothers, major investors in Sahlen, laid out the issues and told him how their entire board had been duped.
Harold Sahlen was later convicted of securities fraud and sentenced to a federal penitentiary. Thank God for my intuition that day and for the courage to act on it. Earlier in my career, Bill Norton had o ften counseled me to do what was right, even in the face of adversity and opposition. He would say, “Cobb, sometimes adversities set in, and you just have to face into the wind. ” I think he was looking over my shoulder that day.
One other high-pro file fraud to hit Fort Lauderdale was the International Gold Bullion Exchange, an alleged gold trader operated by two brothers named Alderdice. They came on the scene with a splash, leasing three floors in our office building and placing a huge, gaudy sign on the roof. They were throwing around money as if it was chump change —political and charitable contributions,
lavish parties and receptions, beautiful women, full-page ads in
The Wall Street Journal, etc. Bill Alderdice, the other brother —who had formerly been a hair stylist and costume jewelry peddler, of all things —came to visit me, requesting that we sign on as their auditors, but it was clear to me that he wasn’t looking for auditors.
He was looking for respectability and wanted to use our name to establish their credentials in the world. I declined, on the basis that I
didn’t really understand their business. (I didn’t.) He left, but came back a second time, this time begging us to take them on as a client.
He offered me $50,000 as a non-refundable retainer, indicating he could make the check out to me personally. I again declined his offer.
Needless to say, the company washed up in a few months. It was a highly publicized $75 million fraud. All of the purported gold bars in their o ften-photographed vault turned out to be wooden blocks painted gold! Bill and Jim Alderdice went to jail, and Bill was later murdered. Thank God, once again, for all that I had learned from the frauds that came before this one. I wrote a song about the folly and,
in celebration of our good fortune, had it performed at company outings. It was called “Has Anybody Seen My Gold” to the tune of
“Five Foot Two, Eyes Are Blue” .
There were dozens of other bizarre incidents in which I was involved one way or another, although some were not as spectacular or public, and I remember every one of them with a twinge of nostalgia. These episodes honed my skills as a professional executive.
Not only did I mature as an auditor; I acquired some valuable skills in judgment, discernment, ethics and communication.
Relationships were the foundation of our success in Fort
Lauderdale. We gave careful a ttention to constantly expanding and cultivating our relationships. Dot and I were both interested in community service, and found ourselves immersed in a variety of local organizations. We enjoyed people and always felt a sincere obligation to be an integral part of the communities where we lived. Being part of community organizations also led inevitably to new and meaningful relationships over time. So, much of our lives outside of the family were centered around the Coral Ridge
Presbyterian Church, Pine Crest School, Lauderdale Yacht Club and a host of community service organizations. We served on the boards of a number of organizations and were frequent volunteers,
if not their leaders, at all sorts of events. At varying times during these years I served as chairman of the Broward Public Library
Foundation, the Museum of Science and Discovery, the United Way and the Community Foundation. Most of my business relationships and many of my closest friends today are traceable directly to these organizations.
I knew from my experience that the success of a professional practice is highly dependent on quality people. Accordingly, we spent a significant amount of time recruiting and cultivating thevery best accountants I could find. I made treks to Florida State University,
the University of Florida and Florida Atlantic University and personally interviewed the top accounting students. As our practice grew faster than our training programs, I also scouted the firm for quality transfers. I put a full-court press on the best people and lured them into our fold. That involved hard work, persistence and a great deal of persuasion. I occasionally overpaid, I am sure, but in the end it was clearly worth it and more. Ours was a superb cadre, and our clients and the business community knew it. The pristine quality of our sta ff is evidenced today by the evolution of their outstanding careers twenty-five years later. Dozens of them are senior financial executives today and many of them have become superstars in a wide array of businesses. Several of them became partners in KPMG. As I
reflect on those days, I think about Hy Indowsky, Tim Gupton, Larry
King, Rosalie Arthur, Bob Liszewski, Mary Wood, T om Kester, Jill
Vales, Dick Norwalk, Don Dawn, Gene Ballard, T erry Kelley, and on and on. Interestingly, several of these outstanding people, including
Rosalie, Mary and Jill, were picked o ff by Mike Egan at Alamo and
Certified T ours and showed up there years later, when I became the CEO. These are all exceptional people, and I look back on our time together with an enormous sense of pride. And with extreme gratitude. As a group, they were the cornerstones of my own success in Fort Lauderdale. I am truly appreciative of their service. I still stay in touch with a number of them and value their friendship.
During the mid-1980s, I developed a wonderful and lasting friendship with Jim Brocksmith, who was serving as managing partner of the Miami office. Jim and I were “birds of a feather” of sorts, both young emerging leaders in the firm and both harboring similar intellects and value systems. We bonded in those days and worked closely together in the South Florida marketplace. Jim later went on to become deputy chairman and chief operating officer of
KPMG. Our friendship continued after we both le ft South Florida,
and I thoroughly enjoyed working directly with him during my last years with the firm.
From 1975 to 1989, when Dot and I moved to Baltimore, our practice grew five-fold, from twenty-four people at the outset to a full staff complement of 125 professionals. By the end of the 1980s,
we were widely recognized as the “go to” firm in South Florida.
We lapped all the other established firms and actually vanquished
Coopers & Lybrand, which closed its Fort Lauderdale office due to the atrophy of its practice. Our only really strong competitor was Arthur Andersen. Theirs was a quality, well-managed firm that handled all of Wayne Huizenga’s businesses, including Blockbuster
Video. Wayne, a high-profile serial entrepreneur, was solidly in the
Andersen camp. But then I had Mike Egan and Alamo Rent A Car in the KPMG camp. Over a ten-year period ending in 1989, the annual fees billed to Alamo grew from about $30,000 to $1.3 million. That was a huge client for an office our size in that day, and it ranked among KPMG’s largest clients nationally. In the midst of this growth and development, we were also consistently profitable.
KPMG recognized our strong performance, and my personal stature in the firm rose along with it. But after running the Fort
Lauderdale practice as managing partner for fourteen years, it was time for a change —for the firm and for me. Harold Stokes, the regional partner at the time, came down for a visit in early 1989.
During the visit, he casually mentioned some issues he was seeing in Baltimore. Reading between the lines was not di fficult; his comments were not idle gossip and were not accidental. Two weeks later, I booked a flight, and Dot and I paid a weekend visit to the city of Baltimore, just to look around. We had never been there before.
We liked what we saw. Not unexpectedly, Harold showed up again about a month later and advanced the proposition that I transfer to
Baltimore as managing partner of the office. Having been favorably impressed with the city already, Dot and I said, “Let’s go for it!” So off we were—saying goodbye to friends and associates, and moving on to a new adventure.

## Principles

- Proactively seek counsel and ask questions to learn the local landscape.
- Invest personal resources—time and money—to build credibility and obtain leadership positions.
- Recruit and retain top talent as the foundation of a successful professional practice.
- When you don't understand a prospective client's business, decline the engagement to protect integrity.
- Act on ethical intuition promptly, even if it risks discomfort with peers.

## Decision heuristics

- When new to a community, directly solicit advice from well-connected locals and offer time or money to demonstrate commitment.
- If a client relationship requires trust, accept personal accountability to build deeper, strategic bonds.
- When answers to probing questions are weak and intuition signals deception, confront directly and escalate to governance.
- Prefer declining engagements you cannot reasonably understand over accepting suspicious-feeling retainers.
- Use community service and board participation to create durable professional networks.
