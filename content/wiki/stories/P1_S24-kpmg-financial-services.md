# KPMG Financial Services

> Keith Cobb was appointed to lead KPMG's newly reorganized Financial Services unit, faced partner skepticism due to his non-specialist background, mounted a highly prepared, consultant-backed presentation at a national meeting to establish credibility and drive adoption of a five-point strategy, and later accepted an external CEO offer from Alamo.

## Full Text

KPMG
Financial Services
The KPMG reorganization in 1993 was a courageous and massive undertaking. Jon Madonna and Jim Brocksmith reasoned that the firm should be more closely aligned with the structure of the modern business world, which they believed was skewed toward and identified with industry segments. They believed it made more sense to organize our firm around our clients’ businesses, not our partners’ individual skills and geographical domiciles. Accordingly,
they convinced the management committee to completely overhaul the organizational structure around five major industry segments.
The segments identi fied were: Financial Services, Manufacturing and Commercial, Health Care, High T echnology and Government.
All clients of the firm and all professionals in the firm were assigned to one of these business units.
National managing partners were appointed for each segment and were given broad authority to establish their own national management structures and teams. These moves were a dramatic shock to the system. The firm had operated for ninety-five years with local practices structured along geographic lines and three rigid silos for audit, tax and management consulting professionals. Outside the management committee, the restructuring was not a particularly popular move. It upset apple carts and fiefdoms. However, even aside from the wisdom of the new organization, change simply for change’s sake was probably a good thing for the firm at that stage in its history.
Organizing the financial services business unit was much like starting a new business from scratch. I established ten regions across the United States and appointed an area partner to oversee the service delivery and practice administration in each region. I
also appointed five sub-industry leaders to coordinate all of the specialized services nationally —one each for banking, insurance,
mutual funds, investment banking and real estate. Our leadership team was therefore comprised of sixteen partners, including myself.
Beyond this group, the organization was essentially flat. I had never worked with most of these team members; in fact, I had never even met a few of them. My appointments were determined largely by due diligence on the backgrounds and reputations of the most highly respected partners with financial services expertise, bu ttressed by consultations with people I respected. As it turned out, they were excellent choices for the most part.
Before many weeks into the new organizational structure, I
became aware of a looming issue. As mentioned, the reorganization was disruptive and unpopular in many circles, inasmuch as it le ft many partners uncomfortable and generally thirsty for things the way they had always been. Accordingly, there was grumbling and second-guessing coming from all corners. One set of questions that
I began to hear was roughly, “Who is Keith Cobb and how on earth did he get chosen for this role? What does he know about financial services? Why didn’t they pick one of us —real financial service guys?” These questions were originating among mature partners who had spent their entire careers in bank auditing, the insurance practice, taxation for financial companies, etc., much more highly specialized and focused than my generic background. I soon realized that I had to dispel this rumbling if I was to lead the group of four thousand professionals. It was a problem that had to be addressed without delay.
The truth of the ma tter was that they had a point. In a sense, it did seem a bit odd. Someone without a recognized background in financial services would lead a completely restructured organization built around specialized industry expertise. (I had served a few bank clients over my career, but I was by no means an expert in the field.)
On the other hand, conversion of the historical structure into a totally new structure and culture was a monumental undertaking,
and I think Jon Madonna and Jim Brocksmith decided to turn to someone they trusted —someone with extensive experience in cultural turnarounds and managing complex environments. At least,
that’s how I rationalized it. Jon and Jim also knew that I was a quick study and that I would pour myself into the conversion.
Roughly three months into the new organization, we scheduled a meeting in Orlando of the four hundred partners assigned to financial services. The purpose of the meeting was organizational in nature: to review the overall strategy for the new business unit and begin building teamwork and camaraderie. I decided this was the pivotal place for me to make my move to ameliorate the hazards posed by all of the doubtful Thomases. I had to do it! This meeting was to be the turning point in the transformation of the financial services practice. I was determined to firmly establish my leadership and drive the narrative and goals of this elite cadre of professionals.
So, I threw my entire energy into the Orlando meeting. I met with several people in our executive offices in New Y ork, looking for references for outside experts to assist me. I was fortunate to meet Marc Gerstein, who would prove tremendously helpful in the design of an overarching strategy for our newly reorganized practice.
Marc was a brilliant man, nothing short of a genius, and I benefitted enormously from his consultation. He was a visiting scholar at MIT
and a recognized thought leader in the field of organizational design of complex enterprises. Working with him wasn’t easy. In fact, it was miserable. But it was fruitful. He challenged me to think hard about our objectives and push aside all of my preconceptions about the public accounting business. He also forced me to wrap my head around something beyond the immediate issues I was facing. With his counsel, we built a cohesive business strategy based on a five-point plan that we believed would be competitive and pro fitable for KPMG —and could be understood and used to advantage by our professionals. The plan centered on harnessing the full range of resources and expertise within KPMG and bringing that skill and experience to bear on our client base. Historically, client service was too o ften extremely provincial, with local partners reluctant to call on the deep experience of the firm in their zeal to protect their local office and departmental revenues. As a result, clients were o ften underserved. Moreover, as too many legal cases had demonstrated, the failure to bring quali fied expertise to bear on client issues exposed the firm to greater risk. Our strategy stripped out the provincial incentives and provided motivation for partners to reach out beyond their immediate environments for specialized expertise. My five-point plan was constructed around the following broad concepts:
• Understand that superior client service is everything,
designing service teams based on specialized skill sets,
irrespective of geographical locations.
• Identify risk management as a central service theme,
recognizing that risk was a critical emerging major issue facing the entire financial service industry.
• Identify companies that are the industry market leaders and concentrate a ttention on them, introducing them to the full menu of KPMG financial service capabilities.
• Build an internal knowledge management system to harness resources and skills, with all professionals capable of accessing the database.
• Allocate KPMG talent effectively and efficiently, with the best talent assigned to the most important clients and opportunities, wherever they might be located.
This planning experience was a bit of an epiphany for me. I had taken great pride in the multi-tasking skills I had developed over thirty years and in my ability to pivot and face situations squarely when they arose. These a ttributes were part of my identity —my
DNA. Marc was able to convince me that these tactical skills were nice to have, but insu fficient to accomplish the transformation I
faced. It was an extremely valuable teaching, and I am profoundly grateful for the personal transformation he spawned for me.
After developing our strategy and vetting it with our leadership team, I turned to my personal presentation at the Orlando meeting.
Our public relations firm in New Y ork recommended I consult with
Myles Martel, another jewel of a find. Myles was, and is, a premier communication consultant. He has few equals in the world. He has written several books on public speaking and interviews, and his client list reads like a Who’s Who of American business and politics.
In fact, he had been Ronald Reagan’s debate coach during his candidacy for president. Ironically, he had a studio near my home in Philadelphia, which made it convenient to visit with him. Myles digested my strategy and cast his magic on the key points, cra fting shibboleths, tag lines and descriptive language that I would never in a million years have come up with. I spent hours in his studio standing behind his presidential-like podium, practicing delivery of our strategy with his stylistic twists and turns. Myles was good!
Expensive—but really, really good! He remains one of my special friends to this day.
I then turned to the presentation itself. Based on a referral,
I engaged Kathleen Hessert as a presentation consultant.
Following a career as a television anchor and talk show host,
Kathleen had launched a highly successful consultancy focused on communications and media relations primarily for high-pro file athletes. She was a world-class expert on communication skills and personal branding. She represented numerous recognizable well-known athletic figures. Before working with her, I thought I was fairly good at public presentations, but I certainly learned differently.
She drilled and drilled me in techniques and fundamentals. She was tough! I practiced my entire presentation a dozen or more times,
with her videotaping it and following up with harsh criticism and detailed recommendations for improvement. She was tireless and relentless. She edited the slides I had prepared as visual aids. She set up the stage, down to footer monitors and placement of Xs where I
should stand. She insisted that I speak without a podium and use no notes. She even told me what to wear.
Armed with the advice and wise counsel of Marc, Myles and
Kathleen, buttressed by my own thirty years of professional training at KPMG, I stood on the stage on that first morning in Orlando in front of four hundred of the best financial services professionals in the world and delivered my strategic platform for their business. “ A
Whole New World” from the film Aladdin (chosen by Kathleen,
of course) played in the background as the crowd assembled and I
strode to the stage. I made a flawless forty-minute presentation with no notes and no podium. Visual aids were shown on a large screen behind me. Invisible to the audience, I had television footers in front of me with the slides reproduced, so I never once had to look back at the screen. Everything went perfectly.
Thanks to all of the detailed preparation and practice, the presentation was an unequivocal success. I received all sorts of compliments and plaudits. More importantly, my critics were immediately silenced. It was as if someone flipped a switch and everyone sat up straight in their chairs. The entire mood of the
Orlando conference changed, essentially on the spot. Partners were excited about the strategy and positive about the direction the firm was going. This one event became a case study for me about how exhaustive preparation and e ffective communications can positively alter the course of a business. Following this conference,
the partner pushback stopped in its tracks. There were only a few very minor exceptions from a handful of partners who felt they had been passed over in the organization reshu ffling. T o the contrary,
our leading partners called on me regularly and invited me to meet with their major clients and to advise them on client service matters.
Magically, our strategy began to unfold across the country. T ruly, it was an amazing turnaround in a short period of time. I am extremely grateful for the valuable lessons learned from this experience.
Financial services was the largest of the five new business units, comprising approximately 40 percent of KPMG’s revenues and served by over four thousand specialized financial services professionals. It consolidated all of the nationwide audit, tax and management consulting practices for banks, insurance companies,
investment banking firms, mutual funds and real estate clients.
KPMG was the recognized financial services leader among accounting firms in the United States. We owned overwhelmingly dominant practices in the banking and insurance industry. We audited approximately 30 percent of the nation’s eleven thousand banks and about 20 percent of the insurance companies. Our stellar client list included such names as Citicorp, First Union, Norwest,
Fleet Bank, Wells Fargo, Liberty Mutual, Nationwide Insurance,
GE Capital, Kidder Peabody, Fannie Mae and hundreds of other household names.
Over the first year, I managed to meet the CEOs of most of our major clients. My schedule was crazy. I remember flying once to San
Francisco to have breakfast with Carl Reichardt, chairman of Wells
Fargo, to discuss a merger with First Interstate Bancorp they were considering and then flying directly to Boston for dinner with Gary
Countryman, chairman of Liberty Mutual. I of course continued my service on the KPMG Management Committee, which met monthly.
I met with my financial services team regularly at various locations around the country and travelled extensively for client introductions and visits. And then there were the isolated problems to be solved at various times all over the country. When people asked me where I
lived, I would jokingly reply, “Seat 4B” . I actually continued to live in
Philadelphia. I had an office in New Y ork, but I kept my main office in
Philadelphia. When not in the air, I shuffled back and forth between
Philadelphia, New Y ork and Washington D.C., to the point that I had all of the train schedules memorized. Actually, I found respite on those train rides; it was a great time to coalesce my thoughts and regroup, following harrowing, hectic days. A few years ago, a friend asked how long I lived in Philadelphia, to which I replied, “Four years. ” Dot chimed in from the sidelines and said, “That’s not true. I
was there four years; you were there only two!”
I moved downstairs from the Philadelphia operating office at 1600 Market Street and put together a small national administrative team of five or six people. These people turned out to be a phenomenal support group. I recruited David Berkowitz from the senior manager ranks of the Philadelphia office to serve as a professional aide for me. David was terri fic! He was smart as a whip and had a work ethic seldom seen in people at his level. Over a period of two years, he became a virtual internal alter ego for me, exercising an extremely mature understanding of our business model and working incessantly to stay a step ahead of me. David later went to Columbia University for his MBA, worked a while with Merrill Lynch and is now one of the top senior executives at
Lincoln Financial Group. I love this guy and have enjoyed watching him ascend in the corporate world. Other sta ff members handled my scheduling, financial reporting, practice communications,
marketing and general practice management functions.
My national leadership team turned out to be an excellent group of professionals. Bill Hannon led the New Y ork group and was the consummate major client professional. He had a close relationship with Sandy Weill of Citicorp and was respected in other financial services circles, as well as by the New Y ork partners and sta ff. Gene O’Kelly led the northwestern U.S. practice from
San Francisco. Gene was one of the brightest professionals I ever worked with, and he brought a uniquely broad perspective to his work. Not surprisingly, he later became chairman of KPMG. I
moved Tim Bentsen from T exas to Atlanta to lead the Southeast practice. He was outstanding. And so it went. I did struggle with a few individuals on the team. The management consulting segment of the firm had always operated in a virtually autonomous style,
and I experienced some di fficulty bringing these professionals into line with the new strategy and structure. But eventually they came around. Also, the real estate partners had never considered themselves financial service people; they saw themselves as a different breed and perhaps misplaced in this business unit. But they, too, eventually signed up for the program. On balance, it was an exceptionally talented and capable group of professionals.
My fast-paced schedule and deep engagement in KPMG’s
Financial Services practice was soon to be overcome by a new surprise. I was in New Y ork one Monday in February 1995 when my phone rang. The voice on the other end said, “Keith, this is Mike
Egan. I need to talk with you. ” He told me that Alamo Rent A Car was having enormous financial difficulties, having lost $50 million in
1994, and he had come to the conclusion that he needed a CEO to take over the business. He said he was thinking of me and wanted to come to New Y ork the next day and discuss a proposition with me. I
agreed, not exactly sure where this was leading. I met with him at the
Four Seasons hotel early the next morning. We spent five or six hours discussing the company’s plight, and he offered me the position of vice chairman and chief executive officer of Alamo. The o ffer was informal, with several loose ends and details le ft open on both sides. I told him I would have to talk with Jon Madonna, KPMG’s chairman, before I made any decision, but agreed to go down to Fort
Lauderdale and continue our discussion in a couple of days.
I left that meeting at the Four Seasons, went down the elevator,
across the street and straight into another elevator that went up to
Jon’s office on the forty-seventh floor of the General Motors building.
I can’t begin to describe the overwhelming emotions on those two long elevator rides. I spent the rest of that day talking with Jon. At the end of our meeting, he said, “Keith, as important as you are to
KPMG and our strategy, I really don’t see how you can turn this down. It’s an incredible opportunity—one that no KPMG partner ever sees!” So, I booked a flight to Fort Lauderdale and, before the end of the week, I had signed a formal contract with Alamo and tendered my resignation from KPMG after a thirty-two-year career there.
