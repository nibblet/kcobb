Frances Cobb—A Spool of Blue Thread
The hill country of Lafaye tte and Pontotoc counties in north
Mississippi was the 1921 birthplace and childhood home of
Mary Frances Clements, one of seven children of Jim and Beulah
Clements. The family was relatively poor, with Jim working in sawmills and farming at various times, struggling to provide for the family and make ends meet. Frances struggled through those years,
trying to make the best of her environment and plight, but at the same time yearning for something better.
After finishing high school at rural T occopola, she set up shop as a beautician in Sarepta, a small community about fifteen miles to the south. Along the way, she met Bayne Cobb, from nearby Banner, and married him in 1940. Bayne and Frances moved to Calhoun City,
where Bayne was working as a truck driver for the Justice Company.
Her entire life took place within a radius of about thirty miles; for the next sixty-one years, it took place mostly on one parcel of property in Calhoun City.
Mother was exceptionally bright and industrious, and when
Sandy and I werevery young, she poured all her energy into our care.
Then, when we were in elementary school, she began to find ways to help supplement the family income and still be home to take care of us. An excellent seamstress, she launched a home-based business as a seamstress. Before long, she was the go-to seamstress for making and tailoring clothing, taking on some of the most di fficult sewing projects in the city.
When Sandy and I were not in school, Mother enlisted us as helpers. I was probably nine or ten years old when she taught me how to match colors and regularly sent me uptown to the Ben
Franklin store with fabric samples to buy matching spools of thread.
I remember one time when she was trying to produce a contrasting seam and didn’t have a color sample. She sent me to the store with a Noxzema container to try to match its dark blue color. If I
did not secure a perfect match, I was immediately sent back to the
Ben Franklin store to get it right. Since the walk there and back was about a mile, I learned fairly quickly. Over time, we accumulated a large inventory of sewing thread, almost as many shades as the store’s inventory. I constructed a couple of racks with finishing nails to accommodate the spools, and regularly kept them organized by color. Using a special tool she purchased, I took over the production of the custom-covered bu ttons and belt buckles that accompanied some of the dresses and other articles of clothing that she made. I
also learned to cut fabric according to the complicated-looking
Simplicity and Butterick patterns; she coached me on how to arrange the pa tterns so as to follow the fabric’s grain and pa ttern without wasting anything. She was exacting. I was expected to be the same.
I can close my eyes and in my mind see Mother working over the Singer sewing machine as it clattered away. Because her eyesight was poor, she would tilt her head to the right, lean over the machine to get as close as she could to the fast-moving needle, and carefully
Frances Cobb, industrious mom push the fabric through. She was always intense at her work, as she was in practically everything. I anxiously listened to the racket made by the sewing machine and watched as the garments took shape.
When the ladies came to the house and oohed and aahed about their new, tailored or repaired garments, I would sit on the sidelines, proud of the work that had been done. One year, she made all the majorette uniforms for the high school band. I was especially fascinated and interested when the girls came over to try those on! Y eah, I peeped!
Same thing for the many fancy evening gowns she made.
When I was about fifteen, Mother began working outside the home. Her first job was at Lunceford’s Bargain Store, south of town,
where she worked six days a week for several years. I worked there with her on Saturdays and during the summer for three years. Her wages were modest, her hours were long and the work was hard. She moved from there to Chandler’s Drug Store on the square, where she was a store clerk and had be tter hours. After several years there,
she was a secretary at Beasley Insurance Agency for several years.
She was diligent at all these jobs and considered each one a step up from the previous one. She was enterprising, incredibly industrious and conscientious all her life.
However, she seemed embarrassed about having to work outside of the home, this being an era when the ideal wife and mother stayed home and devoted herself to family. She was insecure in many ways and seemed to su ffer from a sort of persecution complex and a feeling that she had been denied the life she deserved. She repeated incessantly—it was like a drumbeat—that she had always wanted an education but couldn’t afford it. She was also ambitious and resented that she could not a fford the finer things in life. She was driven to prove her worth to herself and to those around her.
One example was her quest for a new home in the early 1950s.
We lived at the time in a five-room frame house that Daddy had acquired in lieu of a bonus just before she married him. Mother wanted a big brick house with all the amenities. She marveled at the finer homes in town —the Mounger house, the Clint Hardin home, the T .J. Mallory place. They were new and modern, and she desperately wanted a house like one of them. She had an obsession about elevating her status and saw an expensive new house as a status symbol. The ne ttlesome issue naturally was the finances. Daddy’s salary was modest and her income was meager.
I remember si tting on the sofa while she labored for hours over books of floor plans, searching for a solution to the quandary of how to build a big new house with very little money. She finally compromised on something financially reasonable, surrendering the desired brick exterior, air conditioning, enclosed garage and some other features.
Mother’s health was a constant issue for as long as I can remember. It seemed as though she had incessant ailments, literally from head to toe. She underwent numerous surgeries—eye surgery to correct vision problems, a hysterectomy in her early thirties,
stomach surgery, an appendectomy, hip replacements and perhaps others that I don’t recall. She took medications for illnesses, some of them real and some of them perhaps imagined, and became dependent on prescription drugs. Eventually, she resorted to doctor-shopping in order to obtain prescriptions for all of her medications.
Although there is no doubt that she was not a healthy person, I
always felt that the emotional stress that she inflicted on herself was a strong contributor to her ailments. I see her now as a victim of her own mental or emotional disorders. There is also no doubt that drugs and stress dulled her mind and acuity as the years passed. The sharp, intelligent person she was as a younger woman atrophied over the years. It was sad to watch.
When Sandy and I were children, Mother was a strict disciplinarian. From as early as I can remember, she watched over us like a hawk, demanding that we behave and perform up to her towering standards. The slightest misstep was met with swi ft and sure punishment. I remember her sending me out to the willow tree in our yard numerous times to clip my own switch, and then whipping me with it until huge whelps bulged on my legs. Usually,
the instrument of choice was a belt, a fate far worse than the switches.
Mother believed that I should focus completely on academics,
music and working. I loved sports, but she nixed my participation in athletics. Even though I was a dutiful son, this was too much for me. I remember slipping o ff and joining a local baseball team when I was about twelve years old. One time, I was playing second base in a game at Vardaman and made an unassisted triple play, a rare and extremely lucky phenomenon one could only dream of experiencing in a lifetime. There have only been a few such plays in the history of the Major Leagues. I caught a line drive, stepped on the bag at second base, then ran down and tagged the runner from first base. I was walking on air when my ride dropped me off at home,
only to face my Mother. Mother was livid that I had disobeyed her proscription against participating in sports. Punishment was swi ft and intense, as usual. I never told her about the triple play; she would not have been impressed in the least. Over time, I managed to accumulate an incredible baseball card collection that included many famous players and the entire St. Louis Cardinals team. I
spent many happy hours trading cards, analyzing my holdings and reading about the players. Alas, Mother threw them all away when
I was away at college. In her mind, they were a worthless waste of time and space. I was distraught. T oday that collection would be valuable.
Mother was also a taskmaster when it came to chores. As far back as I can remember, in addition to being a seamstress helper, I
had chores and household responsibilities. Besides assisting Mother with wallpapering, painting and other household projects, I worked in the garden, raised chickens, kept the yard mowed and all sorts of other things. Dinner was my responsibility nearly every day during my teenage years and I learned to cook most everything —
vegetables, meats, corn bread, cobbler pies, you name it. I washed clothes and did most of the ironing; back then almost everything was ironed, including jeans, work pants and even the sheets and pillowcases. Mother directed all these activities. She taught me how to do everything and then supervised me to be sure I did them perfectly. Of course, I was never perfect, but I certainly learned a lot in the process.
She made sure we lived in a beehive of activity. There was never a dull moment. As a matter of fact, I do not remember a single instance where I was not hustling to get everything done. I have a mental file cabinet full of memories of school projects, homework, church programs, birthday parties, Cub Scout programs, music lessons,
choirs, dancing lessons, band practice, summer camps, as well as the chores and never-ending jobs—all engineered by Mother. I cannot even begin to describe all the things she taught me and all of the things she had me do, all of the environments she created to ensure that I would learn as much as possible.
I have o ften wondered about her motivation, however. Was it because of her deep love for me? Was it some destiny she envisioned for me? Was it to enhance her own sense of self-worth—and bragging rights? Was she trying to achieve honors and recognition through me? I just know that she was obsessed with me being perfect and was continually disappointed with my imperfect performance.
Unfortunately, there were too many times when I was imperfect and disappointed her. I wasn’t tall enough to reach her high standards no matter how hard I tried. T oo often, it seemed that I could never measure up. Nothing was good enough, no matter how satisfactory my achievements seemed to me. Being selected for a prominent role in the school musical wasn’t enough; it wasn’t the lead role. Being one of nine clarinetists for the All-State Band was not good enough;
I wasn’t first chair. Achieving the rank of Eagle Scout wasn’t cause for celebration; it took me too long. In my first year of college, when I
was awarded a trophy and received widespread praise for achieving the highest academic grade point average among 970 students in our class, I called home to announce the news to my parents. Mother brusquely responded that this was “interesting” and then abruptly changed the subject to begin bitterly complaining about the money
I was spending.
These experiences and others seared me deeply, and I have only recently begun to see them in perspective. Re flecting on my life, I
have become increasingly aware of my mother’s enormous influence on me, positive as well as negative —the realization that there were many positive influences has settled in. I am indebted to her for much of my education, both formal and informal, my strong work ethic,
my fundamental value system and ethical makeup. I am indebted to her for instilling self-discipline in me. Despite all the demons in her head, she paid close attention to her children and went to enormous lengths to motivate and guide us. I shall be forever grateful for all of this.
I feel the need to add that Mother also contributed to some characteristics of mine that might or might not be so noble. For example, I have often felt that my persona was inexorably hardened by having to deal over the years with her fundamental unhappiness and unrest.
Perhaps there is something I could have done to relieve her bitterness, pain and suffering, but I was never able to figure out the formula. I truly wish I had been able to do that. It was sad to witness her insecurity and feelings of persecution, and to see her suffer from so much physical and psychological distress. We all loved her and wanted her to be happy, but we honestly did not know how to help.
Mother lived to be eighty years old, but the yearning and hunger she experienced as a young woman never seemed to subside. If anything,
it worsened with time.
I recently read a novel entitled A Spool of Blue Thread by Anne
Tyler. It is a poignant and insightful story, sensitively written, about the inner workings of a complex Baltimore family. The family drama reminded me so much of my mother and my childhood that I
decided to plagiarize the title for this chapter. It seemed ironically congruous, even though the Ben Franklin store isn’t mentioned.
In September of 2001, Sandy and I stood together beside
Mother’s casket and commented to each other that she looked peaceful for the first time that we could ever remember—the change in her face was miraculous. That powerful thought made the normal grief a little more bearable. The idiomatic phrase “rest in peace” was certainly appropriate for Frances Cobb.
