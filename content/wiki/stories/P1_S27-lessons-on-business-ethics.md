## Full Text

Lessons on
Business Ethics
For some inexplicable reason, I have seen more than my fair share of fraud and corruption over the course of my career. Of course, the public accounting profession naturally exposes one to more than normal, but I somehow seem to have stumbled upon far more than most.
Fortunately, I was raised in an environment where the difference between right and wrong was clear. My parents, my school, my church, the Boy Scouts—all of them supported an admirable set of values. Again, fortunately, and unlike so many people, I never found myself in critical straits where I was severely tempted to breach the values I had been taught.
I provide this background not to imply that my values are better than average or be tter than anyone else’s for that ma tter. But I do believe that the background described keenly sensitized me to the mores of society and may have caused me to draw a brighter line when observing the behavior of others.
Also, the accounting profession trains one to be skeptical and vigilant. In fact, that’s what auditors really do: evaluate the integrity of financial information.
My observations on business ethics deal primarily with financial matters, inasmuch as that is the world in which I have mostly dwelled.
I have been less involved with behavioral standards and ethics in the areas of human resources, sales and marketing, production and other aspects of business. The following anecdotes illustrate some of the fraud and corruption that I have seen and had personal involvement with. Several are stories of cunning and ingenuity. Some are stories of desperation. Others are, I think, merely stories about people with fundamental character flaws.
My earliest recollection of an embezzlement dates back to the
1960s, when I was about twenty-four years old working for Peat
Marwick in Mississippi. One of my clients was a physicians group,
and I got a call one day from one of the doctors, Dr. Raymond Martin,
asking me to come out and take a look at things. He was concerned because the bank had been calling him about overdrafts. I went to his office and began reviewing the records with his bookkeeper, Billie
Pullen. There were some easily discernable discrepancies in the bank deposits. I asked Billie if she knew what might be wrong, and she said, “Sure, I know. ” Thinking she was going to rat on a nurse or someone else, I said, “Well, Billie, if you know, I think you should tell
Dr. Martin or me so we can get to the bo ttom of it. ” She looked me squarely in the eye, not flinching a muscle, and said, “Oh, he knows
I took the money —or he wouldn’t have you out here checking my books!” I was unsure how to react. As it turned out, she had stolen about $6,000. Dr. Martin called the police and they hauled her o ff to jail that morning. I was struck by the boldness of her actions,
and how calm and nonchalant she was about it. Apparently, her husband was not, because a newspaper article a couple of weeks later said that he had commi tted suicide out of humiliation and family embarrassment. We could never determine this woman’s motivations. Her family apparently was not suffering from financial pressure or hardship. Did she take the money because it was there and because she could? This unanswered question has haunted me for forty years. One thing the experience did for me was to set the stage for me to realize early on that fraud and corruption are real and one should be alert for it.
A couple of years later, I discovered another embezzlement.
This one was at Jackson Packing Company. While reconciling the bank accounts used for livestock purchases and flipping through a stack of cancelled checks, I happened to notice the recurring signature of Ham Stocke tt, the livestock buyer, as a secondary endorser on a number of checks. Curious, I went over and asked
Ham about it. He provided a perfectly logical explanation—at least it sounded perfectly logical at first. But I continued to have a nagging feeling, a sixth sense, that something was awry, so I flowcharted the number and weight of the cattle, from the buying lot to the kill floor,
for a month. I tried to reconcile the ca ttle yard purchases with the slaughterhouse numbers. Lo and behold, I discovered an elaborate scheme where the ca ttle buyer was manipulating weights and numbers and embezzling funds, something he had apparently been doing for years. I reported my findings to Ernie Jenkins, the ultra-tough-guy president of the company, who snorted, “Let’s get that bastard over here and see what he has to say!” Stocke tt arrived in a few minutes, confessed and was fired on the spot. Jenkins gave me no credit for uncovering the fraud; he just asked why it took me so long to find it! This was an early lesson about the old adage that “no good deed goes unpunished. ”
Along about the same time frame, I was auditing Alvis &
Company, a small municipal bond dealer in Jackson. I knew nothing about the investment business and found bond trading to be quite complicated. Julian Noel, a career manager at Peat Marwick, came to the office to provide some tutelage. While there, he picked up a stack of cancelled checks and thumbed through them. Suddenly he said,
“Hmmm, these checks feel funny. ” Upon close scrutiny, we observed that several checks were a bit shorter than the others by about a quarter of an inch. A fter digging around for some time, including ordering duplicates from the bank’s microfiche files, we uncovered another intricate scheme. The bookkeeper was preparing bogus checks with a customer and herself as joint payees (a normal practice in that trade). She would then cash the check herself, and when the bank statement and the processed check was returned (we still used paper at that time!), she would clip o ff her tiny endorsement on the back of the cancelled checks and forge the customer’s name as the endorser. This lady had pilfered a lot of money over a period of several years. Had it not been for Julian’s uncanny instincts and years of experience, I would never have noticed this embezzlement.
There were several other instances in those early years, each with its own set of peculiarities, but with a common thread of irregularity or dishonesty. I saw dummies on payrolls, employee self-dealing,
customer/supplier kickbacks, tax dodging and abuses in all shapes and forms. Sometimes the picture was clear, sometimes fuzzy.
Murray Underwood and I came across a $410 shortage in a surprise cash count at the Fine Brothers Madison department stores one year. The cashier’s name was Nora Cook and she had worked at the store for forty-six years. She was a very nice lady, so Murray and I went back later and recounted her fund, just to be sure. The result was the same, so we had no choice but to report the shortage to the president, Harold Madison, who called her in immediately.
Mrs. Cook had no explanation, so Madison fired her without any further investigation. I calculated that this shortage amounted to about $9 for every year this lady had worked. I still don’t know exactly what happened, but I have always questioned the fairness of the firing. I felt badly, but I didn’t know what to do other than report the findings. How was I to know he would be so arbitrary?
I have already chronicled the massive frauds I was involved with at Commonwealth Savings and Loan, Flowerland, and Sahlen and Associates. Each of these cases involved tens of millions of dollars. They all represented outright criminal behavior, but differed from the embezzlements described above, as they were deliberate schemes to defraud investors. I was deeply involved in each of them,
helping to root out the fraud and unravel the complex cover-ups. In each case, the perpetrators went to jail.
Being an auditor, I o ften found myself as the arbiter of ethical behavior in the reporting of financial information. I became accustomed to the stress this causes, but occasionally it became tough to handle. In the 1980s, we had a high- flying client called
Documation, Inc., based in Melbourne, Florida. Documation had developed the fastest impact printers in the U.S. marketplace and the business had taken o ff like a rocket ship. However, the li ft-off apparently was not fast enough for Ray Halbert, the CEO. Halbert began to aggressively push the envelope in the company’s quarterly financial releases. Before long, he was orchestrating deceptive practices and reporting financial information that was materially misleading, in my view. During the annual audit, our team came across several bogus transactions that signi ficantly distorted the company’s reported results. The transactions involved fictitious sales to fictitious customers. I worked for days trying to get them to adjust the numbers, but Ray staunchly refused. So, I attended a meeting of Documation’s board of directors and presented our findings. I
spilled the beans, so to speak. I will never forget Halbert’s reaction.
He stood up at the end of the long mahogany conference table and snorted, “Gentleman, this man is a lying son of a bitch! As a board you have a decision to make. Are you going to believe me or this itinerant numbers geek?” I froze in my seat. I cautiously gazed around the table to judge the reaction of the solemn board members. They stepped out of the room for a while and then returned. A designated leader told Halbert, “Ray, we have made a decision on the challenge you posed. We have opted to believe Peat Marwick. Y ou are hereby terminated as CEO of the company, and we’re asking you to leave the premises immediately. We also recommend you secure personal counsel, because you’re going to need it. ”
There was an interesting sequel to the Documation story.
Shortly after that haunting board meeting, I received a call from Dick
T esta, who was appointed chairman of the board following Halbert’s termination. Dick was the senior partner of a major Boston law firm and also on the board of T extron, a major technology company.
He advised me that the Documation board had voted to terminate
Peat Marwick’s services. I was flabbergasted. Here I had saved the company from perpetrating a massive fraud on the marketplace,
and this was my reward? Dick acknowledged the unfairness of the decision, but implied that they were acting out of a perceived need for some kind of legal protection. I didn’t get it, but the decision was irrevocable. Then Dick called me aside and said in hushed tones,
“Keith, I know this isn’t fair, but I’ll make it up to you. That’s a promise. ” I swallowed hard and thought to myself, “Y eah, yeah. Sure you will!” Then, about four years later, out of the blue, I received a phone call from Dick T esta. It was a short call. Just a few minutes. He said, “Keith, do you remember a private promise I made to you a few years ago? Well, I’m calling to deliver today. ” He had joined the board of a major company in New Y ork and had persuaded them to change auditors from Price Waterhouse to Peat Marwick. No proposal, no interviews, no fee haggling, nothing. His only request was for me to provide him the name of a partner in New Y ork to contact. Now,
there is a positive case of business ethics. An honorable man, living up to his word!
The patterns of ethical and unethical behavior I observed in the business world remained fairly static over the fifty years of my career.
The numbers just got bigger.
Not long after arriving in Philadelphia in 1991, I met the managing partner of one of the major law firms in the city. We seemed to hit it off and soon became friends, having lunch together from time to time and discussing business and political affairs. After about a year, I received an urgent call from him asking me to meet with him on an extremely con fidential matter. I o ffered to go over to his office and he said, “No, no. Can you meet me in the next half hour at a park bench on Ri ttenhouse Square?” I agreed and found myself si tting with him —in heavy overcoats —on a cold blustery day in a deserted downtown park, evocative of some spy movie.
He began to lay out the most bizarre scenario I had ever heard. He had learned that one of his senior partners had been laundering fraudulent funds through the law firm for about three years and had been skimming personal cuts for himself along the way. This man said he had accidentally stumbled upon the scheme and was the only individual in the firm who had any knowledge of it, as far as he knew.
The dollars in this case were enormous. Moreover, the protagonists were prominent individuals with high-pro file national reputations
(hence the vagueness in this paragraph more than twenty years later,
including withholding the attorney’s name). There was not much for me to do except listen to the story and provide some commentary.
The attorney wanted to talk con fidentially with someone he trusted, as he was trying to decide how to react and still protect the reputation of his prestigious law firm. We agreed that he should personally contact the U.S. Attorney General and describe what he knew. He made the phone call right then and there. He and I then walked directly to the train station, several blocks away, where he caught the next train to Washington, D.C. We never spoke of the event again, except for laconic periodic expressions of appreciation for my confidential counsel. I learned through this unusual situation that fraud has no ceiling and no boundaries. I also came to recognize how good it feels to know that a friend trusts you enough to confide something as large and sensitive as this case.
On a Saturday afternoon in 1994, I received a call from Bill
Hannon, our financial services leader in New Y ork, advising me of a large trading loss at Kidder Peabody (Kidder Peabody, a prominent investment banking firm, was our client; it was owned by GE
Capital). Kidder Peabody had called an emergency meeting for
Monday, and Bill asked me to join him there. So, bright and early on Monday morning, I found myself si tting across the table from an irate Jack Welch, chairman of General Electric, and a livid Gary
Wendt, CEO of GE Capital. A rogue trader from Kidder Peabody named Joe Jett, a clever, highly educated guy, had found a loophole in Kidder’s computer systems and had covered $200 million in trading losses with bogus profitable trades, thereby producing huge bonuses for himself. It was the largest investment banking fraud in
U.S. history. The case received intense scrutiny by all levels of GE
and KPMG, and the media a ttention seemed never-ending. An internal investigation evolved into a highly publicized document called the “Lynch Report” . The scandal also found its way into Jack
Welch’s autobiography, Straight from the Gut . It’s one of the classic stories in the annals of American business. I was there and saw it unfold as it emerged.
I have often wondered how I became witness to so many ethical aberrations. Or were they aberrations at all? I was never one to turn a blind eye to problems—does that explain my proximity to so many cases? I have seen literally dozens of serious ethical breakdowns over the years and have worked with numerous clients to try to determine the root causes of aberrant behavior and get to the bottom of internal control issues; maybe I have developed a reputation for that. Over the years, I seem to have developed an unusual sensitivity to, and discernment about, abnormal conduct, perhaps honed and developed by working with these cases.
And these peculiar instincts continue to this day. Recently, a neighbor across the street from my home was fined $6 million and sentenced to house arrest for harboring illegal Swiss bank accounts.
T o be sure, I had nothing to do with this case or the detection of it.
However, I did have a sixth sense about this guy just from talking with him in the street. I had o ften idly wondered what he was up to and had entertained the notion that it was something no good. Why was that? I’m still wondering.
The opening lines of Edgar Allen Poe’s The Raven begins, “Once upon a midnight dreary, while I pondered, weak and weary… ” . I
suppose I’ll keep pondering about business ethics, while keeping eyes open and ears perked for signs of problems out there.

## Principles

- Maintain professional skepticism and vigilance.
- Investigate anomalies thoroughly before concluding.
- Escalate material misstatements to appropriate authority when management resists correction.
- Value confidentiality when counseling individuals facing sensitive disclosures.
- Question the fairness of punitive actions and consider proportionality.

## Decision heuristics

- When numeric or transactional anomalies appear, map the transaction flow end-to-end to reconcile quantities and identify manipulation.
- If physical documents show inconsistencies, obtain bank duplicates or originals to compare endorsements and verify authenticity.
- Persist in requesting management adjustments; if refused, escalate the issue to the board for resolution.
- When a trusted colleague discloses criminal conduct, advise immediate contact with the appropriate legal authority.
- Do not ignore small recurring discrepancies; even modest shortages can indicate long-term or systemic abuse.
