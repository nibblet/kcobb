The Power of Momentum
As with many people, college was a pivotal juncture in my life.
When I packed my bags and le ft Calhoun City behind, in the fall of 1959, to enter the University of Southern Mississippi (named
Mississippi Southern College at the time), the two-hundred-mile journey was a huge distance in my mind and in the perspective of my parents and others. I was still frail and weak from the lung surgery
I had undergone that summer, but my spirits were high and I was excited to be embarking on a new journey in my life.
As I re flect on this transformational time and the ensuing four years, one important concept and life lesson comes to mind.
It is the importance of what I call “momentum” . As a result of my fortunate high school education experiences, I was extremely well prepared for college, particularly academically. As a matter of fact, I
was surprised at how ill prepared some of my fellow students were.
Therefore, I enjoyed a built-in competitive advantage from the get-go. Thankfully, I was somehow alert enough to use this edge to build a pattern of success in my collegiate experience. Then momentum set in and, as the expression goes, success bred success. My early academic performance acted as a magnet and set the stage for success in other endeavors. I believe that positive momentum pushed me forward and allowed me to maximize the academic opportunities before me.
The initial core curriculum in those days was virtually the same for everyone —English, mathematics, history, sciences, etc.
I launched my first school year with these courses, together with
Principles of Accounting, which normally is a second year course—
eighteen credit hours each term. I was astonished at how easy some of these courses were for me. Clearly, I was expecting something more arduous. Freshman English and algebra seemed like nothing more than a repetition of my high school courses. Even the introductory accounting courses seemed eerily reminiscent of Frances Hardin’s bookkeeping course in high school. My performance in these courses was excellent and I finished the first year with a near straight
A average. Somewhere in my memorabilia, I have a slip of paper that recognizes my first-year grade point average as ranking number one out of 970 students. And I was able to do this with an absolute minimum of studying outside the classroom. A skinny country boy from the red clay hill country. Imagine!
Perhaps a few anecdotes might illustrate how my academic career unfolded and how momentum played a pivotal role. The stories also might illustrate how academic performance played a role in other valuable lessons and friendships along the way.
I spent my first year in Forest Hall, a freshman dormitory where rooms and roommates were pre-assigned. My roommate was a student named Vince Colandrea, a rough and tumble guy from New
Jersey. Vince was the stereotypical Italian street guy. He could have played a leading role in the Broadway musical Grease. He was one of several New Jersey and Pennsylvania fellows who had migrated down to Mississippi for some reason. Lower admission standards for athletes probably played a role in the migration. Most were, in fact,
athletes, and a few had made the football team. Through Vince, I met and became friendly with all these fellows, although I actually had little in common with them. It was quickly apparent they had not enjoyed the excellent academic background that I had. It was hard to believe they ever graduated from high school. Their academic skills were atrocious. Most couldn’t even distinguish a noun from a verb.
I’m serious! They all struggled desperately to survive academically and were constantly looking to me for help.
The football players were particularly concerned because their athletic scholarships were at risk. One evening around mid-year,
there was a knock on my dormitory door. It was Coach Thad Vann,
the head football coach. He introduced himself, took a seat on the edge of my bunk bed and said he had heard about me. He told me about his concern for the grades of the football team and asked if I
would be willing to tutor these New Jersey and Pennsylvania football players. Before I could ask what was in it for me, he gave me a lecture about the importance of athletics to the University and collegiate team spirit. He then o ffered me a seat at the training table in the cafeteria. Now, to most people that may not sound like much, but to me it was a really big deal. I accepted and for the rest of that year,
I was no longer subjected to the mystery meat and sloppy macaroni dished out at the cafeteria. I ate every meal in the private athletic dining room and feasted on thick steaks, fresh vegetables and terrific desserts. In my debilitated physical state (I’d had the two surgeries the summer before), that was the perfect compensation for me. So, I
worked with these guys as they struggled through their courses and,
fortunately, no one failed a single course. A miracle, actually! Coach
Vann was pleased and appreciative of my e fforts. He o ffered me a few other perks along the way and invited me to travel with the team the next year for a couple of away games, but I never managed to make it to one.
At the end of my freshman year, I was presented with the Phi Eta
Sigma Outstanding Freshman Award in recognition of my academic performance. Dr. J.R. Switzer, Dean of Student A ffairs, presented a trophy to me in a ceremony and invited me to visit with him in his office the next day. I knew his daughter Carolyn, who was also a freshman. During this visit, he talked about his dreams for her success in life and hoped she would buckle down and get more serious about her education. He also announced that he had actually called my parents the evening before to express his congratulations.
I was impressed that he had done this. I found this man warm and comfortable to be with. He had a fatherly countenance, and I liked him a lot.
Each year at USM, the Winn-Dixie grocery chain awarded three
$500 scholarships to second-year students. T o put that amount of money in perspective, my total tuition, room and board for my freshman year in 1959–1960 was $618. I checked out the criteria for the scholarships, which focused on academic performance and financial need, and filed an application over the summer. Given the
Phi Eta Sigma award, I felt confident that I would receive one of the three scholarships. When registration time came at the outset of my sophomore year, I inquired and was told that I was not a recipient.
Very surprised and curious, I asked who the recipients were. They demurred. A fter some wrangling with the office workers and insistence by me, the Registrar finally came over and coughed up the names. (Incidentally, the Registrar was Aubrey Lucas, who years later became the University president.) Lo and behold, the three recipients were two sons and a daughter of college administrative officials, including Carolyn Switzer of all people! I stood there,
with my mouth agape, on the college gymnasium floor, where the registration process was taking place. On an impulse, I le ft and walked straight over to Dr. Switzer’s office a few blocks away, and barged in. I told him what I had discovered and how dumbfounded
I was. I pointed out how my grades were be tter than all three of these students and my economic station in life was surely inferior to his and probably the others. He was quiet and contemplative for a while. I began to wonder if I might have overstepped. After a long,
pregnant pause, he said to me, “Son, I do understand your concern,
but I’m not going to try to explain this to you. Y ou should just settle down and go on back down to the gym and finish your registration. ”
I did, and when I arrived at the tuition payment station, the cashier said, “Keith, you don’t owe anything. Everything has been taken care of. ” That same message was delivered at the next two quarterly registration processes as well.
This episode was an extraordinary learning experience for me.
First, I was reminded in vivid color that life is not always fair. I learned that it pays to investigate actions and circumstances that appear questionable—perhaps a precursor to my later auditing days. I also learned that when one has the facts on his side, he should not allow himself to be intimidated by anyone. Lastly, I came to realize that
Dr. Switzer was fundamentally a good man who had made a slight misstep in judgment along the way. This event sealed my friendship with him for the next three years and cemented his respect for me as a person. I consulted with him regularly about college policies, and he often looked to me for advice on student affairs. Many years later,
when I was back at U.S.M. for an alumni function and Dr. Switzer was getting up in years, he told the large group of people assembled for the event that I was the smartest student he had ever met. Now,
I understand that this comment was gratuitous —and certainly not true—but it demonstrated a lasting bond that was first established in his office when I was nineteen years old. I consider Dr. Switzer one of my valuable mentors. And this experience served to fuel further momentum in my college career.
My grades continued to be excellent throughout my college days, even when I did not put forth much effort. My solid grounding continued to propel me forward —and somewhere along the line, I
managed to learn the art of listening and paying a ttention in class.
Many students skipped classes regularly. I rarely ever missed a class in my first three years. I always paid close a ttention to the lectures and, somehow, I usually managed to make sense of the material being presented.
I remember one interesting occasion when I was taking English literature with a professor named Mrs. Webb. She assigned the
Russian novel Crime and Punishment by Fyodor Dostoyevsky at the beginning of the term and said we would begin discussion at an assigned date. I procrastinated the reading and, in fact, never bothered to buy the book. Then, suddenly, the witching hour arrived and stared me in the face. Mrs. Webb reminded the class that we should have already read the entire book and announced that class discussion would begin the next day, following a quiz to evaluate our independent comprehension. I froze and then trembled. Later that day, I met with my friend Charlie McDaniels, who was also in the class and had read the book in detail. Charlie was a squirrelly little guy,
but friendly and conscientious to a fault. I said, “Charlie, you have to tell me something about this book. ” So, we sat down on a sofa in the
Student Union building and Charlie proceeded to lay out for me the story of a Russian guy named Raskolnikov who killed a pawnbroker.
He told me about the relationship of Raskolnikov with his mother,
sister and brother, how he likened himself to Napoleon and how a detective dogged him until he was caught and sentenced to Siberia.
A pretty interesting story, I thought at the time. Charlie delivered more detail than I had bargained for. The entire conversation lasted no more than a half hour. The next day we arrived in class and took the quiz, which had twenty questions. The following day Mrs.
Webb pranced into the classroom with a super-serious countenance and announced that the class had not performed well on the quiz.
“However,” she said. “I’m pleased to say that Mr. Cobb scored 100!
Mr. Cobb, I’m looking forward to hearing your perspective on this wonderful novel. ” I turned around and looked back at Charlie, who was shaking his head in confusion. He had scored 80, which was the second best grade in the class. I’m really still not sure how that happened. But I think it had something to do with listening and understanding and mentally assembling and organizing facts. And it probably had something to do with test-taking in general, and perhaps a bit of luck. In any event, I was summarily embarrassed and spent that entire weekend reading Crime and Punishment.
A similar thing occurred a year or so later, when I was taking
Money and Banking with Dr. Joseph Green, dean of the School of
Commerce and Business Administration. My roommate, Charlie
Brumfield, was taking the class with me. I still remember exactly where I was seated—on the second row next to the window. I also remember where Charlie sat, the row behind me two seats to the left.
The material about the Federal Reserve System and monetary policy fascinated me and I found myself absorbed in Dr. Green’s fascinating lectures. He was an acknowledged authority on the subject. By the end of the course, he sensed my keen interest and seemed to be talking directly to me, to the exclusion of the rest of the class.
Charlie noticed this attention and was confounded by it. He was my roommate and of course knew how little effort I applied outside the classroom. Charlie was an economics major and a serious student,
later earning a PhD in economics. Late each evening in our room he would study and slave over Money and Banking—hours and hours.
He chided me because I never purchased the book and predicted
I was going to fail the course because of my lack of e ffort. He was wrong. I made an A and he made a B. Now, I can’t explain that situation any more that I can rationalize the Crime and Punishment story, except for the force of the momentum I had developed by that time.
Dr. Green was a tall and lanky guy from Big Stone Gap, Virginia.
He smoked a pipe and looked the part of a business school dean. And he was one of my fans. He knew me well and was familiar with my background, both academically and through my college leadership roles. Near the end of my senior year, he called me to his office for a serious discussion about post-graduate work. He told me he had taken the liberty of arranging for a fully paid scholarship for me at the University of Virginia, his alma mater, where I could work on a three-year doctoral program in economics. I was totally flattered and taken aback. However, I was not particularly interested in a career in economics. By that time I had several attractive offers from all the Big Eight accounting firms, two banks and IBM. Moreover,
I was about to get married, and I had no money! So, I thanked him profusely but declined the opportunity. He understood my rationale, but was noticeably disappointed. He stayed in touch with me throughout the rest of his tenure at Southern Mississippi while
I was working at KPMG. He was always generous with his time, as well as his compliments. Dr. Green was a true mentor and friend. He is a platinum member of my personal Hall of Fame.
My days at U.S.M. were filled with stories similar to these. There were many great and memorable experiences in a wide spectrum of classes over the four years. I have no illusions that I won the DNA
lottery or was natively brighter than anyone else. But neither can I
assert that I was just a hard worker. I wasn’t. At least academically.
As discussed previously, my excellent high school grounding set the stage and the momentum of that initial edge catapulted me forward.
I think the fact that I was an interested student —as contrasted to someone just passing through —was a huge contributor to my success. And, of course, the Harry Lorayne memory tricks I had learned when I was sick didn’t hurt things at all. My grades slipped a bit in my senior year, when I was totally absorbed in Student
Government affairs, job interviews and shu ttling back and forth to
Mississippi College to see Dot. But I managed to end my college career in 1963 with a straight A, 4.00 academic average in accounting.
My overall average was 3.50, qualifying me to graduate with honors.
I am a great believer in the value of momentum. My three sons can probably recite verbatim my “get ahead early” speech, which they heard countless times during their school years. It is a concept that served me well, both in college and throughout my business career.
