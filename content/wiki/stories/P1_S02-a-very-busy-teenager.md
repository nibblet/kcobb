## Full Text

A V ery Busy T eenager
The four years of high school in Calhoun City rank among the busiest periods of my life. As I re flect on those times and tick o ff all that was going on in my life, it is truly a wonder how it all got crammed into four years. I will attempt to capture some of the more memorable events and circumstances of those busy days.
Perhaps it is appropriate to start with the academics, which were superb. Even though this was a small rural school, the quality of the education delivered was truly outstanding. The curriculum was fairly standard for those days—English, math, sciences, social studies and other subjects. But the teaching was excellent, and fortunately for me, I soaked it up.
I was always a good student, partly because of a fundamental curiosity. Also, the subjects we were taught were naturally interesting,
and my parents expected me to make good grades. Mother made sure I did! I gravitated to the math and science courses. I pushed through algebra my junior year, ahead of the normal schedule, and
I was one of six students in my class who took trigonometry and geometry in my senior year. Mr. Josh Adams made chemistry and physics come alive, particularly the lab experiments. I especially enjoyed the bookkeeping and typing courses, which were taught by
Mrs. Frances Hardin. The office and business orientation of these courses more than likely had some influence on my decision to major in accounting in college. I wasn’t especially fond of English, but I
simply adored Miss Frances Mallory. There was something about her that appealed to my sense of romance, and thus I worked hard to learn the materials in her class. I remember memorizing, at her insistence and along with everyone else, the prologue to Chaucer’s
Canterbury T ales and slaving over Thomas Hardy’s Return of the
Native. More than fifty years later, I can still recite that prologue in
Old English. Occasionally these days many years later, when driving back into town at Calhoun City, I recall an apparently indelible mental image of Clym Y eobright, Thomas Hardy’s native returning to Egdon Heath.
The boys were required to take a few semesters of agriculture.
The girls were required to take home economics. Although I had no farming orientation, I actually enjoyed the agriculture classes. I
learned about plant characteristics, insecticides, crop rotation and all sorts of stuff. As I drive through the Mississippi farmlands today,
I can conjure up some of the old lectures by Jack James about cotton,
corn, soybeans, hogs, cattle, fertilizers and the like.
All sorts of activities outside the classroom supplemented the academic program. These included sports, music, shop and a variety of clubs and organizations. I found myself involved in many of them from time to time. I always liked basketball and wanted to play on the high school team, but my mother did not like that idea at all.
She thought it worthless and saw it as interfering with my focus on band. She simply put her foot down and forbade me from playing it.
Nevertheless, I tried to secretly work it in, early in my freshman year.
I managed to go to a few practices and played a few minutes in one game, dressed out in uniform and skipping band practice in order to do it. As might have been expected, Mother found out and went into a rage. That was it—the end of my basketball career!
I spent a big segment of my extracurricular time with the school band. I was blessed with natural musical ability and I played the clarinet for all four years of high school, sang in the glee club,
directed by Freddie Cox, and participated in a number of school plays and operettas. I liked playing clarinet for a number of reasons.
First, I loved music in general. Second, I found it relatively easy and thus could perform well without an enormous amount of grueling practice. And third, I thoroughly enjoyed the camaraderie of band members and all the activities we shared. One last reason I enjoyed it so well was the recognition that this was something I could do really well and take a back seat to practically no one. Our band marched and played at all the football games during the season —both home games and away games. We also marched in various regional
Christmas parades. Each year, we also had at least one band concert,
which was always a well-attended community event. In addition to the full band, we organized collateral groups. I was an active member of two groups—a Dixieland band and a dance band.
There is an ironic twist to all this concentrated involvement in band. As much as I enjoyed it and was pro ficient at it, my clarinet activity curiously ended abruptly when I graduated high school. I
never played the clarinet again, except for a brief encounter with
Pete Fountain in T allahassee, Florida when I was in college. I
fortuitously met him at the Lafayette Motel after a concert at Florida
State University. I told him I played the clarinet, so he loaned me one of his clarinets and we sat out by the motel swimming pool at about midnight with a couple of his band members playing Dixieland jazz.
Every time I hear the song, “Just a Closer Walk” , memories of that night spring to my mind. But that was it. I put the clarinet on the shelf for good.
I worked on the school newspaper, The Loudspeaker , for a couple of years and was the editor my senior year. The paper was a rather crude and simplistic document that was published monthly.
Despite its simplicity, I found this a tough responsibility. Persuading other students to write articles was always a chore and getting them to meet deadlines was even harder. I would type the entire paper on mimeograph masters and then run o ff copies on an antiquated machine. Mrs. Frances Hardin proofread my work and supervised all my editorial activities in a tiny closet adjacent to her classroom.
Without her leadership, I probably would never have completed the first issue. Throughout my later business career, I struggled from time to time with leadership and management issues —taking on too much of the responsibility myself and failing to distribute the workload. I think that habit probably began with my Loudspeaker experience. Although we had a so-called journalism sta ff of more than a dozen students who showed up for pictures in the annual yearbook, I did about 90 percent of the work required to publish the paper. I learned a lot of things from the Loudspeaker experience, but apparently delegation of workloads wasn’t one of them.
While all this schoolwork was going on, a dozen other tentacles dangled before me and tugged me in di fferent directions. For starters, I worked at Lunceford’s Bargain Store every Saturday throughout high school, so there was rarely a full weekend o ff. I
remained involved in local Boy Scout troop activities, although concentration in this area slipped away a bit as time went by. And there was always church —Sunday school, choir practice, revivals,
etc.—that occupied my time. In the midst of these was packed an occasional trip somewhere. I went to Valley Forge with the Boy
Scouts, New Y ork with the Lions All-State Band, Jackson and the
Gulf Coast for state band clinics, Memphis to compete in a talent contest, Greenwood to march in Christmas parades, and I rode busses to football games in neighboring towns, etc. In summary, I
was quite a busy guy.
Illness took a bite out of my time during my last two years of high school. I developed a collapsed le ft lung, which eventually required surgery at the Baptist Hospital in Memphis. Spontaneous pneumothorax, it was called. T reatment for that condition today is relatively straightforward, but fifty-five years ago it was a critical condition requiring major surgery and significant risk. It was a scary time for me, and full recovery took weeks. Then, whammo, a year later the other lung collapsed. Because of the trauma of the first surgery, I resisted the second one. However, I eventually acquiesced and went under the knife again.
There was one positive outgrowth of the lung surgeries. I read about and ordered a newly published book by Harry Lorayne called
How to Develop a Super Power Memory. While sick and recovering, I
read this book and spent hours practicing Lorayne’s techniques for memory improvement. Many were relatively simple tricks, but they worked amazingly well. In due course, I mastered many of the skills,
skills that served me well for years to come. I learned to memorize abstract lists of things. A person could call o ff any numbered list of words once, and I could repeat them, along with the number of each word. I learned how to associate faces with names and could memorize literally hundreds of names. T o the uninitiated, it was almost magical. Later in college, I estimated that I could call over three thousand students by name. My friends and I would test this skill from time to time by walking around the campus and speaking to people, calling as many by name as possible. My good friend John
Simpson loved to play the role of ringmaster and convince people in bar rooms to bet money against the proposition that I could remember a list of fifty random words of their choosing after hearing them only once. We always won. John would tell people that I had a photographic memory, but of course that wasn’t true. We would split the money, and our winnings paid for a lot of fun nights out on the town! Lorayne became famous over the following years as the foremost memory-training specialist in the world. I can certainly understand why. I found his systems incredibly useful. He saved me untold hours of studying over the next few years. Many of those skills have withered over the years, but I can still memorize the word lists, usually flawlessly.
Somewhere along the way in high school, I developed the terrible habit of smoking cigarettes, along with all my friends. I’m not sure how I fell into this snare, but it was an awful trap. The doctors said that my lung condition was not related to smoking. One would think the surgeries would have been enough to convince me to stop smoking, regardless of what the doctors said, but alas, that didn’t take place until years later. I may have been a good student, but I
wasn’t smart enough to break this habit!
The qualifying age for obtaining a driver’s license in Mississippi was fifteen. Immediately following my birthday, I filed the application and took the test. My parents were liberal in allowing me to use the family car for my personal enjoyment. A usual condition,
however, was that I had to take Sandy along with me—that is, if she wanted to come along. At one point, I actually bought my own car.
Well, almost a car. Daddy’s cousin Lane Dickson had an old, beat-up
1935 Plymouth that he sold me for $50. The car had a garish red and silver homemade paint job and was in very poor condition. The floorboard was rusted out in a dozen places. The car would run, but that’s about all one could say about it. I drove it around town for a while—that is, when I wasn’t working on it—and eventually tired of the novelty. Before long, I put a “for sale” sign and the car in our front yard, and sold it to some stranger for the same $50 that I paid for it.
I had a couple of accidents in the family car, both my fault. I must say that Daddy was understanding about the accidents. He wasn’t rattled at all and he set about helping me learn from the experiences.
Mother, on the other hand, was quick to condemn my recklessness and dream up appropriate punishment. But throughout most of my high school days, I was fortunate to have transportation to maximize my opportunity for activities and social life.
Speaking of social life, I had loads of good friends and spent many hours lolling around town with them. John Edmondson and
Bill Carty were my best friends. We shared many common interests and spent a lot of time together. I also was not oblivious to all the girls in town. I dated a number of di fferent girls over the years, but actually never had a truly long-term serious relationship.
During my senior year, I began to be concerned about my career and what my college major should be. My teachers pushed me toward engineering because of my orientation to math and the sciences. But somehow, that just didn’t seem right. Music might have been an option, but it seemed like too much drudgery, with all the practice involved, and I couldn’t visualize a meaningful future in it for me. I talked to Mr. Harrison, the school superintendent, about it and he suggested I take an aptitude test. I agreed, and he made the arrangements for me to go up to Ole Miss and take a test administered by a professor there. He sent my test papers o ff and a few weeks later got the results back. Among about twenty-five professions, the highest rating by a wide margin was a category called “CPA/Owner” .
So, that was it. Why argue with professional judgment? I resolved to major in accounting and enrolled in my first course at the University of Southern Mississippi the next year. When people asked me how I
happened to choose accounting as a career, I always replied, “It was just my destiny!”
These high school days were pivotal years in my life. There has never been a busier four years. In the midst of the frenzy, my brain was like a sponge. I cannot say enough about the quality of my education at Calhoun City High School. It was a small place —
thirty-nine students in my graduating class—tucked away, as I said,
in northeast Mississippi. But the quality of education delivered was simply superb. Others in my general age bracket have made similar observations. I waltzed through my first year of college with negligible e ffort, largely because of the solid grounding and preparation received in high school. The heroes of this drama were the outstanding educators who were so devoted to their students:
Frances Mallory, Josh Adams, Frances Hardin, Irene Denton,
Freddie Cox and others. I am deeply indebted to these people for their leadership.
