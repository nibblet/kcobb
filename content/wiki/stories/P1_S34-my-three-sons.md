My Three Sons
Of all the memories sca ttered over of my life, among the most joyful are re flections on our three sons, Paul, John and Mark. I am a very fortunate father to have been blessed with such wonderful children—indeed a proud father, in every sense of the word.
Paul arrived in our lives on May 1, 1968, at St. Dominic Hospital in Jackson. I have a vivid memory of standing beside his tiny bed for the first time, picking him up and holding him, and asking myself,
“Can this be real?” It felt like a sort of mirage, or a miracle, the realization that this was really and truly a new life that Dot and I had created and brought into the world.
John was born on October 29, 1971, at Orange Memorial
Hospital in Orlando. I was attending a KPMG partners’ meeting in
Boca Raton and received a call from Dot late in the evening to tell me she was on the way to the hospital. I immediately jumped into my car and raced the two hundred miles, arriving at the hospital just before John arrived in the world in the wee morning hours. Once again, I stood in a brightly lit hospital nursery with wonder and amazement. Another miracle!
Mark was our third gi ft, delivered on April 11, 1977, at Holy
Cross Hospital in Fort Lauderdale. I shall never forget seeing him for the first time —a beautiful doll-like figure with a sprinkling of red hair. It seemed as if he had a faint smile on his face —perhaps a foreshadowing of the fun-loving personality that was later to develop. I remember a pair of twins, the Fetch twins, who were born prematurely at almost the same time and were side by side with him in the nursery. One died almost immediately and the other was born blind. That made Mark’s miracle even more dramatic in my mind.
Dot and I have thousands of wonderful stories about these guys over the last several decades. We also have dozens of albums of photos to go along with the stories —thousands and thousands of pictures. T o try and capsule their lives in a short memoir chapter is actually hopeless. Nevertheless, a few highlights and themes do emerge. The early school days are memorable, as are their varied activities and interests over the years.
From the get-go, Dot and I focused sharply on making sure our guys received as good an education as we could provide for them.
Paul was three years old when we moved to Orlando. He later entered the first grade at a public school at Altamonte Springs. He then went to the second grade at Bay View Elementary School, after we moved to Fort Lauderdale, where he was quickly singled out as a gifted student. We enrolled him in the third grade at the venerable
Pine Crest School, which he attended through his graduation in
1986. Paul was always an excellent student, but perhaps a bit of an underachiever academically. Invariably, he tested higher than his grades. He had lots of fine friends and was a working teenager at
Beach Hardware. He swam on the swim team at Pine Crest and was active on the debate team. Somewhere along the way, he became interested in military and public a ffairs and seemed drawn to the
U.S. Air Force for some reason. He applied and was accepted by a number of universities and also applied for an appointment to the
Air Force Academy. All service academy admissions required a formal appointment by a member of the U.S. Congress. I was friends with Congressman Clay Shaw and told Paul that I would be pleased to speak to Clay about his application. He said, “Dad, please don’t do that. If I’m going to do this, I want to do it on my own. ” So I
backed off and let him proceed. He was accepted by the Academy and received his congressional appointment from U.S. Senator Paula
Hawkins, one of only two Florida appointments allocated to her that year. So off he went into a completely new and different life.
John was only three years old when we moved to Fort Lauderdale.
He followed Paul at Pine Crest and attended school there all twelve years, graduating in 1989. He struggled a bit in school in the first year or two, until we stumbled upon the fact that his eyesight was an issue.
On the advice of an alert teacher, we took him to an optometrist and he emerged with the most amazed look on his face. He could actually see clearly for the first time in his life! He never had a problem in school after that day. John was always smart and a quick study, so he never spent much time hi tting the books. Nevertheless, his grades were always acceptable, despite his insatiable social appetite.
One of his first jobs as a teenager was working at Skippy’s, an ice-cream restaurant. It was a great experience for him, and he learned some valuable lessons there. T oday, we all know of John’s generous nature and his appetite to share with others. This admirable trait was noticeable even at an early age, when we learned that he was sharing part of his wages at Skippy’s with a homeless man who slept in the back of the restaurant. He later wrote a poignant college essay about this man that became a pivotal part of his collegiate application process. As John neared high school graduation, he and I made a tour of potential colleges for him to a ttend. We landed at Lehigh
University in Bethlehem, Pennsylvania, which turned out to be a perfect choice for him.
Mark attended Pine Crest through the sixth grade, when we moved to Baltimore. He attended McDonough School in Baltimore for two years and then finished up his high school career with four years at Shipley School in Philadelphia. Changing schools in mid-stream in junior high and high school is always tough on young students, but Mark’s vibrant, fun-loving personality served him well.
He never broke stride and adapted marvelously. Mark is extremely intelligent, but often drifted to the underperforming side, much like his brothers. Nevertheless, his grades also were always acceptable.
He’s been the instant life of any party and has been a virtual magnet for friends. His boundless sense of humor makes even the most serious people laugh. From as early as I can remember, Mark has been a sports fanatic. When he was only ten or eleven years old, he insisted on playing the football pools in my office and would o ften win the pot, to the amazement and consternation of my associates.
I think he must have been born loving the Dallas Cowboys! Like the other guys, he worked at several jobs while in high school. Mark considered a number of colleges on the eastern seaboard and settled on James Madison University in Harrisonburg, Virginia. JMU fit him well and was an excellent choice for him.
As these three guys completed their education and began their own careers and independent lives, they each set out in different directions. But, notwithstanding their stark di fferences in appearances and personalities, Dot and I began to notice a central theme with them —traits that had been important to us as we had nurtured them through the years. Of all the ways I could describe them and single out their a ttributes, the common trait that stands out prominently is that all three were paragons of character and competence as they matured to manhood. They had solid, well-developed core values —internal compasses by which they have steered their lives.
Keith, Mark, John and Paul in Washington, D.C.
Those same core values emerged for us to admire when they began to choose their spouses and life mates. Second only to our love for our sons is our love for their wives. They all managed to find and marry women who appear to be perfect matches and who have fostered their happiness for many years. Of course, they had an exceptional role model in their mother to form a pa ttern or road map! Seriously, I can see many of the same traits in Dawn, Sarah and Sue that resemble so many of Dot’s core values and personal characteristics. Each of the guys chose well and, as a result, have happy marriages and wonderful families.
Paul, John and Mark are widely different in so many respects—
different careers, di fferent interests and di fferent general outlooks.
One illustration comes to mind. Some time ago, Dot and I sat down and worked up a detailed list of all of our family a ffairs—financial,
assets, records, access codes, passwords, etc. We thought such a comprehensive document might be useful to our children once we are gone. So I typed it all up and sent copies to the three guys. Their responses were interesting —and telling. Paul sent back an email saying, “Dad, thanks for doing this. I have done something similar whenever I have been deployed to the Middle East. Dawn and I
are going to put this document in our safe and hope we never have to use it. ” On the same day, I received an email from John saying,
“Dad, is this all you have to do?” Then Mark followed with his usual banter: “Dad, please don’t send us any more crap like this. Just send money!” These comments are truly emblematic of the three personalities—Paul, the serious one; John, short and to the point;
Mark, who couldn’t resist the comedian opportunity.
Y et, the three share so many common traits. For example, all three have turned out to be exemplary fathers, dedicating a huge part of their lives to their children. It’s almost embarrassing to me as I watch their parenting and look back and wonder about my own investment in my children. As we observe our grandchildren, we notice glimpses of the same abiding characteristics —character and competence—sprouting as they grow up. It’s all so interesting.
Through the years I have enjoyed many special times with my boys, sometimes as a group and at other times one on one. One especially memorable time was a trip to Washington, D.C. with
Paul and John when they were about ten and seven, just the three of us wandering around the historic sites, staying at the Capitol
Hilton, eating at fancy restaurants and soaking up experiences and knowledge that have lasted for decades. On another occasion, I
remember taking Mark, the sports nut, to a Boston Red Sox game when he was about ten. It was a complete surprise to him until we entered the stadium, and I have indelible memories of his shock and excitement. I worked with Paul building a model railroad in his early teen years (some of which he still has today). I worked with John on boat maintenance so he could tool around with his friends in the
Fort Lauderdale waterways. And I tried hard to keep up with Mark’s obsession with sports trivia so that I could at least communicate! We have taken several “guy trips” or excursions over the years, including a couple of fishing trips in Costa Rica, where all four of us bonded together. A few years ago, we reunited as a foursome and went back to Washington, D.C., for a delightful weekend together.
With the guys scattered around the country—Denver, Chicago and Washington—it has been di fficult getting everyone together at the same time. But we’ve managed to do so a few times, and it has always been wonderful. The last such gathering was a family cruise,
when all thirteen of us spent a week together in the Caribbean celebrating Dot’s and my fiftieth wedding anniversary.
One thing I have particularly enjoyed over the years has been counseling with the guys about their careers. I suppose that may be partly because I devoted so much of my life to my own career and have a natural interest. Their careers vary greatly, but each is interesting to me, and I thoroughly enjoy counseling them as they travel along in their career journeys.
I recently was asked what I thought was the secret in raising three such upstanding and successful young men. I fumbled through some words. Sitting out on our dock that evening, Dot and
I pondered this question and came up with three basic things. First,
we insisted on thevery best education we could provide. Second,
we always treated them like real people, as contrasted to wards or subordinates. Third, we always tried to be consistent in our dealings with them. But actually it was a lot more than that. I think that once again serendipity played a major role. Time and again, we found in them traits and characteristics beyond what we were looking for.
They each are just fundamentally good guys and, to a large extent,
we have been along for the ride as they developed.
Paul, John and Mark truly have been God-sent blessings for Dot and me. They have been the source of immense pride and lots of fun over the years. We love them, and it’s so nice to know that the love is reciprocated. It is a wonderful feeling to know we will be leaving behind a legacy in three fine men who have incredible families,
admirable personal values and successful careers. Without a doubt,
each of them will easily eclipse anything I have ever done.

## Principles

- Prioritize providing the best possible education for children.
- Treat children with respect and as autonomous individuals.
- Be consistent in parenting and dealings with children.
- Support independence by stepping back when a child requests ownership of a decision.
- Act promptly on credible external observations about a child's needs (e.g., teacher concerns).

## Decision heuristics

- When a child explicitly requests to handle an important opportunity, step back and let them lead the process.
- Act on reliable external alerts (teachers or professionals) and obtain appropriate evaluations promptly.
- Provide the best available educational resources early and persistently to nurture potential.
- Document important family affairs clearly and share them with adult children to reduce future uncertainty.
