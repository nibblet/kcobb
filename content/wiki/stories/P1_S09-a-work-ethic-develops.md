## Full Text

A Work Ethic Develops
Bayne and Frances Cobb began instilling a work ethic in their children at a young age. Work was considered a fundamental part of childhood development. The process probably began at about age six, with definitive assigned chores around the house. These quickly developed into routine responsibilities. My earliest memories of chores include helping my dad with the vegetable gardening and assisting my mother with washing and folding clothes. Making our beds daily and keeping our clothes and toys picked up were basic responsibilities from as early as I can remember. These duties progressed quickly to more advanced chores.
As I mentioned earlier, Mother had a small business sewing for the public, and one of my early jobs was to make covered bu ttons and belt buckles for her customers; she sewed the bu ttonholes and made the belts. She had a simple mechanical die cutter and press that
I learned to operate when I was about ten years old. I would fold the material, cut circles with the die cu tter to cover the bu tton-shaped metal disks and use the press to crimp the material firmly onto the disks. I made hundreds of these buttons over time. The belt buckles were a little more complicated, but I mastered that too. I actually enjoyed it. The finished products were a source of pride; they never had a wrinkle in the fabric or a crimp in the shape. When they were finished, I would line them up in a row and stare at them with pride.
My sense of accomplishment was sometimes burnished when the customers were complimentary and talked about how remarkable it was that I was doing these things at such a young age.
I also became my mother’s color coordinator, of sorts. I became an expert at color matching, at least in my mind. And, like the button customers, the ladies at the Ben Franklin store would o ften be complimentary. They said things like, “Can you believe that child can get those colors right every single time?” I always beamed and was proud. Like most kids, I enjoyed praise.
Daddy decided I needed to be exposed to outside work and suggested that I try raising chickens. So we ordered fifty baby chicks by mail. Y es, you could actually order baby chickens by mail! I
remember going to the post office and picking them up. There were two boxes of twenty-five chicks each. Daddy built a wire mesh coop for them and bought the feed for them, but I was assigned the job of feeding and watering the flock daily. Periodically, I also had to clean the manure from the cage, a despicable job. The daily grind of taking care of the chickens was drudgery and interfered with other things I
wanted to do, but I actually enjoyed watching the chicks grow.
When they were fully mature, it was time to prepare them for market. At this point, Mother stepped into the picture, and the two of us killed and dressed all fifty chickens in two days. I chased them around the large pen and caught them with a bent coat hanger,
then killed them by wringing their necks. We then soaked them in boiling water, plucked the feathers and butchered them. So, there I
was: eleven years old in our back yard with a large butcher knife in hand, chopping up dead chickens! And Mother made sure I did it correctly. We took the dressed chickens to town and sold them to
Calder’s Grocery on the square.
I don’t recall the dollars the chickens yielded, but I do remember that Mother and Daddy split the proceeds with me. She and Daddy explained that they had paid for the baby chicks, built the cages and bought the feed, and therefore deserved a portion of the proceeds,
even though I’d had the large manual task of raising the chickens.
This was my first lesson in entrepreneurship and division of profits.
My first real job outside the home was with the Mississippi
Valley Gas Company in the summer of 1953. I was twelve years old.
Mrs. Flora Mae Wilson, office manager of the gas company, hired me to sit at the front desk and serve as a receptionist. I worked five days a week for four or five hours each day and was paid $2 per day. From time to time she gave me small odd jobs, but most of the time I just sat there and answered the phone. One job assignment was to file the meter readers’ tickets with customer account cards that were kept alphabetically in a big gray filing tray. As I plodded along with this boring chore, I couldn’t resist reading some of the files. I was curious,
even at that age. Several delinquent accounts popped up, and I asked
Mrs. Flora Mae why some of the customers were not paying their bills. She replied abruptly, “Son, that’s none of your business. Y ou just forget about what you saw and don’t worry yourself about that.
And another thing; don’t mention those customers to anyone! Y ou understand me?” It was my first lesson in confidentiality.
At the end of the summer I went to the post office with some of the money I had saved and bought two $25 Series E U.S. Savings
Bonds for $18.75 each. I understood that they matured in ten years,
so I put them away in a safe place. T en years later, Dot and I went to the Five Points branch of First National Bank in Jackson two weeks after we were married and cashed the bonds. We walked directly from the bank to the Kroger supermarket and used these funds to buy our first groceries.
The next two summers were spent working at various things almost full time. I tried to play a little baseball in those summer days, but Mother never thought that was a good idea. I had to slip around to do it and always got in trouble when she found out. She thought I was old enough to be working full time and insisted that
I get busy with something productive. So I called on some of my earlier entrepreneurial experiences and launched two businesses —
peddling tomatoes and providing lawn maintenance services. I still kept up the button and belt buckle assistance, but there was little or no compensation for that.
I planted two long garden rows of tomatoes with Daddy’s careful supervision. Daddy was an expert on tomatoes and had a reputation for the best tomatoes anywhere around, but these were my tomatoes and my responsibility. I bought the plants at the co-op and paid for them out of my savings. I had actually learned something about the value of capital investment from my chicken venture. When the tomatoes began to ripen and were picked, I would load them up on a little red wagon and begin my marketing expedition. Sandy was my helper, of sorts, and trailed around with me on the sales calls. I called on all the local groceries and then went house to house. I didn’t have any scales, so I sold them by the gallon, while being sure to pile an extra one or two on top to guarantee customer satisfaction. This was a piece of advice from Daddy. I have no idea how many tomatoes
I sold over the two summers, but it was a large number. I can still remember my pockets stuffed with quarters and dollar bills. It felt so good to realize that I had started a venture from scratch and earned real money through my conscientiousness and hard work. This time
I didn’t have to split my earnings with anyone!
I also mowed lawns—a lot of lawns. This was hard, sweaty work in the blistering heat of summer. But the money was good. I learned a lot about the nature of people through my customer relationships.
Some were cheap, always requesting extra work without extra pay.
Others were generous to the bone, always paying a fair price and offering me refreshments while I worked. My best customer was
Mrs. Ethyl Cooner, who paid top dollar and always tipped. This seemed like a strange practice, but I was always grateful, and I
respected this generous woman until the day she died. Lawn service was also an experience that taught me a lot about mechanics. Lawn mowers were invariably unreliable in those days, and ours seemed to be particularly cranky. By necessity, I learned about spark plugs and carburetors, and o ften found myself crouched under a shade tree, changing blades, taking engines apart and replacing rings and bearings.
So much for entrepreneurial ventures. By age fifteen, I had moved on to regular employment. Mother was working full time for
H.W. Lunceford in his bargain stores, so I began working with her on Saturdays, their busiest days and my day o ff from school. When summer arrived, I began working full time. I never worked outside the home on weekdays during the school year, although I had plenty of responsibilities at home, as I mentioned earlier. Since Mother worked fulltime, I cooked a full dinner practically every evening,
did most of the washing and ironing for the family and much of the cleaning. Sandy helped, but she was two and a half years younger and thus carried a somewhat reduced responsibility at the time.
I did a little of everything at Lunceford’s Bargain Store. I
unpacked and organized merchandise, waited on customers, swept the floors, kept the place clean and orderly, counted the money,
made the bank deposits, painted sale signs and did whatever else needed to be done. It was truly a learning factory for me. H.W. liked me and continued to dish out new responsibilities over time. He had an old green Plymouth, with loudspeakers mounted on the top,
that he used to drive around and advertise the store. Before my first summer ended, he had me driving the Plymouth and pitching store sales over the loudspeaker. I drove all over town and all through the cotton fields trying to roust people out of their houses or get the attention of the field hands.
I remember that during my second summer, a salesman came through town with a truckload of surplus merchandise, including some cheap umbrellas made in Japan. He said he had a “gross dozen”
(a new term for me) that he would sell us for $5 a dozen for as few or as many as we wanted. I made a rapid calculation and suggested to H.W., “Let’s offer him $400 for the whole shipment. ” H.W. asked me if I knew how many umbrellas were in a gross dozen, and I boldly told him 1,728 (reasoning that a gross was 144, so a gross dozen must be 144 x 12). He reflected for a moment, smiled wryly and then said, “Let’s do it. ” I especially remember the exhilarating feeling I got from the “let’s” in his comment. He was treating me like a partner!
After some mild haggling, we finally settled on $432 for several huge boxes of umbrellas, which amounted to exactly $0.25 each. We split the shipment between stores in Calhoun City, Vardaman, Bruce and Houston, and priced the umbrellas at $1. They weren’t great umbrellas, but they were certainly worth a dollar, I thought. They looked good—red, blue, yellow and green plaids—and they worked,
at least initially. I painted large signs and we left instructions at each store to position the umbrellas at the front door any time it rained.
They went like hotcakes! By the end of the summer, we had sold the entire lot at $1 apiece, for a whopping profit of almost $1,300. H.W.
loved to tell the umbrella story, claiming he didn’t know what a gross dozen was and bragging about me talking him into the deal.
One day the next summer, H.W. walked into the Calhoun City store, handed me a set of keys and said, “Keith, I want you to run the Houston store this summer. ” Just like that. So, the next day there
I was in Houston, Mississippi at eight a.m., unlocking the front door of Lunceford’s Bargain Store and opening up for business. I
worked there six days a week all summer with a lady helper and full responsibility for everything. H.W. would call occasionally to see how things were going and he would send fresh merchandise from time to time, but he never once came to the store. Looking back on it, the level of con fidence he placed in a seventeen-year-old kid is almost incredible. It’s just as incredible how much a seventeen-year-old kid can learn in an environment like that in a couple of months.
My Lunceford Bargain store career ended when I went o ff to college. H.W. actually tried to talk me out of college and offered me a percentage of his business if I would stay and work with him. I was honored and humbled by his con fidence and generosity, but my head was in a different place.
At the time, I felt as if I had been missing out on a lot of life,
working every Saturday and six days a week during the summer all through high school. In retrospect, those were some of the most fruitful days of my life. I could probably fill an entire book with anecdotes from those years. And, in reality, I probably didn’t miss out on as much as it seemed at the time. I was always allowed time off for band trips, Boy Scout events and other things.
T oday I am extremely thankful for these experiences and grateful for the leadership of my parents in guiding me in these directions.
These experiences take me up to my college days. When I le ft home for college, I le ft with a virtual career under my belt. I had experience in manual labor, office work, entrepreneurship, retailing,
store management and the solid values that come from hard work and responsibility.
I worked each summer while I was in college. The first summer
I worked for H.W. ’s brother Rupert in his new grocery store, with a smaller second job of handling insurance paper work for Dr. Durbo
Harrison, a new young physician in town. The second year I worked for Harry Robinson in the M.M. Winkler & Associates CPA firm in Bruce. I spent the third summer in New Orleans on the internal audit staff of Humble Oil & Refining Co.
I hit the ground running in college, bu ttressed with an outstanding public school education and a fully developed work ethic that most people do not have the opportunity to see at such an early age. I was fortunate in these respects. The lessons learned, both at school and at work, would serve me well in the years ahead.
