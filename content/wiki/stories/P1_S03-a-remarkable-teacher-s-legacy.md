# A Remarkable Teacher’s Legacy

## Full Text

A Remarkable
T eacher’s Legacy
Calhoun City is a back woods farming community with limited opportunity for young people who want to reach out beyond the basics. In the midst of this environment, there was a gentle, erudite woman who taught English to the local students for decades. Rather quiet and serious, with a dab of wit about her, Miss Frances Mallory lived a simple life in this rural community. She never married. Family and friends surrounded her, and she had an abiding allegiance to her students. She dabbled in oil painting, but never displayed much of her work. Legend has it she once wrote a novel, but no one seems to have ever seen or read it. She possessed a voracious appetite as a reader and was a student of classical literature, rather rare traits in this community.
Miss Frances lived at 400 North Jackson Street, just one block from Calhoun City High School. Her 1905 home was one of a handful of original residences in the town. Her family owned the property for seventy-five years, and she lived there throughout that period. Like Willy Loman, she “never made a lot of money” and “her name was never in the paper” . She lived a private life, one that was dedicated almost entirely to her students.
Miss Frances’ life was dominated by an unusual intellectual curiosity, continually poking around like a thorn looking for a so ft spot. She managed to arouse in me, and probably in many others,
some of that same curiosity. T o some, she undoubtedly was nothing more than a stern task master who made unreasonable demands and passed out stiff grading sentences for substandard performances. She was tough; there was no doubt about it. Many former students can remember sitting on her front porch (voluntarily or involuntarily),
while Miss Frances sat on the porch swing, providing private tutoring. Perhaps a few of them despised her, but the overwhelming majority recognized her innate fairness and her genuine caring. They also respected her good-natured goading and calm firmness. She once said to me, upon handing back a poor grade, “I’m not going to tell your mother about this, Keith, because you and I both know you can do be tter. ” She had a knack for reasoned argument, as well as for personal humiliation, that made it difficult to quibble with her verdicts. For me, there was more than ample motivation to meet her expectations.
It was Miss Frances who introduced me to memorization. She believed in memorization, and it was an annual April ritual for the senior English class. Learning to recite the prologue to Chaucer’s
Canterbury T ales in Old English was a requirement, which seemed to most students to be a ridiculous exercise in futility and a cause for great consternation. After all, when would one ever find such an abstruse thing useful? Despite all the groaning, Miss Frances was brutal in her demands that each student stand before the class and recite this meaningless collection of arcane words. As testimony to the stickiness of this exercise, at the fiftieth reunion of the 1959
graduating class, a half-dozen students stood in the living room of
Miss Frances Mallory the Mallory House and jointly recited from memory the first twenty lines of this work—in Old English no less—without a single hiccup!
“Whan that Aprill with his shoures soote, the droghte of march hath perced to the roote, and bathed every veyne in swich licour… ” .
Upon her death in 1994, her home, which was her only asset of any signi ficant value, was donated to the First Baptist Church.
Fred Nabors, a local builder and long time friend, subsequently acquired the property in 1998 and meticulously restored it to its original appearance. On an impulse of nostalgia, I purchased the property from Fred and vowed to keep it as a historical memory of this remarkable lady. I think I sort of looked upon myself as a metaphorical Clym Y eobright (in Return of the Native) reconnecting with my roots. The home stands today in pristine condition complete with white picket fence, rose bushes and a manicured lawn. It would qualify as a cover picture for Life magazine. Although the Cobbs have owned it as a second home since 1998, it appropriately sports a bronze plaque labeled “Mallory House” .
Miss Frances was quietly buried in the local cemetery. Her family never acquired a grave marker for it. A few years later, a group of loyal, respectful alumni, admirers of the legendary teacher, chipped in to fund a gravestone. The inscription is telling:
Honorary gravestone for Miss Frances
We truly treasured her legacy. In addition to working together to fund the gravestone, we launched a campaign to establish a scholarship fund in her memory. Those funds now reside with the
Create Community Foundation, which administers the scholarship program. Perhaps this fund will continue to enable a few local students to benefit from this outstanding woman’s legacy.
For me, Miss Frances was indeed remarkable. I have bene fitted from the solid grounding of her tutelage for the past fifty-plus years.
She has prodded me and nudged me for years, in more ways than fundamental English grammar and composition. She opened my mind to countless new thoughts as well as new words. She motivated me to aspire and look beyond my immediate environment, to wonder what might be out there on the other side. I remember sitting in the porch swing (which I still do, from time to time) wishing that I could be as knowledgeable and worldly as she was. Actually, I also longed to be as firm and tough as she was, because I admired her resolve and determination. Without a doubt, I am a better and more responsible person because of her leadership.

## Principles

- High standards and disciplined practice produce lasting knowledge and character.
- Private, firm encouragement motivates improvement more effectively than public scolding.
- Consistent intellectual curiosity and exposure to literature expand students' horizons in limited-opportunity settings.
- Communities can preserve and extend a leader's legacy through collective action (memorials and scholarships).

## Decision heuristics

- When a student underperforms, address them privately and set a higher expectation instead of immediately escalating to caregivers.
- Implement rigorous, repeatable practice rituals to create durable learning (e.g., annual memorization exercises).
- When honoring a mentor, mobilize peers and alumni to fund tangible memorials and scholarships to continue their impact.
