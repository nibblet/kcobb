# Charles T. Brumfield

> A reflection on a fifty-year friendship between the narrator and Charles Brumfield, tracing their college collaboration, long correspondence, pranks, and Brumfield’s adventurous, cosmopolitan life.

## Full Text

Charles T . Brumfield
Two unlikely kindred spirits stumbled onto the campus of
Mississippi Southern College in the autumn of 1959, neither of them realizing the presence of the other. One was from the Biloxi-
Gulfport area on the Gulf of Mexico, the other from the red clay hills of Calhoun County in the northern part of the state. They hailed from di fferent backgrounds and sported di fferent personalities.
Both, however, were naïve to many of the ways of the world, but eager to learn. Somehow their paths crossed during that freshman year, just how and when is unrecorded or unremembered by either.
Their friendship has lasted for more than a half century.
Charlie Brumfield was a quiet sort of guy who kept pretty much to himself in those early days, but had a familiarity with a few other freshmen from the Gulf Coast area, which gave him a modicum of a built-in network. I, on the other hand, was a lone import from the north, knew no one, and had to quickly begin to make friends in my dormitory and on campus. We both were assigned to live in Forest
Hall, a three-story brick dormitory that was one of the original buildings on the campus. As time passed that freshman year, it turned out that some of our friends were the same. Whatever the catalyst, Keith and Charlie somehow befriended each other. Along the way, we joined forces working in the Student Government
Association and eventually found ourselves taking courses in the business school. By the time our senior year rolled around, we were rooming together in Bond Hall.
As I wrote earlier, I was a busy guy in college, active in numerous organizations and heavily engaged in student politics. Charlie was less busy and involved than I was. However, he was considerably more thoughtful and studious, and he served as a guiding compass for me much of the time. We both found ourselves involved in student politics and we shared similar outlooks on the welfare of the student body. When I was elected president of the SGA, I
immediately appointed Charlie as my administrative assistant. We made a great team. It seemed as though I was always on the stage and he was always in the wings, keeping my head straight. He seemed to like it better that way than if he had been in the spotlight. We werevery different individuals in many respects, but we shared a mutual admiration for each other.
When Dot and I began to plan our wedding, my choice for best man was a slam dunk. It was to be Charlie Brumfield, my best friend.
I’ve never had a better friend. We have remained close and stayed in touch, despite the physical distance between us.
Being best friends did not preclude a bit of horseplay and practical joking from time to time. I recall one example of a time when I probably stepped over the line. Charlie was still in graduate school at USM. He had been the president of the Y oung Republicans
Club in college and was a fervent Republican devotee. (One would never recognize that radical conservative leaning today!) When Paul
Johnson was a Democratic candidate for governor of Mississippi, I
sat down and wrote a letter to his campaign office, pledging support for his campaign and forging Charlie’s signature. I volunteered, on
Charlie’s behalf, to work on the Johnson campaign and requested literature for distribution. Charlie was immediately bombarded with cases of bumper stickers, brochure mailers, signs and other campaign paraphernalia. Indignant and smelling a rat, he called me to protest and complain. In response, I wrote a follow-up letter to the Johnson headquarters saying that I had used up all the materials and requesting another supply. The box arrived at Charlie’s place promptly. I’m still laughing. And I am sure Charlie is still complaining!
After receiving his master’s degree in economics at USM, Charlie went on to achieve a doctorate in economics at the University of
South Carolina. Y ears later, he wrote a book about economic theory,
Basic Macroeconomic Equilibrium Analysis . Although I don’t think he ever published it, I seem to recall that he did submit it to some publishers. He sent me a copy of the manuscript, which I still have stowed away somewhere. The book’s contents were well over my head, and I never did anything with it except scan it and gloss over the chapters—and I did take it upon myself to write a faux New York
Times Book Review-style critique lampooning it. If I must say so, the review was well done. Satirizing the book, I likened it to an a ttempt to prove Fermat’s theorem and wrote, “Brumfield seems to be trying to prove scientifically that nature will take its course —if not in the short run, then certainly in the long run. Wow!” I even went to the trouble of printing it with standard New York Times Book Review section columns and fonts to make it look authentic. Although he never admitted it, I think I might have had him going for a short while. I still have that book review in my bulging file of Charlie mementos.
Such was some of the fun we had together over the years.
While working in Germany in the early years, Charlie met and married a woman named Margot. That relationship foundered after a few years, but in the meantime they had a terri fic son they named
Chuck. Chuck became the joy of Charlie’s life. Charlie moved to
California to be with him, and essentially raised him over a period of about twenty years; Margot was around in the background, but it was
Charlie who provided the critical parenting, it seemed to me. Chuck spent some time as a radio disc jockey in the San Francisco area and is now an executive in the music industry and quite successful.
Charlie taught at the university level in South Carolina and
California. He also had two long stints in a relationship with the
University of Maryland’s overseas extension service. In that capacity,
he lived and taught all over the world. I have lost count, but he has probably lived in twenty different countries over the years and most likely has visited well over one hundred.
Sometime during Chuck’s teenage years, Charlie and he acquired a dog they named Dum Dum. Somehow Chuck managed to teach Dum Dum to “talk” , muttering phrases that were intelligible,
albeit barely so. They gained some notoriety with this talking dog and eventually finagled an appearance on the Johnny Carson Show in Hollywood. So here they were, with Dum Dum talking on nationwide television. It was hilarious! Eventually, Dum Dum died.
Charlie, being the eccentric he was becoming by that time, had Dum
Dum freeze-dried and preserved in a natural state. I actually saw the frozen dog in the trunk of his car during one of my trips to California.
Spooky. I’m guessing the dog is still sitting, all posed and relaxed, in some storage facility somewhere in California.
Following college, Charlie and I launched a correspondence that has lasted for more than fifty years. Sometimes the communications were quite regular; at other times they were spo tty. Usually,
particularly in the earlier years, the letters back and forth were filled with repartee. Our political views were normally at opposite ends of the spectrum, and we reveled in the opportunity to parody each other’s views. The spoofs were o ften sharp-tongued, but we both understood the good nature of the exchange. At one point, Charlie crafted a compendium of cut-and-pasted excerpts from my le tters over the years and bound them up in a book-like folder. As could be expected, the excerpts were taken out of context and cleverly constructed to present me in a ridiculous light. I still have copies of all of his letters stored away in my attic. Perhaps someday I’ll do my own cut-and-paste job!
Charlie and I have sparred over the years about politics. Generally speaking, I have followed a traditional conservative pathway, and
Charlie has leaned to the left. Notwithstanding the polarized politics and in a more serious vein, we have always shared a common set of fundamental values, which has probably been the bedrock of our friendship. We have both valued honesty and integrity and have respected the people we have known. My sense of right and wrong has almost always dovetailed with Charlie’s.
Our face-to-face visits over the years have been short and infrequent. Charlie has visited our home a few times. He drove clear across the country once, in his old beat-up Volkswagen van, to visit with our family on vacation in Nantucket. He also was kind and thoughtful enough to a ttend Paul’s wedding in Arizona. He always brought gifts on his visits, which drove me crazy. I didn’t need any gifts. But that was Charlie! Dot and I visited him once in California.
Once when I was in Berlin on business with Alamo Rent A Car, I
notified Charlie, who travelled all the way from somewhere in the
Netherlands to enable us to spend a couple of days together. Dot and
I met him in Brussels once and spent a few hours visiting, before we had to head back to London.
Charlie has been a full-time vagabond, wandering the globe for more than fifty years. He is the consummate cosmopolitan.
Germany, Belgium, South Korea, Okinawa and Thailand are just a few of the many places he has lived. He has visited every major edifice in the world. He’s climbed Mt. Kilimanjaro, has dived in waters everywhere, ridden horses through Mongolia and done so many other adventurous things that they are too numerous to recite. Indeed, it has been a full and glamorous life, at least from my perspective. He never seems to tire of adventure. A few years ago,
well past age sixty, he fell in love and married a young Thai woman in her twenties. I hope she loves adventure! What a guy!
For as long as I have known Charlie, he has never been focused on material things. He lives for the moment and seems to be interested in accumulating only what he might need for the next moment. He occasionally grumbles about being poor and financially inadequate,
but I have always known that his grumbling is just a front. He is secretly proud of his independence and his ability to survive amidst all the adventure of his fun-loving existence.
Charlie claims that he admires my life and all of the things I have done over the years. I certainly have admired his. He has been the
Indiana Jones that I, like many men, have only dreamed about. He has marched to his own drummer and done his own thing, with little or no ambition for wealth and fame, or even personal security. He’s just been Charlie, roaming the world in search of adventure and the personal satisfaction that come from being a professor and teacher. I
have truly admired those characteristics and count myself extremely fortunate to have known him as a friend. Members of his family refer to him as T ommy. Most of his friends and associates call him
Charles. T o me, he will always be Charlie, best friend forever.
Charlie has indeed been an unusual kindred spirit for a very long time. Whenever I think of all the things in the world that I am grateful for, I put Charles T . Brumfield at or near the top of the list.
