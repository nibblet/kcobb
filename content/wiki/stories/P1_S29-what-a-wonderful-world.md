# What a Wonderful World

## Full Text

What a Wonderful
World
As I stood a few years ago in the doorways of cold and desolate barracks at Auschwitz and Dachau concentration camps in Poland and Germany, sharp tingles shot up my spine. I knew the horrid stories and could describe the circumstances intellectually. But honestly, I didn’t really know the stories. As the expression goes,
seeing is believing. Only by walking through those gruesome venues can one actually truly understand the Holocaust that occurred there more than seventy years ago. Only then could I wrap my head around the plight of those six million Jews and what that all means to society today.
So goes the story of my extensive travels over the years. Seeing has been believing —and understanding. First-hand observation has brought our complex world into a sharper focus for me. I am immensely fortunate to have had those first-hand opportunities.
Dot and I have travelled throughout our adult lives. We started early on with domestic vacation trips, primarily exploring the southern United States, close to our home. We gradually expanded our national reach, ultimately accomplishing a goal of placing our feet on the ground in all fifty states.
The last leg of this protracted domestic expedition was an impromptu visit in 2012 to our last two remaining states to visit —
Nebraska and Iowa. While visiting our son Mark in Denver, we spontaneously changed our travel plans. We rented a car and struck out on this diversion. Along the way, as we gazed over the beautiful western plains, we decided to play a road trip game, just for the fun of it. We challenged each other to describe a speci fic memory from each state we had visited. We both performed that feat without a blip as the miles rolled by, recalling years of experiences as if watching a television travelogue.
Wonderful travel memories have lingered with us over all these years. On this short visit, they were as vivid as the sun on a bright summer day. Our conversation became increasingly more fascinating as the recollections poured out. We described the scores of national parks we have visited, from Acadia in Maine to Y ellowstone in
Wyoming to Carlsbad Caverns in New Mexico. We reminisced about Christmas with our family at Williamsburg and memorable
July 4th celebrations in Manchester, Vermont and Nantucket. A
road trip around the perimeters of the Great Lakes was especially unforgettable. We later made another two-week road trip out west,
where we meticulously followed the trail of the 1804–1806 Lewis and Clark Expedition from Bismarck, North Dakota over the Rocky
Mountains to the Pacific Ocean at Portland, Oregon. We recollected our exciting experiences in many of the great cities of America —
New Y ork, Chicago, San Francisco, Boston, Washington, D.C., et al.
We talked about great hotels and quaint bed and breakfast inns we have visited throughout the country. We talked about visits to our children in all the places they have lived (Arizona, T exas, Arkansas,
New Jersey, Illinois, Colorado, Virginia and Washington, D.C.)
as well as all the states where we have lived (Mississippi, Florida,
Maryland and Pennsylvania). We’ve seen so much. We’ve travelled by air, by car, by motor home and by boat, and we’ve seen the mountains, the prairies, the rivers and rapids, the lakes, the parks,
the forests, wine country, nature’s oddities, the cities, the farms, the monuments and shrines, and the highways and byways from sea to shining sea.
With these panoptic travels, we have sensed the rhythm and vibrancy of our country and have come to better appreciate its history and its grandeur. No amount of reading Fodor’s travel guides or taking formal educational courses could have provided a substitute for these learning experiences. Not even National Geographic magazine could reproduce what we have seen and experienced. We thoroughly understand why the United States is the greatest country in the world, largely because we’ve seen it up close and personal with our own eyes. We have touched it and felt it. We have breathed its air from corner to corner. It’s an old friend. We feel like we know it, and know it well. And we like what we have seen.
A similar thing could be said about our international travels, but in a slightly different context. We have now travelled to over seventy-five countries, crossing all seven continents. We rounded out our continental bucket list in 2014 with a memorable expedition to
Antarctica.
Our first international trip was to Switzerland and Austria in the
1970s. I’m not sure why we picked these countries as a starting place for our international wanderlust. But for whatever reason, we laid out our travel plans without the aid of a travel agent (and well before the Internet) and struck out on a two-week journey to Europe,
leaving our children behind with a reliable caretaker. Looking back,
it was a rather daring move for a young couple recently relocated from Mississippi and with virtually no experience in the wily ways of the world. It was an incredible trip, with vivid experiences that are still ingrained in our memories. We visited all the major cities of these two countries and also took a trip to idyllic Zerma tt,
Switzerland, in the Alps. Among our favorite memories are seeing the Ma tterhorn, Oberammergau (in Bavaria, Germany, another side trip), visiting Mozart’s birthplace in Salzburg and a ttending a thrilling performance in the Grand Opera House at Vienna. Dot still has some jewelry that we purchased in Geneva, and some forty years later I’m still telling tall tales about that remarkable adventure.
That first excursion outside the United States whe tted our appetite for seeing more of the world. It ignited a fire within us for the next four decades. We would read or hear about a place or event and say to each other, “Let’s go!”
Another early trip was to China. My interest was piqued while reading about President Richard Nixon’s visit to China in 1972,
breaking down international barriers and normalizing relations with the outside world. I read the reports and mused, “I would like to see that for myself. ” Not too long after that, we booked a cruise with
Norwegian Cruise Lines and visited Beijing, Shanghai and a number of smaller cities and locales along the eastern seaboard. What an eye opener for us! We stood on the Great Wall in total amazement,
wandered through the Forbidden City, visited a common working family in their modest apartment in Dalian, saw thousands of terracotta soldiers at Xian, ate the crummy food and gawked at the millions of bicycles (only to be gawked at in return by the Chinese people). It was truly a world that we had never imagined. Until we saw all of these things with our own eyes, China had been a veritable dreamy fairytale to us. Not actually real. But now, suddenly, we had the picture in vivid T echnicolor. It was indeed real. And tangible.
And fascinating, to say the least!
Exactly twenty years later, we made a return trip and were equally amazed. The changes in the country were incredible beyond description. Glass and steel skyscrapers had magically replaced the old terracotta brick buildings in Beijing. Shanghai looked like Dallas on steroids, with buildings that were even bigger, taller, shinier and more luxurious. Cars, trucks and busses jammed the overcrowded streets. The bicycles were nowhere to be seen. High technology abounded and the public infrastructure was remarkably advanced.
Most people who interfaced with tourists spoke English—excellent
English. All of the school children even seemed to speak English.
What an amazing transformation in twenty short years! Despite all we could read about China, we would never have understood and appreciated the progress and momentum occurring there without these two polarizing visits. The indelible impressions will be with us forever.
In stark contrast, there are other wonderful places in the world that have not been tarnished by such progress and modern development. They have stood virtually unchanged for centuries.
Seeing those places and standing in their shadows caused history to come alive for me. Examples of these timeless places are the pyramids of Egypt, Machu Picchu in Peru, Angkor Wat in Cambodia and Petra in Jordan.
They are venues that originated thousands of years ago and where time has seemingly stood still. As I stood before these and other great monuments to human ingenuity, I scratched my head in bewilderment. How did this come to be? Why? How did such beauty and scale develop without the aid of modern equipment and technology? Who was responsible for leading such developments?
How did they do it? How many lives were sacrificed in the endeavor?
How has society managed to preserve it? And so on with an endless series of unanswered questions. Somehow history texts and colorful coffee table books do not capture the answers to these questions.
Nor do television documentaries. Although answers to my nagging questions were not always forthcoming, just being there in the midst of such history left deep impressions.
Dot and I are o ften asked about the best place we have ever visited. Both our answers are always the same, without hesitation:
Israel. We have been there twice —once on a two-week religious/
cultural tour and another on a three-day trip, where we were just passing through and enjoying the countryside. A fter spending a virtual lifetime thinking I was learning about the land where God planted his seed, it was only by going there and visiting the sites did it actually begin to take on a real and physical meaning. It is hard to imagine an agnostic spending any time there and not scratching his or her head at the wonder of all the imbedded history. The ground is hallowed; there can be no doubt about it. There is no other place on earth like Israel.
Much of our worldwide travel has been independently arranged by us or spent on cruise ships. Organized tour groups have never appealed to us, due to what we see as con fining rigidity and structure. We have found cruising to have a lot of advantages. The accommodations are always comfortable, and one covers a lot of
“ground” with little itinerary management and luggage schlepping.
As has been said, you check in and the destinations come to you!
We have travelled from time to time with friends, sometimes with mixed results. We have done several cruises with the Weils —
Sherra and Michael —our terri fic friends and marvelous travelling companions. Like us, they enjoy flexibility and independence when exploring the world. Who could ever ask for two more compatible and fun people with whom to share the sites of the world?
Our cruises have taken us to Scandinavia, the Mediterranean,
Europe, South America, China, Southeast Asia, Africa, Alaska and of course various venues in the Caribbean. We’ve travelled with Crystal
Cruises, Silversea Cruises, Royal Caribbean, Princess Cruise Lines and other minor lines. They’re all great! We have strolled the streets of virtually every signi ficant city in Europe over the years, enjoying the special charm and character of each. My personal favorites have included the usual high-pro file venues —Paris, Rome, Florence,
Madrid, Vienna, et al. But just as interesting and educational have been some locales that are not as famous, among them Hamburg,
Munich, Geneva, Krakow, Budapest, Lisbon, Copenhagen, Oslo and Brussels. We spent five weeks in Italy in 2014, roaming Tuscany and the Lake Como areas and enjoying the marvelous Italian lifestyle. Europe is truly a special part of the world. So rich in history and culture. So much to learn. We have enjoyed the architecture and scenery, wonderful museums, great food, unusual customs and charming people. With all that said, I do believe we have seen enough Catholic churches to last us for the rest of our lives!
Michael and Sherra Weil, the world’s best travelling buddies
We have also visited several quaint, o ff-the-beaten-path places all over the world. Examples would be Estonia in the Baltic, the
Seychelles Islands in the Indian Ocean, San Miguel de Allende,
buried in the highlands of central Mexico, and T ogo on the west coast of Africa.
One of my choice experiences was a casual two-week trip to
Ireland and Scotland. Dot and I rented a car in Dublin and spent ten days meandering around the countryside with no pre-established plans other than to implant ourselves in the everyday lives of the Irish. We navigated the narrow roads from village to village,
spending our days enjoying the scenic beauty of the emerald countryside and its people. Most of our evenings were spent in small bed and breakfast inns that we booked day by day as we moved along the byways. On a couple of occasions, we stayed in old converted castles. We o ften stopped on the roadside and talked with local people and wandered into classic Irish pubs and cha tted with the customers, actually experiencing first-hand the conviviality and gift of gab for which the Irish are so famous. The experience was authentic, in every sense of the word. Real people living real lives.
Unpretentious and genuine. We le ft Ireland on a high note and then migrated over to Scotland to do a similar thing. The climate and atmosphere in Scotland were different, but we felt the same way about the culture. We gained a new appreciation for these people—
their history, their spirit, their attitudes, essentially their love of life.
One doesn’t learn about such things by staying at home.
And then there have been several ventures with just the guys—
simply fun trips. Many of these were deep sea fishing expeditions,
some productive and some not so successful. I’ve owned several boats over the years and have fished the waters of South Florida and the Bahamas. My good friend Pat Kelley and I signed up for a big fishing tournament in Bimini in 1982. We chartered the Happy
Day T oday, out of Fort Lauderdale, with a captain and mate and got ourselves all fitted out for the occasion. We even had a writer and photographer from World Magazine on board. On the last afternoon of three days’ fishing, we hooked up on the big one —a huge blue marlin estimated at six hundred pounds. Massive jaws lurched out of the ocean just behind our boat and gobbled a large red artificial lure.
Pandemonium set in immediately. Our large Penn 80 reel squealed away as the giant marlin spun the line almost down to the core. After battling for about a half hour, our line mysteriously snapped and we lost it. It was a huge disappointment. Aside from the thrill of it all,
we would have won the $15,000 grand prize in the tournament. But
I have some fabulous photo memoirs of the event, including several with our giant marlin’s tail dancing in the ocean!
I have also made several trips to Costa Rica, fishing the bountiful waters of the Paci fic. My son Mark and I went there together once and had an incredible experience. We landed several sail fish and rare roosterfish. One day, fishing in a dilapidated twenty- five-foot charter boat, we hooked a 450-pound black marlin. We fought it together for two and a half hours, finally bringing it alongside our boat to share in the exhaustion. We released him, of course. What an incredible moment—both catching and releasing this giant animal!
Those were the days! I have now sold my last boat and fish only occasionally these days.
A short domestic trip in 2013 has added even additional spice to the diversity of my travels. The trip was short —five days—but the experience was up close and personal. I joined a group of twelve people from our church on a humanitarian expedition to Moore,
Oklahoma to assist in the disaster cleanup following a record-shattering tornado. There I came face-to-face with the incredible destruction that nature’s wrath can bring on a community.
Unfathomable devastation. At the same time, I witnessed the faith,
determination, generosity and loving spirit of the American people.
All in one place. A local church transformed itself overnight into a disaster recovery center, and people miraculously appeared from all over the United States with helping hands and generous donations.
Our group rebuilt a fence and a front porch, and collected tons of debris from destroyed neighborhoods. Relative to the magnitude of the ruin, the products of our labor were miniscule. However, we felt wonderful knowing that we were able to help, if only in a small way.
I had seen all of the television coverage of the ravages of the tornado.
But…the point of including this trip in my travelogue is to once again underscore the fact that no amount of description or news coverage is a fitting substitute for being there live and in person.
There is a colloquial expression in the business world called
“MBWA”—management by wandering around. This, of course,
refers to knowledge that comes from first-hand observance of a business operation as contrasted to memos, meetings and manuals.
That thought captures much of what Dot and I have experienced in our travels over the years. We have learned so much about our world simply by wandering around, stumbling about in nooks and crannies everywhere, and absorbing what we saw. It has allowed us to be connected to our wonderful country and the world in ways we never could have achieved if we had remained closeted in our cocoon. We have witnessed the trappings of the rich and the poor of the world (e.g., the Palace of Versailles contrasted with the slums of Cairo) and we have seen the spectacular dichotomy of the old and the new (e.g., the ancient temples of Cambodia’s Angkor Wat vs. glistening modern Singapore —not so far apart, these two). We have gazed in wonder at the grandeur of the natural and the man-made (e.g., the Grand Canyon and Mount Rushmore). We’ve seen mountains, rivers, canyons, oceans, beaches, forests, deserts and glaciers. Y ou name it; it seems as though we’ve seen it.
Along the way, we have carted home a lot of stu ff. Most of it we have classi fied in the category of “J&S” (you can guess what that stands for) and it has gradually disappeared. But a few things remain with us as prize remembrances of our travels and tangible linkages to the places we have been. We have a set of two beautiful white marble fish on our patio that we purchased spontaneously in a marble quarry in Vietnam. I stood in the hot sun and watched a frail old Vietnamese man toiling away on the fish with a crude hammer and chisel. I was moved both by the beauty of the fish and the raw talent of this old man. We cherish an incredible piece of Chinese embroidery that hangs above our grand piano; it is a meticulous copy of a famous Chen Yifei oil painting, and reportedly took a Chinese woman twenty-two months to weave. These mementos remind us of the talents and skills that are tucked away in remote places, places we don’t always think of with respect. They serve to keep us connected to our experiences. I’m not sure why I ever thought I needed a didgeridoo carved and painted by the Aborigines in Australia, but I
have one sitting in my study!
So, where do we go from here? There are still a number of choice places on our list. These include Brazil and India, the two largest countries that we have not visited. Both have a lot to offer in education and enchantment, and we’ll probably make it to both of them sometime in the future. I also have a hankering to revisit some of our earlier destinations, such as Switzerland and Austria. Other than London, where we’ve been several times, we haven’t been to many places more than once. We think it might be time for a few revisits.
Of all the things we’ve experienced in our more than fifty years of marriage —places we have lived, people we have known, jobs,
organizations, trials and tribulations, etc.—our travels are among the most memorable and rewarding experiences, providing insights into our world and enriching our lives immeasurably. They have allowed us to stand in the unique doorways of history and the contemporary world, and to view them through special lenses. Besides, we have enjoyed ourselves immensely.
