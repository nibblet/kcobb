## Full Text

Launching a Career
This vignette is included in the memoir collection because of its pivotal influence on me. Forks in the road define life’s journey, with the choices made having a dramatic in fluence on one’s entire life.
Such was the case as I stood at a set of crossroads in the final three months of 1962.
In the midst of all the important distractions of my senior year at USM, I found myself facing a lot of di fficult questions with not-so-obvious answers. What should I do when this is all over? Where should I launch my career? Where are the best opportunities? How should I evaluate career opportunities in relation to other personal goals? Where should we live? How can I best provide for a wife and family? And on and on.
In the fall of that year, I paid a visit to the placement office on campus and consulted with Dorothy Lenoir, the director there. She advised me to sign up for interviews with a variety of companies scheduled to visit the campus. So I did. I registered for interviews with all the Big Eight accounting firms and IBM, which seemed the most interesting. For good measure and comparison, I also signed up to see a few other companies, including State Farm Insurance,
Humble Oil and Refining and a couple of banks.
Initially, I was apprehensive about the interviews. These were big firms represented by mature, well-dressed and articulate executives who had the power over my future in their grasp. The interviews would be critically important. I couldn’t afford to stub my toe and I
was naturally nervous. The campus interviews were typically a half-hour, and the recruiters were well prepared —they generally knew all about me before I walked into the room. Importantly, most of them were skillful at pu tting students like me at ease. Much to my surprise, I realized that that the recruiters were in a selling mode and spent most of their allotted time trying to persuade me that the opportunities with their particular firm were my best option.
I had not expected that. I had underestimated the value of the credentials I had built up. All of the firms placed a high value on my academic record and campus leadership. So, while I had embarked on the project of meeting with various firms’ representatives thinking
I was looking for a job, I suddenly found myself in the driver’s seat.
I set aside my apprehensions and wended my way through the brochures and recruiters, trying to sort out my best overall career opportunity.
Conscientious about being careful and deliberate, I made a list of the criteria that I deemed important. The list was typical. It included assessments of the overall reputation of each firm, its size,
number of offices, any specific industry concentration or specialties,
and other demographics. I was especially interested in advancement opportunities—the pace of upward movement of the firms’ young professionals. On a more subjective side, I decided to look hard at their people and their clients.
My bias was toward the public accounting firms, inasmuch as
I had been majoring in accounting for four years. I had worked in two summer internships in that field—one for M.M. Winkler &
Associates, a CPA firm in Tupelo, and one on the internal audit staff of Humble Oil & Refining Company in New Orleans. All of the Big
Eight accounting firms were prestigious and offered excellent career advancement opportunities. They were the crème-de-la-crème of the professional world at that time. Their starting salaries ranged from
$500 to $550 per month. And they all seemed like a good fit for me.
Then IBM threw a monkey wrench into my thought process.
During the first few minutes of the campus interview, the recruiter made an on-the-spot offer for me to join them in their Jackson office at a starting salary of $600, with a guarantee of $10,000 annually in two years. He said that IBM had authorized only two o ffers for
USM students. The other o ffer was going to Linda Escobedo, a straight-A math major from Ha ttiesburg and a friend of mine. The difference in compensation between this o ffer and the low end of the public accounting firms was 20 percent. Moreover, IBM’s fringe benefits were far superior. Economics signi ficantly complicated my decision process! I remember observing that all the IBM recruiters
I’d seen over the years werevery tall men —invariably well over six feet. I wondered about this. Seriously, I worried about it! But then
I reasoned that Linda, who was about five feet two inches, certainly didn’t meet the perceived criteria that an IBM executive be a man who is at least six feet tall. In due course, I was able to put short-term monetary considerations and peculiar physical observations aside and concentrate on the professional aspects of my opportunities. I
made a decision to narrow my selection to the accounting firms.
All of the accounting firms invited me to visit their offices. In
November and December of 1962, I made several trips to New
Orleans, visiting Arthur Andersen, Haskins & Sells, Ernst & Ernst,
Peat Marwick Mitchell & Co., and Price Waterhouse. I visited
Arthur Y oung in Houston. I had already dismissed T ouche Ross and Lybrand as candidates and therefore didn’t visit them. Each of the companies I visited was impressive, to say the least. All the partners and managers I met were extremely professional. They were sincere, articulate, well dressed and urbane. They reeked of success and competence. As I sat in their plush offices, all of them in high-rise buildings, I could dreamily envision myself as one of them.
I was quite taken by them! Their client lists were impressive, too,
mostly recognizable large companies in a wide variety of industries.
Their policies and terms of employment were similar, with only a few unremarkable di fferences. I had a di fficult time distinguishing between them.
I was probably the most impressed with Arthur Y oung, which was somewhat smaller than the other firms and had fewer offices,
but seemed a bit more sophisticated and classy. They treated me royally during my visit to Houston, made an a ttractive offer during the visit and made a full court press for an early decision. They had a professional recruiter named Hal Reister (a non-accountant based in Dallas) who had interviewed me on the campus at USM and who stayed in constant contact. He was a personable, persuasive guy with piercing blue eyes and a beguiling smile. I thought his bright white teeth resembled Chiclets. I bonded with him quickly. My primary hesitancy, however, was that Houston seemed a million miles away from my roots. Despite my wanderlust and attraction to the corporate world, I felt a subtle desire to take a few baby steps as I embarked on my career. I also knew that Dot would have a natural reluctance to start our lives together so far away from home. I gradually began to narrow my focus to New Orleans.
During the Peat Marwick campus interview, I had met Harold
Dey and Bill Norton, managing partners of the Peat Marwick offices in New Orleans and Jackson. Harold Dey took the lead and followed up our interview with an o ffer for a position in New
Orleans. However, I sensed a subtle connection with Bill Norton. It was a sixth sense that seemed to be reciprocated. A fter the campus interview, he stayed in touch with me and sometime over the next month extended an invitation for me to “stop by” and visit his
Jackson office in mid-December, when I visited Dot at Mississippi
College. So I did, along with my friend Bob Hishon. Though the offices and the people in Jackson were not quite as impressive as the ones in New Orleans, I felt comfortable there. I wouldn’t say that I
was intimidated in the other office environments, but certainly the
Jackson office gave me a feeling of self-con fidence and that I would do well there. I can remember making a casual observation to myself about the people there and could see myself competing successfully with them for career advancement. Even though I mentioned that to no one, I felt embarrassed at the thought, frankly. But it was there nevertheless. In fact, I was bullish about my success probabilities in that particular environment. Also, by that time, I had read all the materials from all the firms and studied their business models and strategies. My gut instincts kept leading me back to Peat Marwick.
The culture seemed to match up with my fundamental values and work ethic. Many years later during a program at Harvard, I heard
Peat Marwick described as the classic “hunter” in a hunter/gatherer survival analogy. Although I didn’t know anything about such an analogy or organizational strategies at the time, I think I sensed the essence of that description and how it fit my basic persona and skill set. I saw Peat Marwick as aggressive, progressive and moving faster than the competition. It seemed less systematic and stilted than the other firms. More flexible.
Dot and I discussed all the opportunities extensively over the
Christmas holidays, and we decided to accept the Peat Marwick offer in Jackson, notwithstanding the fact that the salary was the lowest of the competing o ffers. I contacted all the firms upon returning to
USM in January and notified them of my decision. That put my two-month career search to bed, and I started making arrangements to transition from college to career in July.
The entire interview process was both educational and enjoyable.
I learned so much about the business world as I visited with each of the firms, and I met some terri fic people. Several of the partners in the other firms stayed in casual touch with me, in case I might have a change in heart. I also bumped into a few of them over the years.
Hal Reister from Arthur Y oung frequently touched base with me and wrote me a nice le tter when I made partner at Peat Marwick seven years later. The letter started out with, “I knew this was going to happen!” It was a warm feeling to know that I had credentials that were highly regarded and sought after.
I should mention more about the good advice and counsel I had along the way as I went through this process. Dorothy Lenoir, the placement director, was a crusty old lady with a brusque personality;
I think she might have had a military background. She could be condescending to students and actually vulgar at times. However,
she turned a warmer side to me, for some strange reason, and insisted on a report from me every step along the way. She advised me how to handle the a ttention I was ge tting, and without a doubt was extraordinarily helpful. Dr. Joe Green, Dean of the Business
School, sat with me on a few occasions and talked to me about my future. I can remember my rapt a ttention as he sat in his rocking swivel chair, smoking his pipe and prophesying about my future,
much as I imagined a grandfather might have done. I felt as if I was in the presence of Mark Twain or some other luminary. All of the accounting professors were keenly interested and helpful, as they tried to steer me to the public accounting firms—perhaps they saw me as an extension of their work and a symbol of their well-deserved reputations as excellent professors. And then there was Dr. McCain,
the university president, who beseeched me to stay in Mississippi,
whatever I chose to do!
I never once looked back with any regret over my decision. Peat
Marwick turned out to be the absolute perfect match for me. The decision was a complex product of diligent research, focused effort,
hands-on inspection, listening to good advice from others and my personal career and lifestyle ambitions. It was a carefully weighed decision. I ultimately spent thirty-two years with Peat Marwick.
Within seven years, I became the youngest partner in the history of the largest accounting firm in the world and then advanced over the years to become the third-ranking executive in the entire firm before retirement. So, clearly no regrets!

## Principles

- Make a deliberate list of evaluation criteria before choosing.
- Consider both objective measures (reputation, size, advancement) and subjective fit (people, clients, culture).
- Prioritize long-term fit and advancement over short-term salary.
- Use in-person visits to assess organizations and people.
- Seek and weigh counsel from trusted advisors and family when making major career decisions.

## Decision heuristics

- List and weigh specific objective and subjective criteria (reputation, size, advancement, people, clients) before deciding.
- Visit employers' offices and meet partners to judge cultural fit and working environment.
- Discuss major career choices with your partner and consider lifestyle and location impacts.
- Accept lower short-term compensation when long-term advancement and cultural fit indicate better career outcomes.
- Maintain relationships with recruiters and keep communication open to preserve options.
