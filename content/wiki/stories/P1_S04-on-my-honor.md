On My Honor
There is no finer organization for young people in this country than the Boy Scouts of America. I’m certain of that. My scouting journey began as a Cub Scout at about age ten. I worked my way up to become an Eagle Scout several years later. Scouting represented years of adventure, fun, learning and personal character development. The fundamental fiber of the Boy Scout organization and the quality of the leaders who shepherded me through these years would be di fficult to exaggerate.
My earliest scouting memories begin in the living room of my home, where a small den of Cub Scouts gathered periodically.
Mother was the den mother and guided a handful of guys through various cra fts and games. The young Cub Scouts all had small scouting books with pictures of various animals on the cover. We were issued individual score sheets that kept track of exercises and projects. An added plus was the spiffy blue uniforms we wore to the meetings. The activities taught us teamwork, sportsmanship and a variety of other physical and character traits. We were challenged to advance through the various levels —Bobcat, Wolf, Bear, etc., with an ultimate goal of reaching something called Webelos. At each level, achievement badges were awarded, and I proudly sewed them on my blue uniform as they were awarded. I instinctively liked this scene. The meetings were always fun. The activities were challenging and invariably ended with a sense of achievement. The badges and awards fed my personal desire for recognition. There was a sense of order and structure that appealed to me, not the least of which was that formal uniform that I wore so proudly.
When the time came, at about age twelve, I moved up to the official Boy Scout organization and became a member of T roop
40 as a T enderfoot, the base level of ranking. Mother took some of us on a trip to Reed’s Department Store in Tupelo, where we each acquired a complete uniform with the entire trappings —khaki outfit with cap, scarf, belt, etc. As the years went by, I outgrew the uniforms, and we made other trips for new pants and shirts. As we became designated Explorer Scouts, somewhere along the line, the uniforms morphed into dark green outfits. I have a sharp memory of one fundamental character lesson learned in this uniform migration.
With Mother’s guidance, we always tried to find a less fortunate kid who could not afford a uniform and we would donate my outgrown outfit. I always had a warm feeling about this. This act of kindness and charity seemed to resemble some of the citizenship lessons embedded in the Boy Scout program.
T roop 40 met regularly in the Scout Hut, an old one-room building on a hill, near the football field where community tennis courts stand today. The building was a dark brick-red color, like an old country barn. It was a one-room structure that was empty inside except for an old wood-burning stove that served as a heater. More than likely, it was built for some other purpose and perhaps used for something else at the time, but for us the Scout Hut was our place.
If I were led blindfolded back to this building, the nostalgic smell of the place would be immediately recognizable.
Some extraordinary volunteers led our troop from time to time.
George Swindle comes to mind first. George was a young adult who devoted hundreds of hours to the local scouting program. He was a former scout himself and seemingly knew everything there was to know about the subject. He was a skinny, gangly fellow, a relatively weak guy physically, with several physical limitations, and he had been in ill health much of his life. Nonetheless, his mind was razor sharp and he was undaunted in his devotion to the troop. George later became a lawyer, married and then unfortunately died at a young age. We all owe him a debt of gratitude for his tutelage. There were several others who provided quality leadership and guidance along the way. And then there was Dr. Carter Dobbs, a terrific role model for character, citizenship, patriotism and personal development —
the fundamental things for which the Boy Scouts of America stands.
More about him later.
My Boy Scout Handbook became my virtual Bible at this juncture in my life. It was a three-hundred-plus page book, packed with fascinating information about all phases of personal development—
camping, hiking, first aid, life saving, personal health, citizenship and many other subjects. Mine was ragged around the edges from persistent use. The Handbook contained requirements and guidelines for progressing through the achievement ranks —Second Class,
First Class, Star, Life and Eagle. It also included several symbols of the scout creed that the program required us to memorize, such as the Boy Scout oath, law and mo tto. But it was more than rote memorization; we were expertly guided to learn what it all meant,
and we could explain it in terms we all understood. Sixty years later,
I can still recite the law without faltering: “ A Scout is trustworthy,
loyal, helpful, friendly, courteous, kind, obedient, cheerful, thri fty,
brave, clean and reverent. ” I still know what all that means! T oday,
it means the same thing it meant sixty years ago. The Boy Scout
Handbook was designed to accomplish the avowed purpose of the organization: to produce a “well-developed, well-informed boy” . As far as I am concerned, the members of T roop 40 achieved that goal,
thanks to the Handbook and extraordinary, dedicated leaders.
Over time, I accumulated a lot of Boy Scout paraphernalia,
including all sorts of camping gear. The inventory included knives,
hatchets, cooking gear, first-aid kits and water purification pills—all kinds of stu ff. My personal sleeping bag was a makeshift thing that
I fashioned out of an old quilt by sewing two sides together with fishing line. The crude, homemade appearance slightly embarrassed me, but the sleeping bag served its purpose. I even had a beat-up antique tent that we used a few times. The tent dated back to World
War I and had been handed down in the family from my dad’s Uncle
Vernon, who had been in the Army.
Our camping excursions were terri fic experiences. They were lots of fun and had the added benefit of learning all sorts of new and useful outdoor skills.
I took the ranking system seriously and plodded through the various levels steadily. Steadily, that is, until I arrived at the Life level.
The steps required significant learning and skill demonstration, and much testing along the way. I found the hurdle from Life to Eagle to be difficult. I squa tted at that Life level for a couple of years. Hugh
Steve Swink and I both seemed to be stuck there. By that time, we had other things on our minds. I found myself procrastinating on accomplishing the rigorous achievements in a twenty-one merit badge program. Hugh Steve and I were not alone hanging in this suspension. This is the level where many well-intentioned scouts falter and never make it over the hump. As a ma tter of fact, I have read that less than 5 percent of scouts become Eagle Scouts. The desire for recognition as an Eagle Scout was there for me, but I found my discipline and tenacity to be weak. I felt badly about that and had a sense of shame about my procrastination. Eventually, I buckled down though and got it all done. It was indeed a proud moment for me to stand in the First Baptist Church and receive that sterling silver eagle with the red, white and blue ribbon. It hangs today in a beautiful frame in a prominent spot in my study. The inscription says, “Be Prepared” , an apt message.
I attended Boy Scout camps at Camp Palila in Louisville on two or three occasions and eventually served as a counselor there one year. I supervised a cabin of about a dozen kids and taught lifesaving at the lake. One of my perks was my own private canoe that was used to tool around the large lake. Also, my crowning personal achievement that summer was swimming a mile nonstop —four lengths of the lake. My last signi ficant camp excursion was a trip to the National Boy Scout Jamboree at Valley Forge, Pennsylvania in
1957. It was an unbelievable experience, sprinkled with dozens of larger-than-life memories.
The mentor and motivator-in-chief behind my Boy Scout days was Dr. Carter Dobbs. Dr. Dobbs was a local dentist with an excellent reputation as a superb professional and a respected health care provider in our community. But he also had a second job. He was the local scoutmaster. He took on this responsibility with the same degree of vigor and serious expertise that he applied to his dental practice. He wore the uniform, talked the talk and walked the walk of the Boy Scouts of America. He spent untold hours with T roop 40. He instructed, guided, supervised, disciplined and prodded guys of all ages and all backgrounds as though they all were his sons. He made all the meetings, participated in the hikes and slept on the ground with the troop on the campouts. And he monitored the development and progress of each scout individually.
In his book, one had to do the work—all of it—to achieve the goals before basking in any rewards. Dr. Dobbs didn’t believe in breaks,
gimmies or mulligans. Without a doubt, he was a tough guy and a stern taskmaster. But his rigor never got in the way of us having fun along the way.
There were eleven young boys in T roop 40 who a ttained the
Eagle Scout award under Dr. Dobbs’ direction in those years. That’s an unusually large number for such a small community! Thanks to his son Jimmy Gage, I now have in my memorabilia a treasured photograph of all eleven guys posed together in uniform with their
Eagle Scout medals.
Dr. Dobbs was a serious man who hardly ever laughed—at least as I recall—but a shadow of a smile sneaks out in this photograph. It is the smile of an intensely proud man. It is a smile of accomplishment.
It is a smile that says, “Don’t look at me; look at these guys. ” His pride was certainly well deserved. And I doubt that he ever looked at anyone’s teeth and smiled like that!
Eagle Scouts mentored by Dr. Carter Dobbs
As I look back over my life, I can’t think of a more signi ficant teacher and role model than Dr. Dobbs. His positive leadership helped keep me grounded during these formative stages of my life.
He kept my feet on a positive pathway. I owe him an extreme debt of gratitude for this personal leadership. I stayed in touch with him over the years. I attended his funeral back in Calhoun City a few years ago and stood sadly beside his casket with tears welling up in my eyes.
The lessons of my Boy Scout experiences have followed me throughout my life. As a matter of appreciation and duty, I have tried to keep alive some level of fidelity to the organization over the years.
That allegiance has waxed and waned from time to time, but my heart has always been there with the program. In college, I volunteered to be an assistant scoutmaster with a troop in Ha ttiesburg. Y ears later,
when I was living in Philadelphia, I served as an executive member of the regional Boy Scout Council. I have contributed money to various Boy Scout programs over the years and I have belonged to the National Eagle Scouts Association for many years. In the year
2000, I was honored with a presentation of the prestigious national
Distinguished Eagle Scout Award at a dinner in Miami. In my acceptance remarks, I made sure to mention Dr. Dobbs and pay tribute to the significant role he played in my life.
I am a be tter person today because of the Boy Scouts of
America. I invested heavily in the program as a teenager, but others invested more heavily in me during those years. The returns on these investments cannot be measured in numbers, badges, medals or awards —or even good memories. The returns are represented by the ethical and moral choices that one makes throughout life. In my case, these choices have been heavily in fluenced by the values instilled in me during these impressionable years. The Boy Scout
Oath still lives with me and, I hope, has been a catalyst in keeping me “ …physically strong, mentally awake and morally straight” .

## Principles

- Leaders should model the behaviors they expect from others.
- Require genuine effort before granting rewards to instill discipline and competence.
- Consistent mentorship during formative years shapes long-term character and choices.
- Use structured milestones and visible recognition to motivate progress and learning.
- Embed charity and service as routine practices to reinforce citizenship.

## Decision heuristics

- Require completion of substantive work before awarding recognition to build discipline.
- Create clear ranks, badges, and score-keeping to turn progress into tangible, motivating milestones.
- Monitor each person's development individually to tailor guidance and accountability.
