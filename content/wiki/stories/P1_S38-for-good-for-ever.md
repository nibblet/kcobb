## Full Text

For Good. For Ever.
“We make a living by what we do, but we make a life by what we give. ” That sage quotation by Winston Churchill fairly well sums up my fundamental philosophy about community service. From our earliest days, Dot and I have made community service a cornerstone of our lives. Wherever we have lived, we have tried to be generous in the giving of both our money and our time for the bene fit of the better good.
I suppose the concept of community service goes all the way back to my Boy Scout days, when service to others was a basic part of the learning. As one early example of this, I remember dressing up in my brown long-sleeved uniform and accompanying the local troop on a rickety bus to Mississippi State University football games,
where we walked up and down the stadium steps in the sweltering heat, serving as ushers for the fans a ttending the game. Granted,
there was a quid pro quo in this service; we got to watch the game.
Nevertheless, it was voluntary work and it was a service —helping people find their seats. And I saw it as just that.
All through college, I was deeply involved in all sorts of public service organizations, many of them centered on the Student
Government Association and student a ffairs. Upon joining Peat
Marwick, it became clear that management of the firm expected everyone to be involved in the community in some way. Oddly,
despite the clarity of this message, most just ignored it and went on with their personal lives. Having been preconditioned, I saddled up and finagled my way into a few things. I knew that such things could be interesting and quickly realized that they were an excellent way to meet people and make friends. They also generated a feeling,
important to me, of having done something worthwhile.
The most continuous charitable endeavor for us over the years has been the United Way. Our financial contributions to this organization started with a modest payroll deduction from my first paycheck at Peat Marwick in 1963 and have continued uninterrupted ever since. The United Way of Broward County recently recognized us, with a degree of local hoopla, for over fifty consecutive years of support to various United Ways where we have lived.
We gradually escalated our United Way contributions over time, trying to do a little be tter every year. And we’ve had a few nudges along the way. Once, back in the 1980s, Bob Elmore, a local businessman, called on me at my office and suggested an increase in our annual contribution. I responded, “Why Bob, we’ve been giving
$1,000 a year!” He said, “Keith, I already know that, but it’s just not enough. We both know you can do more, and I’m asking you to consider doing that. ” So, I doubled our pledge. A few years later, in
Philadelphia, with a similar nudge by Pete Musser, a client, we again bumped up our pledge, this time to $10,000. We have contributed at least $10,000 every year since. We have belonged to the United
Way’s T ocqueville Society for more than twenty years now.
Our involvement, however, has been more than just monetary contributions. We have contributed our time and energy as well. I
served on the board in Broward County for several years and was chairman for two years. Dot served on the executive commi ttee of the T ocqueville Society and founded a United Way agency in
Baltimore. We have hosted a number of United Way events in our home and attended countless events to support the organization.
We like to believe our personal support of United Way has made a difference in our community.
We have also supported our local church during that same period of time. We gradually increased our contributions over the years as our financial position improved. At one time or another,
we have been involved in all aspects of the various churches that we have attended. Most of my involvement has been focused on financial and administrative ma tters, while Dot has concentrated on music programs and women’s activities. We’ve spent many, many hours behind those church doors and, again, we like to believe we have made solid contributions to the various congregations.
Along the way, I was also involved in other community organizations, usually at the behest of friends. I served as chairman of the Museum of Science and Discovery board when the beautiful downtown museum in Fort Lauderdale was conceived and constructed. I also served as chairman of the Broward Library
Foundation during its formative stages. Following that gig, I
was asked to join the board of the Community Foundation of
Broward and ended up as chairman for a couple of years. I was a charter member of the Broward Workshop, a business coalition of corporate CEOs, and remain a member today, thirty years later. And so the story has evolved over the years, serving as a board member or volunteer in dozens of organizations, which have ranged from the local Kidney Foundation to the Federal Reserve Board. More recently, my leadership roles have subsided a bit, but commi ttee work continues with the United Way, the Community Foundation,
The Performing Arts Center and First Presbyterian Church. I am also called on from time to time to head up citizen commi ttees for the City of Fort Lauderdale. Community service has actually become a way of life for me.
And it has not all been organizational work. In 2013, for example,
I travelled to Oklahoma City following the horrible tornado there and spent a week in the blazing sun helping with the cleanup. I came home physically weary but with a wonderful inner feeling, a sense that I had done some good work for some good people in need.
Along the way, community service has had the e ffect of enhancing my leadership skills. As I slogged my way through all of these organizations and activities, I was able to bring some of my business training and experience to bear on the organizations—but
I learned a lot from them in return. Community leadership roles have taught me a lot about motivating the behavior of people, setting definitive goals and ge tting things done. Many well-intentioned volunteers are essentially inept at organizational work. Accordingly,
working with them is a virtual laboratory for leadership. I learned a great deal about running an e fficient meeting, about compromise and about so many other things that I might have missed otherwise.
In other words, I gained some extremely valuable skills from these associations.
I also gained some wonderful friends and relationships. Many of my closest friends today can be traced back to mutual community service activity. The same is true for many of my business relationships. That has never been a raison d’être for my involvement,
but it has certainly proved to be a fringe benefit.
The term “pay it forward” has been popularized in recent years to characterize the receipt of a good deed and then repaying the gesture to someone other than the original benefactor. As Ralph Waldo
Emerson wrote in 1841, “In the order of nature we cannot render benefits to those from whom we receive them, or only seldom. But the benefit we receive must be rendered again, line for line, deed for deed, cent for cent, to somebody. ” Although it might sound cheesy,
Dot and I have adopted that as our essential creed throughout the years. We completely recognize the good fortune that has rained upon us and see ourselves as privileged indeed. Most of the world is not nearly as fortunate. We have sensed a responsibility to “pay it forward” and, hence, our immersion in so much community service over the years.
My study and my closet are filled with photos, plaques and mementos presented in recognition of our community service. But those are not nearly as valuable as the memories packed inside our hearts and the notion that we have played a role in making the little world around us a better place for others.
The Community Foundation of Broward has a catchy slogan that they use in all their literature: “For Good. For Ever. ” I like that a lot.

## Principles

- Sustained, consistent giving builds long-term community impact.
- Volunteer leadership roles are opportunities to apply professional skills and to learn leadership.
- Combine monetary support with time and leadership for greater impact.
- Adopt an ethic of reciprocity—'pay it forward'—as a guiding motive for service.

## Decision heuristics

- When company culture expects community involvement, participate to build networks and experience.
- When a trusted peer asks you to increase your support, consider promptly raising your pledge.
- Use board service to transfer business expertise to nonprofits and treat it as a learning laboratory.
- When disaster relief needs hands, volunteer on-site even if physically demanding.
- Gradually escalate financial support as personal financial position improves.
