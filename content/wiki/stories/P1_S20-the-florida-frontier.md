## Full Text

The Florida Frontier
Walt Disney World opened its massive theme park In Orlando in August 1971, and the powers that be at Peat Marwick reasoned that there would be an in flux of business, which would be good for the firm. As sophisticated as Peat Marwick was, the rationale for this conclusion was nothing more complicated than that. When I
learned that the firm had done practically no market research on
Orlando, my mind wandered o ff to the California gold rush in the
1800s and how people trudged o ff to a new life with nothing more than a rumor or perhaps a scrap of paper saying things would be better there. I went back to Atlanta to meet again with Norm White to see if he had any advice for me. He was friendly and cordial, as much as a crusty old curmudgeon or salty dog can be. But at the end of our bland meeting, he summed up his advice with, “Hell,
what do I know? I never opened a damn office! Just go down there and beat the bushes. Y ou’ll do fine!” So, we loaded up our virtual covered wagon and o ff we went to Orlando in August 1971. I was the “managing partner” of an office that didn’t even exist. It was not exactly an excursion for the faint of heart!
Dot and I reconfigured our lives as we adjusted to our new home in Altamonte Springs, an Orlando suburb. I was thirty years old and
Dot was twenty-nine. Our first son, Paul, was three years old, and Dot was six months pregnant with John. We felt like true pioneers, setting out to explore a new land. Our family life was a positive experience in multiple respects, right from the get-go. We had the good fortune of settling in next door to two of the most wonderful families —the
Letourneaus and the Morans. Both became fast friends from the start and both were extremely helpful in getting us acclimated to our new surroundings. They have remained valued friends to this day. In late 2014, I had the opportunity to spend a wonderful day with an ailing Roy Letourneau in Dallas, just a month before he died.
As it turned out, I was the first Peat Marwick partner to work officially in the state of Florida. Florida had what were locally called
“snow bird laws” that prohibited national CPA or non-Florida law firms from practicing in the state, as well as some types of medical practices. So, the major firms had a ffiliates to handle their Florida work. These affiliate firms operated under profit-sharing agreements and were essentially shadows of the national firms. The laws were in the process of being changed in 1971, with a ra ft of restrictive clauses being implemented. Compliance was tricky and penalties for non-compliance were stiff. T echnically I was supposed to resign from the firm and join an a ffiliated firm in Jacksonville for about a month until my reciprocal CPA certificate was issued by the state.
Peat Marwick had no clients in the Orlando area and I had no staff to work with at the beginning. It was just me—a lonely feeling.
But I plunged in head first, trying to find my way around and get connected in the business community. I remember si tting at my kitchen table working on plans, while my thoughts dri fted off into the clouds wondering what in the world I had gotten myself into.
After a few weeks, I came across a secretary who had worked for Peat Marwick in Boston. I hired her immediately and rented a temporary one-room office downtown. It was just this secretary and me sitting face-to-face in a small, lonely office. As it turned out, the office was adjacent to a real estate promoter named George Barley,
who I met in the hallway. George operated on a shoestring and always had projects in the works. He was invariably short on cash—
and financial documentation—for his ideas. He asked me to help him with some financial projections, and in lieu of fees, he agreed to introduce me around town and recommend me to others. George was a rather gay blade, a bit of a bon vivant and a bit of a flaneur. He seemed to be friendly with virtually everyone in town.
George Barley was a lucky find. Through him, I began to pick up bits and pieces of work, clients that actually paid fees for the services. George introduced me to his brother Kim, who headed the Dean Wi tter Reynolds stock brokerage firm in town. Like
George, Kim seemed to take pleasure tucking me under his wings.
He sponsored me for membership in the exclusive University Club downtown. He introduced me to Don Senterfitt, the senior partner in the prestigious law firm of Akerman Senterfitt; Don later became the chairman of SunT rust. Don insisted that I move to the brand new
CNA building, in the heart of the business district, where his firm was located. This made a lot of sense to me. Don was also generous in his client recommendations. And so the wheels began to turn.
All of these growing recommendations were then supplemented by serendipity. Our Minneapolis client General Mills acquired Red
Lobster restaurants, a still-embryonic restaurant chain that had been started by William Darden in Orlando. Our office promptly took over all of the audit and tax work from a small firm, and Red
Lobster thus became our office’s first sizable, vibrant audit client.
A Rochester, New Y ork office client, Ganne tt Newspapers, quickly followed that good fortune. Ganne tt was secretly developing an experimental prototype for what eventually became USA T oday.
It was called Cocoa T oday and was located in Cocoa Beach, only fifty miles away. In a chance meeting, the Rochester partner on the Gannett account con fided in me about this highly con fidential
R&D project. I told him I had some signi ficant experience with the
Hederman newspapers in Mississippi, and volunteered to help his client. Bingo! I was suddenly on the ground floor of a burgeoning national newspaper success story, analyzing the financial dynamics and preparing confidential pro forma financial information. T oday,
every time I pick up a copy of USA T oday, my mind races back to that grubby, windowless room in a nondescript warehouse a stone’s throw from the Kennedy Space Center, where I pored over yellow working papers as the Ganne tt executives struggled with the new concept. In an interesting twist ten years later, Ganne tt purchased the Clarion-Ledger, the Hederman family flagship newspaper in
Jackson, Mississippi.
About six months after we arrived in Orlando and in the midst of scrambling around trying to get something going, I received an interesting phone call from Bob Boutillier. Bob was the deputy senior partner of the firm and effectively the chief operating officer.
He opened the conversation by saying, “Keith, do you realize you’re losing money down there? We don’t have any offices that actually lose money!” My initial reaction was, “What the hell do you expect?” but
I restrained my outrage and explained the situation as best I could.
Bob knew absolutely nothing about the situation. Once I made my case and he learned about the firm’s strategy of investing in a new marketplace with a young partner, he was very supportive.
We gradually began to construct a skeleton sta ff for the office.
With some heavy persuasion, I was able to arrange a few transfers of experienced personnel from other offices—Bill McKeever from
Philadelphia, Jim Felcyn from Chicago, Paul Breazeale from Jackson and David Graham from Jacksonville. Lower-level sta ff members were recruited from Florida colleges. Over a four-year period we assembled a staff of fourteen professionals and three administrative people. Although small, it was a tight knit and e ffective team. I
remember them all with fondness. Paul Breazeale was especially helpful in those days. He took a huge leap of faith to follow me to
Orlando and worked his tail off to help me make it work. After a few years he moved back to Jackson and established his own accounting firm. His firm is now a flagship business in Jackson, and Paul is a distinguished professional in the community. Paul and his family have remained dear and valuable friends to this day and I stay in touch with him regularly.
I worked hard during those four years and was proud of our accomplishments. I had started from absolute scratch and now had built a legitimate Peat Marwick office. Also, my family was comfortable and happy in Orlando.
But despite our apparent success and personal happiness, I was plagued with a nagging notion that the stars were not lining up for the firm long term. Orlando was a virtual boomtown, reminiscent of scenes from Old West movies, recast in modern glass, concrete and multi-lane highways. Construction and new businesses were springing up in all directions, most of them tethered in some way to Walt Disney World and the tourism boom it promised. I began to realize that all of this hustle and bustle did not necessarily translate to opportunity for Peat Marwick. The overwhelming majority of commercial development there was owned, operated and accounted for elsewhere, in some other headquarters. There were actually very few sizable, locally headquartered businesses —
businesses that could potentially be profitable clients for the firm—
in the Orlando area. And none of this was helped by the 1973–1975
recession throughout the United States, which, coupled with a major international oil crisis, put a temporary damper on travel and tourism. Accordingly, as I reviewed our revenue base I came to the stark realization that further development of a mature Peat Marwick office was likely to string out over several years down the road.
I brooded privately about this prognosis for several months. I
wanted to keep a positive front for my staff to keep them motivated.
And I didn’t want to upset the apple cart with my family as they basked in their happiness. Y et, I felt an overwhelming sense of responsibility to the firm to do the right thing. I had to provide the firm’s management with my on-the-ground best judgment as to the future of our business. Finally, I bit the bullet and wrote a serious letter to Walter Hanson. I slaved over the words. I outlined my assessment of the marketplace and feasibility of signi ficant growth and development in the short run. I concluded with a recommendation that we close the office and lay plans to come back in five or ten years, after Orlando had matured to a stage beyond a branch office town centered on the hospitality industry. I was thirty-four years old when I wrote that le tter and didn’t have a clue about what this might mean for my career.
A few days after dropping my situation assessment in the mailbox, Walter Hanson called me to say he agreed with my report.
He complimented me on the courage (as he characterized it) to voluntarily submit this recommendation and assured me he would personally see that a meaningful career opportunity was placed in my pathway. He instructed me to lay plans to close the office, transfer client responsibilities to the T ampa office and await further word as to my future. And so I did just that.
About a month later, I received a call from Norm White. He advised me that I had been selected as the managing partner of the
Fort Lauderdale office, replacing Howard Miller, who was moving to
Switzerland. So, off we were to Fort Lauderdale and a new adventure in our odyssey.

## Principles

- Assess local market fundamentals before committing long-term resources.
- Be candid with leadership and present a documented, on-the-ground assessment even when recommendations are difficult.
- Build local networks and leverage introductions to generate early clients when starting from scratch.
- Start small and iterate: use temporary arrangements to establish presence before scaling.
- Accept responsibility for firm resources and act to prevent prolonged unprofitability.

## Decision heuristics

- When entering a new geography, verify market demand with research instead of relying on general optimism.
- Use local connectors and barter (e.g., services for introductions) to build initial paid engagements quickly.
- If a candid assessment shows limited near-term prospects, recommend an orderly exit rather than prolonging losses.
- Document and communicate a clear, evidence-based market assessment to firm leadership to enable constructive decisions.
