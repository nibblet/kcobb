Moments to Remember
When one begins to look back and try to document a lifetime of experiences, the methodology of the exercise sometimes becomes confusing. Does a year-by-year chronology make sense? Should the memories be grouped topically? Should they be associated with particular people or places? Should things be ranked in some sort of priority of importance? What is the best way to organize things? Thinking about this, I have decided that random is as good a methodology as any.
On a long flight one day, I decided to use the idle time to reflect on my life and on this memoir, which was a work-in-progress and to make a list of some of the most memorable moments in my life. The list is still incomplete, but consists of an eclectic mix of experiences that have ground their way into my memory bank and serve as touch points for my emotional nerve center. Many of those moments are chronicled in this memoir. I will attempt to summarize a few examples of the highlights in the following paragraphs. There is no particular order to the moments, just a random recollection of nostalgic pieces stuffed into the corners of my mind.
There is only place to begin the list. It is with my marriage to
Dot. By a huge margin this was the most pivotal point in my life. I
stepped out of a world of independence into a lifetime partnership. I
was indeed so fortunate to have found a soul mate and, ironically, just down the street from my previous existence. As I stood at the altar in the First Baptist Church in 1963, my mind raced a million miles an hour thinking of the transformation that was taking place. Though a bit nervous, I listened to the vows carefully and took them seriously.
I loved this woman with all my heart and realized I was making an irrevocable commitment—possibly the only one I had ever made up until that point. If I should ever contract Alzheimer’s disease and lose my memory, I suspect this will be the last experience of my life that I would forget. It was the most special day of my life.
College life was a special time in my life —so many experiences to recall. Probably the most significant was graduation day. This was a distinctively emotional time for both my family and me. I was the first person on either side of the family to graduate from college.
That observation included parents, grandparents, aunts, uncles and cousins. That uniqueness was not lost on me. I felt both fortunate and proud. I felt fortunate because I was undoubtedly the first one who ever had the opportunity to attend college. I was proud because
I had worked hard and not only graduated, but also graduated with honors.
Aside from graduation day, the other most memorable occasion in college was the day I was elected president of the Student
Government Association. My emotions were all over the place. I felt a sense of pride that five thousand people had elected me as their leader. A duality flooded over me, as I realized that this euphoria came along with a responsibility to live up to their expectations.
There was a strong sense of gratitude for all the support I had in the process. Optimism overwhelmed me as I thought of all the good things I should be able to accomplish with the newfound power and authority. I can still think about that day and feel all those disparate emotions converge inside me.
The day we learned of Dot’s breast cancer was a traumatic one.
If one had asked me, I would have said that I understood illness and mortality; however, the honest fact was that I had never spent any time reflecting on the tragedies that so many people deal with. All of a sudden, we were faced with this looming reality. What were we to do? It was simultaneously confusing and scary. I will never forget that day.
Family vacations are special times in family memory bank.
There were lots of them over the years. One of the most memorable was a motor home trip we took out West in about 1982. We rented a twenty-six-foot motor home from our friend Roy Letourneau in
T exas and spent three weeks touring Colorado, Arizona and New
Mexico. We visited the Grand Canyon, Carlsbad Caverns, Mesa
Verde and all the other famous sites and a ttractions along the way.
Our children have wonderful memories of this trip —the sites, the campgrounds, the campfires, and family songs as we moved over the highways. Such wonderful memories!
There were also several special holidays along the way that trigger emotional tugs. One that rises to the top of the list was a Christmas in Williamsburg, Virginia. There is really nothing else like it in the
United States. T ruly spectacular! We stayed at the magni ficent
Williamsburg Inn, which we had to book a year in advance because of its popularity. We felt like we had actually rolled the clock back two hundred years and were living in colonial times for a few days.
Close to this experience was another Christmas when Dot and
I travelled to Banff, Canada, and spent the holidays at the fabulous
Banff Fairmont Springs Hotel. This was a fairy tale Christmas. The hotel went all out in decorations and activities, and the weather was holiday-perfect. Everything was covered in white snow. We both
Idyllic Christmas in Ban ff, Canada remember a special open-sleigh ride through snow-lined pathways in the countryside. It was Currier and Ives personified!
Our first international vacation was also a memorable time for
Dot and me. We were young and adventurous. Switzerland and
Austria were both beautiful and fascinating. We won’t forget the emotions as we rumbled along the railways and walked the streets of
European cities for the first time.
There have been lots of firsts that stir up emotional memories for me. I can still almost relive every moment of the first major league baseball game I saw in 1957. Likewise, I get goosebumps whenever
I recall my first Broadway musical in 1959 and the stirring overture to My Fair Lady. I remember the first time I voted in an election, my first car, my first home, my first KPMG partners’ meeting, and on and on. Unforgettable experiences.
For some rather odd and unexplainable reason, another unforgettable experience sticks in my mind and creeps out occasionally. In 1990 I was invited to a dinner at the Governor’s mansion in Maryland. It was a cold and rainy evening, and I had to park several blocks away from the mansion. As I walked in the stinging rain along the cobbled streets of Annapolis with my overcoat and umbrella, I was struck by the colonial architecture and gas streetlights of this historic city. Time didn’t stand still; it reversed. I imagined myself a ttending a government function as a founding father two hundred years ago and thinking about the drama of such an event. It was American history brought alive in my consciousness. This was a most unusual and highly emotional fifteen or twenty minutes for me. I’m not sure exactly why. But I have had similar moments at other places, such as following the Lewis &
Clark trail out west. These moments qualify for inclusion in my list of special remembrances.
Births and deaths also qualify. No time travel is complete for me without a mental visit to the births of my three sons. These miraculous events are eternal bright spots in my mind, stirring up emotions about new life and continuation of our own existence.
And the death of my father was a transformative time for me. I saw a good man disappear from view, but at the same time saw his legacy continue in the form of wonderful memories of his character and demeanor.
I’ll round out this rambling list with the inclusion of our fiftieth wedding anniversary celebration, when we gathered the entire family of thirteen for a Caribbean cruise aboard the Oasis of the Seas.
With our family so sca ttered geographically and each having their own employment and other commitments, it is difficult to get them all together at once, but for this occasion we managed it. It was a great time of family sharing and celebration. It ranks near the top of my Hall of Fame of family events over the many years, and I will carry those memories with me to the grave.
There is a seemingly endless list of these special memories. The foregoing random collection is intended to illustrate the wide range of my recollections as I reflect on my life.
Conclusion
As I have pecked away at the stories in this memoir, my consciousness has been piqued by many revelations about myself.
Before this project I had never given too much thought about my historical journey’s impact on my life. I seem to have been consistently focused on the future—or the moment—without a lot of self-reflection or retrospection along the way.
Thumbing through some albums and mementos I have stu ffed away in my relics, I came across a stack of annual goals and priorities that I routinely prepared for many years. The first of these from the early years were crude notes scratched out on yellow lined paper.
Over time, they then progressed to a more comprehensive approach to strategizing all aspects of my personal life. I went through this annual exercise consistently for twenty-five or thirty years. For some reason, I have kept all these private notes. It is interesting now to read through them and reflect on my personal struggle to structure and improve my life. Interestingly, there is not a word in all these documents about past accomplishments. If there is a central theme to all these goals and objectives, it was a forward-thinking focus on improving myself in a wide range of areas and providing a balance in my life. That’s what I was about. I wasn’t looking back.
But now, in my twilight years, it has been cathartic to take a peek into the rear view mirror. I have thoroughly enjoyed the re flection.
I have also been able to re flect on the impact of the world around me. I realize that living in different locales and travelling widely have added spice and substance to my life. Moreover, the variety of these experiences has taught me much about the world I inhabit and the people I have encountered.
I have learned a lot about myself in the process. Before embarking on this memoir, I never had a comprehensive thought about all the help I have had along the way. But as I began to weave these stories, I gradually began to notice a pa ttern of personalities that profoundly shaped my life. T eachers, bosses, mentors, partners,
friends and family began to be seen through a di fferent lens. I don’t believe I ever thought I had been alone in my life’s journey; however,
the level of support received along the way had simply not sunk in.
The new focus on these influences has washed me in a deep emotion of gratitude. I am truly grateful for the enormous leverage all these wonderful people have provided.
I am also immensely grateful for my good fortune. Serendipity has been my strongest ally. Despite all the goal se tting, so much of my good fortune has not been sought; so o ften I merely stumbled upon it. It seems as though I was wearing some sort of magic good luck charm that has inoculated me from most of the maladies that plague so many people. Admi ttedly, there have been a few rough spots for me, but not many. Not even close to the norm. I’m not sure why this is. I suppose I’ll just accept it as one of those queer facts of life and move on. Nevertheless, as a result of these memoirs, I am now acutely aware of my good fortune and extremely grateful for the blessings.
In the introduction, I mentioned a metaphorical jigsaw puzzle box filled with odd-shaped pieces that somehow were supposed to fit together to produce the picture on the cover of the box. Well, my box cover now has hundreds of pieces that include many of the stories I
have recorded. As I gaze at this figurative box cover, I realize those puzzle pieces have taken on a shape and form, roughly similar to the picture I see in the mirror. However, the picture remains a bit fuzzy.
I suppose it will stay that way. But I feel be tter having documented a bit of my life and brought the picture into better focus. Perhaps, as a result, the story of the boy from the red clay hills might be a little clearer for my family as well. I hope so.
