## About Frances Mallory

Frances Mallory was Keith's high school English teacher at Calhoun City High School and one of the most formative influences in his life, someone he described as "remarkable" and whose legacy he treasured for over fifty years.

Miss Frances, as she was known, was a gentle, erudite woman who taught English in the rural community for decades. She never married and lived a simple, private life at 400 North Jackson Street, just one block from the school, in a home her family owned for seventy-five years. A voracious reader with an unusual intellectual curiosity and a student of classical literature, she was known for her stern but fair teaching style, her good-natured goading, and her calm firmness. She tutored students on her front porch and believed deeply in memorization as a learning tool (P1_S03). Keith credited her with arousing in him a similar intellectual curiosity, opening his mind to new thoughts and words, and motivating him to "aspire and look beyond" his immediate environment. He noted that he was "a better and more responsible person because of her leadership" (P1_S03). Upon her death in 1994, her home—her only significant asset—was donated to First Baptist Church. Keith later purchased the restored property in 1998, naming it the Mallory House in her honor (P1_S03).

**Notable moments**

- Required Keith and his classmates to memorize the prologue to Chaucer's *Canterbury Tales* in Old English—a demanding April ritual that stuck with students for life; at their fiftieth reunion, classmates recited the first twenty lines from memory in her home (P1_S03)
- Once returned a poor grade to Keith saying, "I'm not going to tell your mother about this, Keith, because you and I both know you can do better," demonstrating her knack for reasoned argument and personal accountability (P1_S03)
- Taught Keith *Return of the Native* so memorably that decades later he still conjured "an apparently indelible mental image of Clym Yeobright" when driving into Calhoun City (P1_S02)
- Was quietly buried without a family grave marker; loyal alumni later chipped in to fund a gravestone and established a scholarship fund in her memory (P1_S03)

**A representative quote**

> "I wasn't especially fond of English, but I simply adored Miss Frances Mallory. There was something about her that appealed to my sense of romance, and thus I worked hard to learn the materials in her class."  
> — Keith reflecting on his high school years (P1_S02)
