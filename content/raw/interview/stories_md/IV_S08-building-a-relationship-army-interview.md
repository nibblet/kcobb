# Building a Relationship Army (Interview)

*Transcript edited lightly for clarity and readability; wording and voice preserved.*

## Full Text

**Cagnetta:** Well, let's talk about young people—you like to mentor young people.

**Keith:** I do.

**Cagnetta:** What's some advice for young people these days in the business world? What do you see out there? We were talking about ChatGPT before we started—the amazing things that are happening, and some challenges too.

**Keith:** There are challenges. I've done guest lectures out at Nova on leadership, opportunity, and building relationships. The biggest advice I've been able to pass along to younger people is to develop an army of relationships—business relationships. You have to be technically capable in whatever field or profession you've chosen, and you have to work hard—that's the standard advice: work hard, treat people fairly, all of that. But I think the biggest key—and the missing link for so many people—is building relationships. I learned that over the years at KPMG. The type of people the accounting profession attracts are not necessarily extroverted, outgoing people—they tend to be more introverted and more specifically focused. That's the nature of the profession. I saw over the years how difficult it was to get those people to move from doing the client's work toward building relationships with their clients. So I've tried to coach younger people I've worked with in that part of their development—to get out there, really get out there and get involved.

**Cagnetta:** You've led by example on getting involved. I do a lot of that too. I think it's been key to our success here at Transworld.

**Keith:** Yeah. I was having breakfast with Keith Costello yesterday—CEO of our bank—and he does something I really admire. In connection with what we're discussing: I think it's bi-weekly—he writes a message to all of his employees about what he's been doing the last couple of weeks. It's staggering. I told him it's unbelievable to read all the things he's done. He distributes that to his employees and they see the level of involvement—it's leading by example.

**Cagnetta:** Yeah—because he's very involved in the community as well.

**Keith:** He is. He's chairman of the Florida Bankers Association now, which is another big job. I admired that—that's a message to younger people. Back to our subject.

**Cagnetta:** Yeah, sure—what's the key? That type of leadership is motivational. Staying in touch with the people you work with—and I know you do that—you have to communicate with your employees and associates all the way down the line. You seem like a guy who didn't stay in his office too often.

**Keith:** Oh, not very much. You were walking around. I mentioned in my book one of the things I did at Alamo Rent A Car—I had breakfast once a month with five people from the company who were not supervisory people, regular people. They were chosen at random through some random-number generator; they announced to everybody they were going to have breakfast with me, and I did that for eighteen months in a row.

**Cagnetta:** Wow.

**Keith:** I had breakfast with ninety employees—everything from secretary to bus driver. I learned more about what was going on in the company than I did from my weekly management meetings. Yeah, sure—it was unbelievable what you could learn, and what they could learn. They'd say, "Ask him this and ask him that." I got pretty good at it after a few months. Fascinating—I could go on about examples of things I learned that I wouldn't have learned sitting in my office dictating messages. So it was good.
