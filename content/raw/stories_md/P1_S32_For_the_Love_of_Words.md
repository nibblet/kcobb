For the Love of Words
I step into the subject of vocabulary with trepidation, lest I leave an impression that I visualize myself as some sort of English scholar.
Clearly that’s not the case, but I will confess to being an aficionado.
Not a connoisseur, not a cognoscente; but simply someone who has admired and observed expressive language over the years—perhaps one who could be characterized as an esthete. Not quite right, but close.
I can’t be sure when or where my focus on words originated.
It probably all started early in my career with Bill Norton, my first managing partner at Peat Marwick. Bill was indeed a connoisseur.
Despite all of his peculiar qualities and idiosyncrasies, he used extremely descriptive language, often spiced with arcane words and phrases that induced a trip to be dictionary. He appeared to delight in confounding his audiences with his unusual lingo, and for some reason I was just as delighted to be confounded.
Bill’s expressions were challenging. And so we began our strange word riddle game that continued for more than forty years. It was a private game without formal rules and without scoring. Part of the fun was that neither of us ever outwardly acknowledged that the game was going on. Bill died a few years ago. I miss him and I miss the game. But I take pleasure that he improved my vocabulary, even though it took a long time to do it.
Bill Safire was one of my favorite word mavens for many years.
He wrote a column “On Language” for the New York Times Magazine for twenty years. I subscribed to the Sunday Times for most of that time and read his column religiously. Safire was a smart guy with an unusual gift for word craftsmanship. I loved his writings and have missed him since his death a few years ago. Another interesting journalist was Edwin Newman, whose excellent descriptive vocabulary fueled his humor. He was a master punster and linguist.
I remember reading two of his best-selling books, Strictly Speaking:
Will America be the Death of English? and A Civil Tongue. I miss him, too.
Years ago I started a collection of words that I happened to like for one reason or another. At first, the word collection consisted of scraps of miscellaneous notes. Then, somewhere along the line I
began an official list on my computer. The criteria were: (1) words that we have read or heard, but might not be able to clearly define if called upon; (2) words that are extremely descriptive of a speci ficabstract concept; (3) words that some people occasionally define or pronounce incorrectly (often, the opposite of the true meaning);
and (4) words that simply sound pleasant or have a crisp, descriptive tone. Just a few examples are: callow, didactic, heuristic, panoply,
truculent and obdurate. I really don’t have a cogent reason for this avocation. It was just a labor of love that I found enjoyable.
A few years ago a friend tipped me off to a website called
Thinkmap Visual Thesaurus. I subscribed and through it receive a
“Word of the Day” each day of the week, with an accompanying paragraph chronicling its origin and evolution. It has been fascinating to begin each day with this short lesson. As I am writing this chapter,
for example, the word that popped up today was “garbanzo” . That’s a new one for me, one I have never seen before. It’s actually a legume—a chickpea relative. So now I know!
The Thinkmap website and a corollary website called Vocabulary.
comregularly publish handy articles on vocabulary usage and grammar, and a compendium called “Featured Word Lists”, where hundreds of readers have submitted all sorts of word collections —
words from famous documents, books, speeches and writings of famous people as well as various topical collections. About five years ago, I submitted a portion of my word collection and labeled it “93
Eclectic Words” . For quite a while this list ranked number one on the website. Today, even after five years, it remains ranked number nineteen on the website’s published “Top Fi fty” ever submi tted.
It has been fun watching the durability of my list and reading the dozens of comments submitted by readers.
Related to this attraction to words, I have worked on crossword puzzles for years. In one sense, it is a silly waste of time every day—
sitting around filling in little letters in little boxes. Nevertheless,
I continue to find it a challenging and enjoyable exercise. As is commonly known, the daily newspaper crosswords begin the week with easy puzzles and get progressively harder every day until the next
Sunday, when I find full completion almost impossible. Whenever
I try to tackle the really difficult ones and get stumped early on, I
wonder to myself, “Who on earth is smart enough to breeze through something like this?” But I suppose those brains are out there. Not me. By mid-week, I’m struggling. However, my struggle continues on week after week. Hope springs eternal!
Many studies have confirmed a correlation between vocabulary and real-world competence, yet there seems to be confusion as to the most effective way to enhance vocabulary. Today, numerous computer programs are designed to improve vocabulary, and some actually seem to be effective. One interesting computer game that is both educational and enjoyable is called “Words with Friends” .
I play this competitive game every day with Dot, Polly York, one of Dot’s sisters, and my sister, Sandy. The competition is fun, but it is also an excellent learning experience. New words continue to pop up regularly. Dot and I also play a game called “Upwords” and keep a running score that has reached into the thousands now. Our cumulative scores are less than one percent apart.
There are also a lot of computer and smart phone apps to test and assess the use of words. Many, for example, will count the words in a document and a few will actually calculate the number of unique words and develop other useful statistics. For example,
according to this technology, this memoir contains approximately
85,000 words, with more than 10,000 of those being unique —that is, 10,000 different words in the document. There are more than 800
paragraphs and 5,000 sentences. For a numbers guy like me, that’s all very interesting.
Tosummarize, I love words. They have life. They have power.
They deliver clarity. I wish I knew more of them.

## Principles

- Regular, small exposures build vocabulary over time.
- Curate a personal, persistent list to deepen familiarity with chosen words.
- Learning driven by intrinsic enjoyment sustains long-term practice.
- Use playful competition to reinforce learning and maintain engagement.
- Sharing work publicly can create durability and useful feedback.

## Decision heuristics

- Capture interesting words immediately in a personal list on your computer.
- Subscribe to a daily 'Word of the Day' service to ensure regular exposure.
- Use competitive word games daily with peers to learn new vocabulary through play.
- Publish a curated selection to public forums to test durability and gather feedback.
